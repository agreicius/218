    Subsection 4.5.2 Derivatives Do NOT Distribute Over Products and Quotients

    As mentioned in the previous section, the following are NOT valid rules for taking derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[f(x)\cdot g(x)\right] = \frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\cdot\frac{d}{dx}[g(x)] \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]}{\frac{d}{dx}[g(x)]} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Here are two basic examples illustrating why:
    ðŸ”—

    Example 4.5.1. Non-example: The Derivative Does NOT Distribute Over a Product.

    We can calculate the derivative of \(x^2\cdot x^3\) by simplifying:
    \begin{equation*} x^2\cdot x^3 = x^5 \end{equation*}
    and then taking the derivative:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}(x^2\cdot x^3) = \frac{d}{dx}(x^5) = 5x^4\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    However, if we try to multiply the derivatives \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 3x^2\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)\cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = (2x)\cdot (3x^2) = 6x^3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    So we can clearly see that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}(x^2\cdot x^3) \neq \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)\cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x^3) \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 4.5.2. Non-example: The Derivative Does NOT Distribute Over a Quotient.

    Similarly, we can calculate the derivative of \(\dfrac{x^3}{x^2}\) by simplifying first:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^3}{x^2}\right) = \frac{d}{dx}(x) = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    However, if we try to make a quotient from the derivatives \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) = 2x\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^3) = 3x^2\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{d}{dx}(x^3)}{\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) } = \frac{3x^2}{2x} = \frac{3x}{2} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    So we can clearly see that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^3}{x^2}\right)\neq \frac{\frac{d}{dx}(x^3)}{\frac{d}{dx}(x^2) } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 4.5.3 Real-World Motivation for the Derivative of a Product

    Say that an investor is regularly purchasing stock in a particular company. Let \(N(t)\) represent the number of shares owned on day \(t\text{,}\) where \(t=0\) represents the first day on which shares were purchased. Let \(S(t)\) give the value of one share of the stock on day \(t\text{;}\) note that the units on \(S(t)\) are dollars per share. To compute the total value of the stock on day \(t\text{,}\) we take the product
    \begin{equation*} V(t) \text{ dollars } = N(t)\cdot S(t) \text{ (shares)\(\cdot\) (dollars per share). } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Observe that over time, both the number of shares and the value of a given share will vary. The derivative \(N\,'(t)\) measures the rate at which the number of shares is changing, while \(S\,'(t)\) measures the rate at which the value per share is changing. How do these respective rates of change affect the rate of change of the total value function?
    ðŸ”—
    To help us understand the relationship among changes in \(N,S\text{,}\) and \(V\text{,}\) letâ€™s consider some specific data.
    • Suppose that on day 100, the investor owns 520 shares of stock and the stockâ€™s current value is $27.50 per share. This tells us that \(N(100) = 520\) and \(S(100) = 27.50\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • On day 100, the investor purchases an additional 12 shares (so the number of shares held is rising at a rate of 12 shares per day).
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • On that same day the price of the stock is rising at a rate of 0.75 dollars per share per day.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In calculus notation, the latter two facts tell us that \(N\,'(100)=12\) (shares per day) and \(S\,'(100)=0.75\) (dollars per share per day). At what rate is the value of the investorâ€™s total holdings changing on day 100?
    ðŸ”—
    Observe that the increase in total value comes from two sources: the growing number of shares, and the rising value of each share. If only the number of shares is increasing (and the value of each share is constant), the rate at which which total value would rise is the product of the current value of the shares and the rate at which the number of shares is changing. That is, the rate at which total value would change is given by
    \begin{equation*} S(100)\cdot N\,'(100) = 27.50 \frac{\text{ dollars } }{\text{ share } }\cdot 12\frac{\text{ shares } }{\text{ day } } = 330\frac{\text{ dollars } }{\text{ day } } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Note particularly how the units make sense and show the rate at which the total value \(V\) is changing, measured in dollars per day.
    ðŸ”—
    If instead the number of shares is constant, but the value of each share is rising, the rate at which the total value would rise is the product of the number of shares and the rate of change of share value. The total value is rising at a rate of
    \begin{equation*} N(100)\cdot S\,'(100) = 520\text{ shares } \cdot 0.75\frac{\text{ dollars per share } }{\text{ day } } = 390\frac{\text{ dollars } }{\text{ day } } \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.5.6 The Proof of the Product Rule

    The proof of the product rule is interesting enough to include, though you will not need to do any calculations like it; feel free to skip it if you are running short on time or attention. The proof uses the definition of the derivative with a small "trick" of adding a fancy "0" in the numerator. In the next section, we will prove the quotient rule once we have learned the chain rule.
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions (meaning that the derivatives \(f'\) and \(g'\) exist. We wish to show that \((fg)' = f'g+fg'\text{.}\) We will apply the definition of the derivative to the left side of that equation and then use a long string of algebra and limit rules to rewrite it in terms of \(f', g, f\text{,}\) and \(g'\text{.}\) This will be made possible in the second step of the following calculating by adding and subtracting the same expression,
    \begin{equation*} f(x)g(x+h) \end{equation*}
    from the numerator, effectively adding an overly-complicated 0.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}(fg)' \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h)+f(x)g(x)}{h} \\ \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h)-f(x)g(x+h)+f(x)g(x+h)+f(x)g(x)}{h} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Notice in the middle of the numerator, we have inserted \(-f(x)g(x+h)+f(x)g(x+h)\text{,}\) which is equal to 0. This allows us in the next step to factor \(g(x+h)\) from the first two terms and \(f(x)\) from the third and fourth terms. Then we split up the fractions slightly using algebra and distribute the limits, using limit rules.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}(fg)' \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\Big(f(x+h)-f(x)\Big)\cdot g(x+h)+f(x)\cdot\Big(g(x+h)+g(x)\Big)}{h} \\ \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(f(x+h)-f(x)\Big)\cdot g(x+h)}{h}+\frac{f(x)\cdot\Big(g(x+h)+g(x)\Big)}{h} \right)\\ \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(f(x+h)-f(x)\Big)}{h}\cdot g(x+h)+f(x)\cdot\frac{\Big(g(x+h)+g(x)\Big)}{h} \right)\\ \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(f(x+h)-f(x)\Big)}{h}\right)\cdot\lim_{h\to 0}g(x+h)+ \lim_{h\to 0}f(x)\cdot \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(g(x+h)+g(x)\Big)}{h} \right)\\ \amp = \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(f(x+h)-f(x)\Big)}{h}\right)\cdot g(x)+f(x)\cdot \lim_{h\to 0}\left(\frac{\Big(g(x+h)+g(x)\Big)}{h} \right) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    In this last step, we have used the fact that \(g(x)\) must be continuous, since it is differentiable, so the limit at \(h=0\) of \(g(x+h)\) is the value \(g(x+0) = g(x)\text{.}\) Now we recognize the derivative of \(f\) at the beginning of the first term, and the derivative of \(g\) a the end of the second term. At last, this is equal to
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}(fg)' = f'(x)g(x)+f(x)g'(x) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Notice that throughout the entire calculation, our goal was to extract the expressions that represent the derivatives of \(f\) and \(g\text{.}\) The algebra steps were not meant to "simplify"; they were meant to get us closer to the final form of \(f'g+fg'\text{.}\)
