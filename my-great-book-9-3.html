      🔗
      🔗
    • See how to rewrite other indeterminate forms in order to apply L’Hôpital’s rule.
      🔗

    Subsection 7.2.2 A Visual Approach to Indeterminate Limits

    As you know very well by now, limits of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) are indeterminate. This means that, at first glance, it is not clear whether such a limit exists or what it might be equal to. In some cases we can resolve the indeterminate form with algebra, as we did last semester. However, with the introduction of exponential and logarithmic functions, algebra at last meets its "limit."
    🔗

    Example 7.2.1.

    Consider the limits
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+x)}{x}\qquad\text{ and } \qquad\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x}{e^x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The first limit evaluates to
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+x)}{x} = \frac{\ln(1)}{0} = \frac{0}{0} \end{equation*}
    and thus is indeterminate. There is no algebra that will let us alter this expression so that it is not indeterminate.
    🔗
    The second limit evaluates to
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x}{e^x} = \frac{\infty}{\infty} \end{equation*}
    and thus is also indeterminate. Again, there is no algebra that will let us alter this expression so that it is not indeterminate.
    🔗
    🔗
    In general, limits of fractions that involve a mixture of different types of functions (polynomial, exponential, logarithmic …) are usually more resistant to algebra. By looking at the graphs of the functions that make up such a fraction, we get some clue as to how to approach such limits, as the next two examples illustrate.
    🔗

    Example 7.2.2. Graphically Comparing Functions in a $\frac{0}{0}$ Limit.

    For another example, consider the limit
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    As \(x\to0\text{,}\) we have \(x^2\to0\) and \((2^x-1)\to (1-1)\to0\) and thus this limit is of the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) This indeterminate limit cannot be simplified with algebra. So what can we do?
    🔗
    We can approach the problem graphically. The graphs of \(f(x)=x^2\) and \(g(x)=2^x-1\) are shown below. Since we are interested in the limit as \(x\to0\text{,}\) we should focus on what happens as we look at smaller and smaller graphing windows around \(x=0\text{,}\) as shown in the second and third graphs.
    🔗
    Near \(x=0\text{,}\) the graph of \(f(x)=x^2\) loks a lot like its horizontal tangent line \(y=0\text{,}\) and the graph of \(g(x)=2^x-1\) looks a lot like its tangent line \(y=x\text{.}\) Thus, we would anticipate the behavior of the quotient \(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\) as \(x\to0\) to be similar to that of the quotient \(\frac{0}{x}\) of the corresponding tangent lines at \(x=0\text{.}\) From this information it would be reasonable to guess that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}=\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{0}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}0=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The previous example suggests that we may be able to use the limit of tangent lines in place of the limit of the functions. We can also use tangent lines to examine limits of the indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) as we will see in the next example.
    🔗

    Example 7.2.4. Graphically Comparing Functions in a $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ Limit.

    For example, consider the limit
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the same quotient \(\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\) of functions as before, but with the limit as \(x\to\infty\) instead of as \(x\to0\text{.}\) As \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) we have \(x^2\to\infty\) and \(2^x-1\to\infty\text{,}\) and therefore the limit is of the indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Again, we cannot simplify this expression with algebra. Let’s examine what happens as we look at larger graphing windows to see the behavior of \(f(x)=x^2\) and \(g(x)=2^x-1\) as \(x\to\infty\text{:}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    From the right most graph, we can see that the heights on the graph of \(g(x)=2^x-1\) are in some sense approaching \(\infty\) in a fundamentally faster way than the heights on the graph of \(f(x)=x^2\text{.}\) Specifically, the slopes of \(g(x)=2^x-1\) are significantly steeper than the slopes of \(f(x)=x^2\) for large values of \(x\text{.}\) Accordingly, we would anticipate that as \(x\to\infty\) the values of \(2^x-1\) would win the race to infinity and that the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}=0\text{.}\) Once again, a ratio of slopes has helped us to guess the value of the limit of a quotient.
    🔗
    🔗
    This geometric interpretation is the basis for the powerful limit technique known as L’Hôpital’s Rule. To summarize, we can use the derivatives of the functions involved in either \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) limits in order to evaluate the limit.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 7.2.6.

    The above two examples suggest that we can use the derivatives of the functions involved in either \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) limits in order to evaluate the limit.
    1. The limit
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+x)}{x} \end{equation*}
      has indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Find the derivatives of \(f(x) = \ln(1+x)\) and \(g(x) = x\text{.}\) Is the limit of the derivatives
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \end{equation*}
      still indeterminate?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The limit
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to \infty}\frac{x}{e^x} \end{equation*}
      has indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) Find the derivatives of \(f(x) = x\) and \(g(x) = e^x\text{.}\) Is the limit of the derivatives
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to \infty}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \end{equation*}
      still indeterminate?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 7.2.3 L’Hôpital’s Rule for the Indeterminate Forms \(\frac{0}{0}\) and \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\)

    The ideas we have developed regarding limits of fractions suggest the theorem that follows. This key theorem will allow us to solve some limits of the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) by relating them to the fractions of the corresponding derivatives:
    🔗

    Theorem 7.2.7. L’Hôpital’s Rule.

    If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable and both approach zero or both approach \(\pm\infty\) as \(x\to a\) (where \(a\) is allowed to be \(\infty\)), then
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x\to a}\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \end{equation*}
    provided the righthand limit exists.
    🔗
    Theoretical detail: To be technically correct, we need to add the additional hypothesis that \(g'(x)\neq0\) on an open interval that contains \(a\) or in every neighborhood of infinity if \(a\) is \(\infty\text{;}\) this is almost always met in practice.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.2.8.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate the limit of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\) is of the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) L’Hôpital’s rule applies and says that we can calculate it by considering instead the limit of the quotient of the derivatives of the numerator and denominator:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1} \amp = \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)}{\frac{d}{dx}(2^x-1)}\\ \amp =\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{2x}{(\ln2)2^x}\\ \amp =\frac{0}{(\ln2)2^0}\\ \amp =\frac{0}{(\ln2)(1)}\\ \amp =0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    \fbox{Two things to note:
    • It is considered good mathematical practice to write ``L’H" under the equals sign where you apply L’Hôpital’s rule, to indicate your reasoning in that step. It not only makes the calculation more clear, but also indicates that we are using a major Theorem.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Do NOT using the quotient rule to compute derivatives because you are NOT taking the derivative
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right) \end{equation*}
      when using L’Hôpital’s rule. As explained in the previous section, this theorem is comparing the rates of change of each function (meaning their derivatives) in order to compute the limit.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.2.9.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate the limit of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\) is of the indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{,}\) so L’Hôpital’s rule applies. Again, we replace the numerator and denominator of the quoitent with their derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\amp = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(x^2)}{\frac{d}{dx}(2^x-1)}\\ \amp =\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2x}{(\ln2)2^x}\\ \amp =\frac{\infty}{\infty}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Unfortunately, our application of L’Hôpital’s rule was not sufficient to resolve the limit here, because if we let \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) we have \(2x\to\infty\) and \((\ln2)2^x\to\infty\text{,}\) so our limit is still in the indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) However, this means that we can apply L’Hôpital’s rule again!
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2x}{(\ln2)2^x}\amp = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\frac{d}{dx}(2x)}{\frac{d}{dx}((\ln2)2^x)}\\ \amp =\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2}{(\ln2)^22^x}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{\infty}\\ \amp = 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    As \(x\to\infty\) the denominator of this limit approaches \(\infty\) while the numerator is equal to \(2\text{,}\) hence the limit goes to 0. Combining these two applications of L’Hôpital’s rule, we see that the original limit is equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that the answers in Example 7.2.8 and Example 7.2.2 agree, and that the answers in Example Example 7.2.9 and Example 7.2.4 agree. The graphical motivation of comparing the growth of the two functions is precisely the reason why L’Hôpital’s rule is true.
    🔗
    \fbox{ Pay special attention to the fact that L’Hôpital’s Rule is a Theorem and has hypotheses: The limit must be of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) It is vital to check when applying L’Hôpital’s rule is that the limit is of one of those two indeterminate forms. If the limit is not in one of those two forms, then L’Hôpital’s rule cannot be applied. Not only that, if you DO use L’Hôpital’s rule when it does not apply, you will not necessarily get the correct value of the limit. }
    🔗

    Example 7.2.10.

    Determine whether or not L’Hôpital’s rule applies to \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{1+2^x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    L’Hôpital’s rule does not apply, as \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{1+2^x}\) does not have either form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) Instead, it approaches \(\frac{0}{1+2^0}=\frac{0}{1+1}=0\) as \(x\to0\text{.}\)

    Evaluate each of the following limits. If you use L’Hôpital’s rule, indicate where it was used, and be certain its hypotheses are met before you apply it.
    1. \(\displaystyle \ds\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{2\ln(x)}{1-e^{x-1}}\)

      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Since \(\ds\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{2\ln(x)}{1-e^{x-1}}\) is indeterminate of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) we may apply L’Hôpital’s rule.
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\ds\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{2\ln(x)}{1-e^{x-1}} \amp = \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\ds\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\frac{2}{x}}{-e^{x-1}} \\ \amp =\frac{2}{-e^{0}}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{1}\\ \amp =-2. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Since \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2\ln(x)}{1-e^{x-1}}\) is indeterminate of the form \(\frac{\infty}{-\infty}\text{,}\) we may apply L’Hôpital’s rule.
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\ds\lim_{x\to \infty}\frac{2\ln(x)}{1-e^{x-1}} \amp = \frac{\infty}{-\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\ds\lim_{x\to \infty}\frac{\frac{2}{x}}{-e^{x-1}} \\ \amp =\frac{\frac{2}{\infty}}{-e^{\infty}}\\ \amp =\frac{0}{-\infty}\\ \amp =0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Sometimes L’Hôpital’s rule must be applied multiple times to a limit. After each application, we must check to make sure that the hypotheses of L’Hôpital’s rule still apply (in other words, that the limit is still of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\)) before applying it again. We should also ask ourselves whether L’Hôpital’s rule has made the limit easier to compute.

    Example 7.2.14.

    The limit
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} \end{equation*}
    has the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{,}\) and so we may apply L’Hôpital’s rule:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} \amp = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2x}\\ \amp = \frac{\infty}{\infty} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The new limit \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2x}\) still has the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{,}\) and so we may use L’Hôpital’s rule again. Additionally, we note that while the numerator has not changed, the denominator has gone down a degree, and so we have made the limit a little simpler than it was originally:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2x} \amp = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2}\\ \amp = \frac{\infty}{2} \\ \amp = \infty \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus the original limit is also equal to \(\infty\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{x^2} = \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that it would be wrong to use L’Hôpital’s rule for a third time because \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{e^x}{2}\) does not have the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\)

    Subsection 7.2.4 L’Hôpital’s Rule for the Indeterminate Forms \(0\cdot \infty\) and \(\infty-\infty\)

    As mentioned, L’Hôpital’s rule can only be used for limits of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) However, we can use it for limits of other forms, for example \(0\cdot \infty\) and \(\infty-\infty\text{,}\) if we are first able to use algebra to rewrite the limit to have the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 7.2.15.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate the limit of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}x^2e^{-3x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x\to\infty\) we have \(x^2\to\infty\) and \(e^{-3x}\to0\text{,}\) so the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}x^2e^{-3x}\) is in the indeterminate form \(\infty\cdot 0\) :
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}x^2e^{-3x} = \infty\cdot 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, this limit is not yet in a form to which we can apply L’Hôpital’s rule. Luckily, limits of the indeterminate form \(\infty\cdot0\) can always be rewritten as a quotient of the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) or \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) by moving one of the factors to the denominator via using an exponent of \(-1\text{.}\) In this example, since \(e^{-3x}\) has a negative exponent already, it is natural to move it to the denominator:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}x^2e^{-3x}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{e^{3x}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The limit is now indeterminate and has the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) Hence we can apply L’Hôpital’s rule:
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}x^2e^{-3x} \amp =\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x^2}{e^{3x}} = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ rewrite to form } \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2x}{3e^{3x}} = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ apply L'H\^opital's rule; still of the form } \frac{\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{2}{9e^{3x}} \amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ apply L'H\^opital's rule }\\ \amp = \frac{2}{\infty} \amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ Since } 9e^{3x}\to\infty\textrm{ as } x\to\infty\\ \amp =0\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 7.2.17.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate the limit of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that we consider only the limit from the right of 0, since \(\ln x\) is not defined for negative numbers.
    🔗
    As \(x\to0^{+}\text{,}\) we have \(x\to0\) and \(\ln x\to-\infty\text{,}\) and therefore the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x\) is in the indeterminate form \(0(-\infty)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x = 0(-\infty) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In order to apply L’Hôpital’s rule we must rewrite the limit in the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) In this case, it is easier to leave \(\ln x\) in the numerator; as \(x\to0^+\) we have
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x \amp =\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{\ln x}{x^{-1}} = \frac{-\infty}{\infty}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ limit is now in form } \frac{-\infty}{\infty}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \frac{\infty}{-\infty}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ apply L'H\^opital's rule } \end{align*}
    🔗
    This last expression still has an indeterminate form, but now it can be simplified using algebra:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \frac{1}{x}\cdot\frac{-x^2}{1} = \frac{-x^2}{x} = -x \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Lastly, we finish computing the limit
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}-x=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus we have found that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0^+}x\ln x = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note: We could have applied L’Hôpital’s rule a second time in this problem. Immediately after the first application of the rule, the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^2}\) was again in the indeterminate form \(\frac{\infty}{-\infty}\text{.}\) However, simplifying via algebra instead resulted in a very easy limit that we could evaluate. You should always look for the simplest possible means of evaluating any limit, applying algebraic methods before using a fancy theorem like L’Hôpital’s rule.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    \fbox{In general, we have two choices for rewriting limits of the form \(0\cdot\infty\) in order to use L’Hôpital’s rule. We can rewrite
    \begin{equation*} 0\cdot\infty = \frac{0}{\infty^{-1}} = \frac{0}{0} \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} 0\cdot\infty = \frac{\infty}{0^{-1}} = \frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Which one we choose depends entirely on whether the derivatives of the rewritten numerator and denominator are "easier" to work with in the new limit. }}
    🔗
    We can also rewrite some limits of the form \(\infty-\infty\) in order to use L’Hôpital’s rule. However, this generally only works if the original limit contains fractions in one or both terms:
    🔗

    Example 7.2.18.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate the limit of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{e^x-1}\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x\to0\text{,}\) both \(\dfrac{1}{x}\) and \(\dfrac{1}{e^x-1}\) become infinite, so the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{e^x-1}\right)\) is potentially of the indeterminate form \(\infty-\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To be honest, we are playing pretty fast and loose here. Specifically, we are not bothering to examine whether the terms \(\dfrac{1}{x}\) and \(\dfrac{1}{e^x-1}\) approach \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\text{.}\) Without knowing that, we don’t know for sure whether this limit is of the indeterminate form \(\infty-\infty\) or the non-indeterminate form \(\infty+\infty\text{.}\) We could look from the left and right and do this more precisely if we are asked to, but it is easier to instead do some algebra so that we can apply L’Hôpital’s rule:
    \begin{align*} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{e^x-1}\right)\amp = \ds\lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{e^x-1}{x(e^x-1)}-\frac{x}{x(e^x-1)}\right) \amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ find a common denominator}\\ \amp = \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{(e^x-1)-x}{x(e^x-1)} = \frac{0}{0}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ combine fractions; form is now} \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x-1}{(e^x-1)+xe^x} = \frac{0}{0}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ apply L'H\^opital's rule; still } \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}=\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x}{e^x+e^x+xe^x}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ apply L'H\^opital's rule again }\\ \amp =\frac{e^0}{e^0+e^0+0e^0}\amp \leftarrow\amp \textrm{ evaluate the limit }\\ \amp = \frac{1}{1+1+0}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{2} \end{align*}
    🔗

    Subsection 7.2.5 Summary

    The following theorem was introduced in this section:
    🔗
    Key ideas: L’Hôpital’s rule applies only to limits of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) and \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\) L’Hôpital’s rule does NOT involve using the quotient rule. L’Hôpital’s rule can be applied to limits of the forms \(0\cdot \infty\) or \(\infty-\infty\) only after algebraically manipulating them to be of either form \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Other ideas introduced: Algebra is still the best way to evaluate a limit. Only resort to fancy methods if there are no algebraic options.
