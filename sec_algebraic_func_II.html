    Example 1.5.4. Power function: range.

    Let \(f(x)=x^n\text{,}\) where \(n\) is a positive even integer. Show that \(\range f=[0,\infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since \(n=2k\) for some integer \(k\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp =x^{2k}\\ \amp =(x^k)^2 \amp (\text{iter. powers})\\ \amp \geq 0 \text{,} \end{align*}
    where the last inequality follows from the observation that the square of any real number is nonnegative. Since the outputs of \(f\) are all nonnegative, we conclude
    \begin{equation*} \range f\subseteq [0,\infty)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We now set about proving the reverse inclusion: \([0,\infty)\subseteq \range f\text{.}\) To this end, take any \(b\in [0,\infty)\text{.}\) Since \(r\geq 0\text{,}\) the number \(r^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{r}\) is well defined. (In this case, since \(n\) is even, \(r^{1/n}\) by definition is the unique nonnegative \(n\)-th root of \(r\text{.}\)) Setting \(a=r^{1/n}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} f(a) \amp =a^{n}\\ \amp =\left(r ^{1/n}\right)^n\\ \amp = r^{\tfrac{1}{n}\cdot n} \amp (\text{iterated powers})\\ \amp =r^1\\ \amp =r\text{.} \end{align*}
    This shows that \(r\) is an output of \(f\text{,}\) and thus that \(r\in \range f\text{,}\) as desired. We conclude that \(\range f=[0,\infty)\text{.}\)

    Subsection Polynomials

    In contrast to the simple power functions considered above, the behavior of polynomials can be quite complicated, and as a general rule becomes more and more complicated as the degree of the polynomial increases. This is illustrated in FigureÂ 1.5.9.
    ðŸ”—
    We see that as the degree of the polynomials increases, the graphs of the functions tend to have more â€œturning pointsâ€ (what we will later define as local maxima and local minimima). Furthermore, it is not clear from these examples how the range of a polynomial \(f(x)=\anpoly\) is determined by its degree and/or coefficients. Once again, calculus will provide us with the theoretical tools to answer questions related to these issues. For now, we will address another important question: how do we find the roots of a polynomial? Looking at the \(x\)-intercepts of the graphs in FigureÂ 1.5.9, it appears that the number of roots of a polynomial has something to do with its degree. The following theorem gives a precise (if somewhat incomplete) articulation of this relationship.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 1.5.10. Roots of polynomials.

    Let \(f(x)\) be a polynomial of positive degree \(n\text{.}\)
    1. \(f\) has at most \(n\) distinct roots.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. A real number \(a\in \R\) is a root of \(f\) if and only if \(f\) factors as
      \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-a)g(x) \end{equation*}
      for some polynomial \(g(x)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Statement (1) of TheoremÂ 1.5.10 tells us that the degree of a polynomial bounds the number of possible real roots, and thus also the number of possible \(x\)-intercepts. Thus, a quadratic polynomial has at most two roots, a cubic polynomial has at most three roots, a quartic polynomial has at most four roots, etc.. Note that the theorem does not claim that a degree-\(n\) polynomial must have \(n\) roots. Indeed we have seen already examples of quadratic polynomials (degree-2) that have no (real) roots!
    ðŸ”—

    Aside

    If we move from \(\R\) to the larger number system \(\C\) of complex numbers, then we can strengthen (1) of TheoremÂ 1.5.10: namely, every polynomial of positive degree \(n\) has \(n\) roots (counting multiplicity). This is the content of the fundamental theorem of algebra. The crucial detail here is the modifier â€œcomplexâ€ in â€œcomplex rootâ€.
    ðŸ”—
    Statement (2) of TheoremÂ 1.5.10 makes the connection between roots of polynomials and factorization. As a result, the more we are able to factor a polynomial, the more we can say about its roots; conversely, every time we find a root of polynomial \(f\text{,}\) we can make progress on the factorization of \(f\) by â€œfactoring outâ€ that root. Factoring polynomials is thus an important skill, and we gather here some techniques for doing this.

    1. Let \(f\) and \(g\) be polynomials of positive degree. There are unique polynomials \(q(x)\) and \(r(x)\) satisfying

      We call \(q\) and \(r\) the quotient and remainder upon division of \(f\) by \(g\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—

    You may have noticed that the two candidate roots of the polynomial in ExampleÂ 1.5.14 were integer factors of its constant term. As it turns out, given a polynomial with integer coefficients, any integer root of that polynomial must be a factor of its constant term.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 1.5.15. Integer root theorem.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\anpoly \end{equation*}
    and assume that all the coefficients of \(f\) are integers: i.e., \(a_k\in \Z\) for all \(0\leq k\leq n\text{.}\) Any integer root of \(f\) must be a factor of \(a_0\text{:}\) i.e., if \(f(c)=0\) and \(c\in \Z\text{,}\) then \(c\) is a factor of \(a_0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Procedure 1.5.18. Factoring polynomials and finding roots.

    To find all roots of a polynomial \(f\text{,}\) you may proceed as follows.
    1. Factor \(f(x)\) as best you can as a product of polynomials of smaller degree:
      \begin{equation} f(x)=f_1(x)f_2(x)\cdots f_r(x)\text{.}\tag{1.43} \end{equation}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. A real number \(a\) is a root of \(f\) if and only if it is a root of one of the polynomials \(f_k(x)\text{.}\) Thus by finding all roots of each polynomial \(f_k(x)\text{,}\) we find all roots of \(f\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Note that the success ProcedureÂ 1.5.18 depends on our ability to find all the roots of the factor polynomials \(f_k(x)\text{.}\) Accordingly, the smaller the degree of the \(f_k\text{,}\) the more likely we are in being able to find all roots of \(f\text{.}\) In particular, we will be home free if we are able to factor \(f\) far enough so that all factor polynomials are of degree one or two.

    Example 1.5.19. Roots of a quartic.

    Find all roots of \(f(x)=x^4-4x^3+5x^2-4x+4\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since the coefficients of \(f\) are integers, we first try and find an integer root of \(f\) using TheoremÂ 1.5.15. Evaluating \(f(a)\) for \(a=\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 4\) (the factors of the constant term of \(f\)), we see that \(f(2)=0\text{,}\) and thus that \((x-2)\) is a factor of \(f\text{.}\) Using polynomial longdivision (we skip the steps here), we see that
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-2)(x^3-2x^2+x-2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now continue the process by factoring \(g(x)=x^3-2x^2+x-2\text{.}\) Again using TheoremÂ 1.5.15, we try \(\pm 1, \pm 2\) as potential roots. It turns out that \(g(2)=0\text{,}\) and thus \(x-2\) can be factored out (again). Using polynomial longdivision (again we omit the steps), we find that
    \begin{equation*} g(x)=(x-2)(x^2+1)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and thus that
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-2)g(x)=(x-2)(x-2)(x^2+1)=(x-2)^2(x^2+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Lastly, we apply TheoremÂ 1.2.27 to the remaining factor \(x^2+1\text{,}\) concluding that it has no roots. By ProcedureÂ 1.5.18, we conclude that the only root of \(f\) is \(2\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.5.20. Multiplicity of a root.

    The factorization
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-2)^2(x^2+1) \end{equation*}
    from ExampleÂ 1.5.19 tells us that \(2\) is a repeated root of \(f\) of multiplicity two. In general, if a polynomial \(f\) factors as
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=(x-a)^mq(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a\) is not a root of \(q\) (i.e., \(q(a)\ne 0\)), then \(a\) is called a root of multiplicity \(m\). For example, the polynomial
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=x^2(x+1)(x-6)^4(x^2+1) \end{equation*}

    Another important consequence of TheoremÂ 1.5.10 that we will make use of later in the course has to do with when two polynomials are equal (as functions), as articulated in CorollaryÂ 1.5.26.     Remark 1.5.25. Function equality.

    Note well the all important â€œfor allâ€ quantifier appearing in condition (2) of DefinitionÂ 1.5.24. That detail makes the notion of function equality more nuanced than a simple numeric equality (like \(5=2^2+1\)). Given two functions \(f\) and \(g\) with common domain \(D\text{,}\) the statement \(f=g\) is in fact a claim that a whole bunch of numeric equalities hold: namely, we have \(f(x)=g(x)\) for every \(x\in D\text{.}\) Thatâ€™s one equality for each \(x\in D\text{.}\) Since \(D\) is most often an infinite set (like an interval) for us, this means that \(f=g\) typically asserts that infinitely many numeric equalities hold. In this sense a function equality is very similar to the notion of an identity (like \((x+1)^2=x^2+2x+1\)), which you may have encountered before.
