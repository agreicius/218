    Example 1.5.1.

    Here are some examples of polynomials:
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = 1-5x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = x^3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = 3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = x^4+1 \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} f(x) = x^7+x^6+x^5+x^4+x^3+x^2+x+1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = x^5 -2x^3+2x^2-8x+10 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = -x^3-x-1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = 4.23x^{517}-0.5x^{24}+x^{6}-\pi \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Some things that we notice: there can be many terms added together in a polynomial, rather than just one, two, or three, but there cannot be an unlimited number of terms added together. Each term is either constant or a number times a power of \(x\text{.}\) The power of \(x\) is always a positive whole number, though the numbers in front of the \(x\)â€™s may be decimals. Here is another way of saying this exact same thing using more formal mathematical language:
    ðŸ”—
    polynomial function\(ax^i\)\(a\)\(i\)\(i=0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\)
    The following definition provides a general notation for writing down an arbitrary polynomial, in much the same way that \(f(x)=mx+b\) is an arbitrary linear function and \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) is an arbitrary quadratic function (those are also both polynomials). Since we may have many many terms, we use indices for the parameters \(a_0, a_1, a_2\text{,}\) etc. in front of the powers of \(x\text{,}\) and itâ€™s convenient to have the index match the power:
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.5.2. Polynomial Functions.

    A polynomial function is a function that can be written in the form
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+a_{n-2}x^{n-2}+\ldots+a_2x^2+a_1x+a_0 \end{equation*}
    for some integer \(n\geq 0\) and some real numbers \(a_0, a_1, \ldots a_n\) with \(a_n\neq 0\text{.}\) The integer \(n\) is called the degree of the polynomial, and is the largest power of \(x\) that appears in the polynomial.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 1.5.3.

    As a matter of convention, we also say that the constant zero function \(f(x) = 0\) is a polynomial function (and that its degree is undefined).
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The parameters \(a_n, a_{n-1}, a_{n-2}, \ldots a_1, a_0\) are called the coefficients of the polynomial. The subscripts \(n, n-1, n-2, \ldots 2, 1, 0\) on the coefficients are called indices (a single one is called an index) and are a way of labeling lots of different parameters without running out of letters. Note that the coefficient belonging to the term containing the power \(x^i\) is conveniently named \(a_i\text{.}\) For example, the coefficient of the term \(x^2\) is called \(a_2\text{.}\) Each of the coefficients represents a single real number. There are no restrictions on what the coefficients could be equal to, but if we want the highest power of \(x\) in the polynomial to be \(n\) (so that the degree is \(n\)), then we need that coefficient, labeled \(a_n\) to not be zero. The coefficient \(a_n\) belonging to the highest power of \(x\) is called the leading coefficient, and the term \(a_nx^n\) containing the highest power of \(x\) with \(a_n\neq 0\) is called the leading term. The coefficient \(a_0\) is called the constant term.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.4.

    For the polynomial \(f(x) = x(3x+1)(x-2)^2\text{,}\) determine the leading coefficient, leading term, degree, constant term, and coefficients \(a_1\) and \(a_3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We can expand \(f(x)\) as
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}f(x) \amp = x(3x+1)(x-2)^2\\ \amp = (3x^2+x)(x^2-4x+4)\\ \amp = 3 x^4 - 11 x^3 + 8 x^2 + 4 x \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    From this, we can see that the leading coefficient is 3, the leading term is \(3x^4\text{,}\) the degree is 4, the constant term is 0, the coefficient of \(x=x^1\) is \(a_1\) and so is equal to 4, and the coefficient of \(x^3\) is \(a_3\) and so is equal to \(-11\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Checkpoint 1.5.5.

    For the polynomial \(g(x) = x^5(1-2x)(1-x^3)\text{,}\) determine the leading coefficient, leading term, degree, constant term, and coefficients \(a_1\) and \(a_5\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    We have special names for some polynomials according to their degrees. For example, polynomials of degree 0 are called constant, of degree 1 are called linear, of degree 2 are called quadratic, of degree 3 are called cubic, of degree 4 are called quartic, and of degree 5 are called quintic polynomials. (Note: We require non-zero leading coefficients for quadratics and higher degrees, but not for linear functions; in other words, we will consider constant functions to be linear as we did in SectionÂ 1.2.)

    Subsection The Structure of Polynomial Functions: Factors and Zeros

    Higher degrees often result in more \(x\)-intercepts, a.k.a. zeros, and more local minima and maxima in the graph of a polynomial.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.6.

    Examine the cubic, quartic, and quintic polynomials in FigureÂ 1.5.7 below.
    ðŸ”—
    The cubic polynomial \(f(x) = x^3+x^2-2x\) on the left has three \(x\)-intercepts at \(x=-2\text{,}\) \(x=0\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) Note that we can see this from the factored form of \(f(x) = x(x-1)(x+2)\) by setting each factor equal to zero and solving for \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} x=0\text{ and } x-1=0\text{ and } x+2=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The quartic polynomial \(g(x) = x^4+x^3-2x^2\) in the middle also has three \(x\)-intercepts \(x=-2\text{,}\) \(x=0\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) When factored, \(g(x) = x^2(x-1)(x+2)\text{,}\) and we can see that the \(x\)-intercept \(x=0\) comes from setting the factor \(x^2\) equal to 0, while \(x=1\) and \(x=-2\) come from \(x-1=0\) and \(x+2=0\text{,}\) respectively.
    ðŸ”—
    The quintic polynomial \(h(x) = x^5-x^4-4x^3+4x^2\) on the right has four zeros: \(x=-2, 0, 1\text{,}\) and \(2\text{.}\) When factored, \(h(x) = x^2(x-1)(x+2)(x-2)\) and we can see that each factor corresponds to an \(x\)-intercept again.
    ðŸ”—
    Note that \(f(x)\) has one local minimum and one local maximum. \(g(x)\) has two local minima and one local maxima, and that the local minimum at \(x\approx-1.5\) is also a global minimum. \(h(x)\) has two local maxima and two local minima.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    You may be wondering if there is a pattern or rule in the number of \(x\)-intercepts and local minima/maxima that a polynomial function has. It turns out that, yes, there is, but it is more complicated than our observations of linear and quadratic functions. There is a very powerful theorem (called The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra) that says that every polynomial function can be factored into linear factors and/or irreducible quadratic factors. Each of the linear factors corresponds to an \(x\)-intercept.
    ðŸ”—
    A quadratic expression is irreducible if it cannot be factored with real-number coefficients; that is \(ax^2+bx+c\) is irreducible if it cannot be written in the form \(a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)\) for some real numbers \(r_1\) and \(r_2\text{.}\) Since each factor would correspond to an \(x\)-intercept:
    \begin{equation*} x-r_1=0\text{ when } x=r_1\text{ is an \(x\)-intercept } \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x-r_2=0\text{ when } x=r_2\text{ is an \(x\)-intercept } \end{equation*}
    a quadratic expression is irreducible if the equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has no solution. We already know that the equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has no solution if the discriminant \(b^2-4ac\) is negative, so we know that a quadratic expression \(ax^2+bx+c\) is irreducible if and only if its discriminant \(b^2-4ac\) is negative
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.8.

    For example, the quadratic expression \(x^2+1\) is irreducible. We can rewrite
    \begin{equation*} x^2+1 =1\cdot x^2+0\cdot x+1 \end{equation*}
    in order to see that \(a=1, b=0, c=1\) and then compute the discriminant:
    \begin{equation*} b^2-4ac = 0^2-4(1)(1) = -4 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Since the discriminant \(-4\) is negative, the quadratic expression \(x^2+1\) is irreducible. It does not factor and we can see graphically that it does not have any zeros:
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Checkpoint 1.5.10.

    \fbox{ Note that every linear factor \((x-r)\) of a polynomial corresponds to an \(x\)-intercept \(x=r\) of that polynomial, since if a polynomial \(f(x)\) can be factored as \((x-r)g(x)\) for some other polynomial \(g(x)\) (meaning, if \((x-r)\) is a factor of \(f(x)\)), then
    \begin{equation*} f(r)=(r-r)g(r) = 0 \end{equation*}
    and thus \(x=r\) is an \(x\)-intercept of \(f(x)\text{.}\) The converse is also true: if \(x=r\) is an \(x\)-intercept of \(f(x)\text{,}\) then \(f(x)\) has linear factor \(x-r\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = (x-r)g(x) \end{equation*}
    for some other polynomial \(g(x)\text{.}\) }}

    Example 1.5.11.

    The polynomials in FigureÂ 1.5.7 on the previous page have already been factored for us. We can see that the cubic polynomial \(f(x) = x^3+x^2-2x = x(x-1)(x+2)\) has three \(x\)-intercepts. The \(x\)-intercept at \(x=-2\) corresponds to the linear factor \(x+2\text{.}\) The \(x\)-intercept at \(x=1\) corresponds to the linear factor \(x-1\text{.}\) The \(x\)-intercept at \(x=0\) corresponds to the linear factor \(x=x-0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Note that \(g(x)\) also has three \(x\)-intercept, despite being a fourth-degree polynomial. This is because there are two linear factors with the same \(x\)-intercept of \(x=0\) since the factorization of \(g(x)\) includes \(x^2 = x\cdot x\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The quintic (degree-five) polynomial \(h(x)\) has four \(x\)-intercepts, again with a double-\(x\)-intercept at \(x=0\) corresponding to the linear factor \(x^2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    When a linear factor \(x-r\) occurs more than once in the factorization of a polynomial \(f\text{,}\) we say that \(f\) has a repeated zero at \(x=r\text{.}\) We say that repeated zeros are double zeros if the factorization includes \((x-r)^2\text{,}\) triple zeros if the factorization includes \((x-r)^3\text{,}\) and so on, according to the number of times the factor \(x-r\) is repeated.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.12.

    For example, the function \(f(x) = (x+1)(x-2)^2(x-6)\) has a double zero at \(x=2\) and the polynomial \(g(x) = (x+1)^3(x-2)^4\) has a triple zero at \(x=-1\) and a quadruple zero at \(x=2\text{.}\) Notice in the graphs of these functions that there is some interesting behavior at the repeated zero:
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In the first figure, the graph of \(f\) does not go straight through the \(x\)-axis at the double zero \(x=2\text{;}\) instead, it bounces off, much like a concave-down parabola. In the second graph, the triple zero at \(x=-1\) behaves like the graph of \(y=x^3\) near its \(x\)-intercept and the quadruple zero behaves like the graph of \(y=x^4\) next its \(x\)-intercept. This phenomenon happens in general: a zero repeated \(m\)-times as a linear factor is graphically similar to the graph of \(y=x^m\) near its \(x\)-intercept.

    However, just because a polynomial has a factorization doesnâ€™t mean that we have an easy way to actually factor that polynomial! In general, factoring a polynomial is extremely hard, and is often impossible; there is no mathematical formula for finding the zeros of polynomials of degree 5 and higher.
    â€‰26â€‰
    Itâ€™s not that nobody has found such a formula yet. It has actually been proven as mathematical fact that it is impossible to find such a formula. How would you even prove that something is impossible? Mathematics can!
    The only "manageable" formulas for factoring higher-degree polynomials are generalizations of the "difference of squares" formula from SectionÂ 1.2:
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 1.5.16. Formulas for Factoring Differences of Powers.

    For all real numbers \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) and any positive integer \(n\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}a^2-b^2 \amp = (a-b)(a+b)\\ a^3-b^3 \amp = (a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2)\\ a^5-b^5 \amp = (a-b)(a^4+a^3b+a^2b^2+ab^3+b^4)\\ a^n-b^n \amp = (a-b)(a^{n-1}+a^{n-2}b+a^{n-3}b^2+a^{n-4}b^3+\ldots+a^2b^{n-3}+ab^{n-2}+b^{n-1}) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.17.

    The sixth-degree polynomial \(x^6-64\) can first factor as a difference of squares:
    \begin{equation*} x^6-64 = ((x^3)^2-8^2) = (x^3-8)(x^3+8) \end{equation*}
    and then we can factor the cubic polynomial \(x^3-8\) using the above formula for the difference of cubes:
    \begin{equation*} x^3-8 = x^3-2^3 = (x-2)(x^2+2x+4) \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The factorization
    \begin{equation*} x^6-64 = (x-2)(x^2+2x+4)(x^3+8) \end{equation*}
    is the best that we can do with our current methods, though the cubic factor \(x^3+8\) does have a zero and can be reduced (we just donâ€™t know how).
    ðŸ”—
    Note that we also could have used the formula for 6-degree differences first and gotten
    \begin{equation*} x^6-64 = x^6-2^6 = (x-2)(x^5+2x^4+4x^3+8x^2+16x+32) \end{equation*}
    but then we do not know how to factor the resulting degree-five polynomial. As we already mentioned, factoring is a seriously non-trivial mathematical endeavor.

    This could be another Expansion Project, if you are very interested in knowing how to do it. Just ask.

      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. Multiplying Fractions: \(\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)\left(\dfrac{c}{d}\right) = \dfrac{ac}{bd}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. Dividing Fractions: \(\dfrac{(a/b)}{(c/d)} = \left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)\left(\dfrac{d}{c}\right) = \dfrac{ad}{bc}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Definition of Rational Functions

    A rational number is a number that can be written as a quotient (a.k.a. fraction) of the simplest possible numbers you can imagine, namely, integers. For example, \(\frac{1}{3}\) and \(-\frac{10}{7}\text{.}\) Similarly, a rational function is a quotient of simpler functions, namely, polynomials.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.5.23. Rational Functions are Quotients of Polynomials.

    A rational function is a function that can be written as the quotient of two polynomial functions
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \frac{a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\ldots+a_1x+a_0}{b_mx^m+b_{m-1}x^{m-1}+\ldots+b_1x+b_0} \end{equation*}
    for any \(x\) such that \(q(x)\neq 0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Just like our previous definitions for functions, the key realization is that a rational function can be written in such a form. It does not have to begin that way.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.24.

    We rewrite the function \(f(x) = x-1+\frac{1}{2x+1}\) as a rational function by finding a common denominator, adding fractions, then simplifying:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}x-1+\frac{1}{2x+1} \amp = (x-1)\cdot\frac{2x+1}{2x+1}+\frac{1}{2x+1}\\ \amp \\ \amp = \frac{(x-1)(2x+1)}{2x+1}+\frac{1}{2x+1}\\ \amp \\ \amp = \frac{(x-1)(2x+1)+1}{2x+1}\\ \amp \\ \amp = \frac{(2x^2-x-2x-1)+1}{2x+1}\\ \amp \\ \amp =\frac{2x^2-3x}{2x+1} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus we see that \(f(x)\) is rational because it can be written as a fraction of the polynomial \(2x^2-3x\) divided by the polynomial \(2x+1\text{.}\)

    Subsection Domain and Zeros of Rational Functions

    Remember that we cannot divide by 0. Using this idea, we can see from Definition DefinitionÂ 1.5.23 that the domain of a rational function is all \(x\)-values that are not zeros of the denominator of that function.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.26.

    Letâ€™s find the domains of \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\text{,}\) \(g(x) = \frac{1}{x-1}\text{,}\) and \(h(x) = \frac{1}{(x-1)(x+2)}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The domain of \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) is \(x\neq 0\text{.}\) We can write this using interval notation as \((-\infty,0)\cup(0,\infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The domain of \(g(x) = \frac{1}{x-1}\) is \(x\neq 1\text{.}\) We can write this using interval notation as \((-\infty,1)\cup(1,\infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The domain of \(h(x) = \frac{1}{(x-1)(x+2)}\) is \(x\neq 1, -2\text{.}\) We can write this using interval notation as \((-\infty,-2)\cup(-2,1)\cup(1,\infty)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.27.

    Find the domain of \(f(x) = \dfrac{x+5}{x^3+x^2-2x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We know that the domain of a rational function is all \(x\)-values that are not zeros of the denominator. Thus we set the denominator \(x^3+x^2-2x\) equal to 0 and solve for \(x\text{,}\) which will give us the \(x\)-values to exclude from the domain.
    ðŸ”—
    Factoring, we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}x^3+x^2-2x \amp = x(x^2+x-2)\\ \amp = x(x+2)(x-1) \amp =0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    when
    \begin{equation*} x=0 x+2=0 x-1=0 \end{equation*}
    and so when \(x=0, -2, 1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Thus the domain is \(x\neq -2, 0, 1\) since those are the \(x\)-values where the denominator is equal to 0. Using interval notation we can write the domain as
    \begin{equation*} (-\infty,-2)\cup(-2,0)\cup(0,1)\cup(1,\infty) \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    What do the graphs of rational functions look like? They can be very interesting, especially because as we get close to \(x\)-values that are not in the domain of the function, we are getting close to a point where we are dividing by 0. However, graphs of rational functions are highly dependent on small changes in their numerators and denominators. Pay careful attention to the details in the next example, especially the number of times that each linear factor is repeated in the numerator and/or denominator:
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.28.

    What is the domain of the three following rational functions? What do the graphs look like near the \(x\)-values that are not in the domain?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The domain of all three functions is \(\{x\in\mathbb{R}\mid x\neq 1, 3\} = (-\infty,1)\cup(1,3)\cup(3,\infty)\) since in all three functions, \(x=1\) and \(x=3\) are the zeros of the denominator. However, we can see that the functions have very different properties at \(x=1\) and \(x=3\text{.}\) \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) have a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\) and a hole at \(x=3\text{.}\) On the other hand, \(h(x)\) has vertical asymptotes at both \(x=1\) and \(x=3\text{.}\) We will look at some of the reasons for these here, but we will have to leave the details to explore later when we develop our language of limits.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The reason why \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) above have a hole at \(x=3\) instead of a vertical asymptote is related to the following theorem:
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 1.5.29. Cancellation when Variables are Involved.

    Given any real expression \(A\) that depends on a variable \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{A}{A}= \begin{cases}1, \amp \text{ for values of } x\text{ that make } A\neq 0\\ \text{ undefined } ,\amp \text{ for values of } x\text{ that make } A=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.30.

    For example, if you can cancel a common factor \(x-c\) from the numerator and denominator of a fraction, the cancelled expression will equal the original expression for all values of \(x\) except \(x=c\text{.}\) One illustration of this theorem is the cancellation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{(x-3)}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \begin{cases}\frac{1}{x-1},\amp \text{ if } x\neq 3\\ \text{ undefined } , \amp \text{ if } x=3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.31.

    In ExampleÂ 1.5.28, the function \(f(x) = \frac{x-3}{(x-3)(x-1)}\) has a hole at \(x=3\) instead of a zero or vertical asymptote because algebraically, we can cancel the common \(x-3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{(x-3)}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \begin{cases}\frac{1}{x-1},\amp \text{ if } x\neq 3\\ \text{ undefined } , \amp \text{ if } x=3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus the graph of \(f(x)\) in ExampleÂ 1.5.28 looks identical to the graph of \(y=\frac{1}{x-1}\) except at the point \(x=3\) where \(f(x)\) is undefined. We represent this undefined point on the graph with a hole.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The next theorem tells us when a quotient \(\frac{A}{B}\) can be equal to zero. This theorem will help us solve equations that can be written as quotients.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 1.5.32. When Is a Quotient Equal to Zero?

    If \(A\) and \(B\) are any real expressions, then \(\dfrac{A}{B}=0\) if and only if \(A=0\) and \(B\neq 0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.33.

    For example, the function \(g(x)\) in ExampleÂ 1.5.28 does not have a zero at \(x=3\text{,}\) even though its numerator \(x-3\) is equal to zero when \(x=3\text{,}\) even after canceling:
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \frac{(x-3)^2}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \begin{cases}\frac{x-3}{x-1},\amp \text{ if } x\neq 3\\ \text{ undefined } , \amp \text{ if } x=3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Because the original denominator of the function is zero when \(x=3\text{,}\) the function is forever undefined at \(x=3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \fbox{Note in particular that \(B=0\) does not imply that \(\dfrac{A}{B}=0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    In fact, if \(B=0\text{,}\) then \(\dfrac{A}{B}\) is undefined. That is why we require not only that \(A=0\) but also that \(B\neq 0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Note also that TheoremÂ 1.5.32 is valid only for equations that are set to zero. }}
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.5.34.

    Find the zeros of the function \(f(x) = \dfrac{x+5}{x^3+x^2-2x}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    As we saw in Example ExampleÂ 1.5.27, the domain of the function \(f(x) = \dfrac{x+5}{x^3+x^2-2x}\) is found by setting the denominator equal to zero. According to Theorem TheoremÂ 1.5.32, we find the zeros by setting the numerator equal to zero, and also checking that the denominator is not equal to zero.
    ðŸ”—
    The numerator is \(x+5\) and
    \begin{equation*} x+5=0\text{ when } x=-5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We already found in Example ExampleÂ 1.5.27 that the denominator is equal to zero for \(x=-2,0,1\text{,}\) and so is not equal to zero when \(x=-5\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Thus the only zero of \(f(x) = \dfrac{x+5}{x^3+x^2-2x}\) is at \(x=-5\text{.}\)

    Subsection Summary

    The following terms were introduced in this section:
    ðŸ”—
    Key ideas: Linear factors of a polynomial function correspond to zeros, and a polynomial function of degree \(n\) can have at most \(n\) zeros. Rational functions are undefined where their denominators are equal to zero. They have zeros where their numerators are equal to zero, provided that the denominator is not also equal to zero.
    ðŸ”—
    Other ideas introduced: Polynomial functions of degree \(n\) can have at most \(n-1\) turning points. Rational functions can have either vertical asymptotes or holes at points where they are undefined. Factoring is really hard and often impossible for higher-degree polynomials.
