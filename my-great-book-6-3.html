    Subsection 4.2.2 The Limit Definition of the Derivative at a Point

    In the previous section, we examined derivatives intuitively by discussing instantaneous rate of change, tangent lines, and associated slope functions. In this section, we revisit the meaning of the definition of the derivative and practice computing derivatives.
    ðŸ”—
    Recall from Definition DefinitionÂ 4.1.6 that the derivative at \(x=a\) of a function \(f\) is the number
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(a)=\frac{df}{dx}\Big|_{x=a}=\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
    provided that this limit exists.
    ðŸ”—
    The expression \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\)arises as the simplification of the average change of \(f(x)\) on the interval \([a,a+h]\text{,}\) which measures the change in value of \(f\) between \(x=a\) and \(x=a+h\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[a,a+h]} = \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}= \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{(a+h)-a}=\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Visually, the average change is the slope between the points on the graph at \(x=a\) and \(x=a+h\text{,}\) also called a secant line to the graph (a line between two points).
    ðŸ”—
    The \(\ds\lim_{h\to0}\) narrows the interval and causes it to approach the single point \(x=a\text{,}\) thus visually the secant lines approach the tangent line at \(x=a\text{.}\) Hence the derivative measures
    • the slope of the tangent line to \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\) and
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 4.2.3.

    For the function \(f(x) = x-x^2\text{,}\) use the \(h\to 0\) definition of the derivative to compute \(f\,'(2)\text{.}\) In addition, discuss the meaning of this value and draw a labeled graph that supports your explanation.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    From the limit definition, we know that
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now we use the rule for \(f\text{,}\) and observe that
    \begin{equation*} f(2) = 2-2^2=-2 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}f(2+h)\amp =(2+h)-(2+h)^2\\ \amp = (2+h)-(4+4h+h^2)\\ \amp = -2-3h-h^2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Substituting these values into the limit definition, we have that
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(-2-3h-h^2)-(-2)}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    this limit has the form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) In order to evaluate \(\ds\lim h\to 0\text{,}\) we must simplify the quotient. Combining like terms, we have
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-3h-h^2}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} f\,'(2) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{h(-3-h)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}(-3-h)=-3\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.2.3 The Derivative as a Function

    We saw in SectionÂ 4.1.4 that we can think of the derivative as a function, specifically the function that assigns to every \(x\)-value the slope of the tangent line to \(f(x)\) at that point. In other words, by putting all of the point-derivatives of a function together, we can define a function \(f\,'(x)\) whose output at any value of \(x\) is defined to be the derivative, or instantaneous rate of change, of \(f\) at that point.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 4.2.10. The Derivative of a Function.

    The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) is the function \(f\,'(x)\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(x)=\frac{df}{dx} = \displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    In terms of the change in the \(x\)- and \(f\)-values, we can also write
    \begin{equation*} f\,'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    meaning that the derivative is the limit of the change in \(f\) as the change in \(x\) becomes infinitely small. The domain of \(f\,'(x)\) is the set of values \(x\) for which the defining limit of \(f\,'(x)\) exists.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The function \(f\,'(x)\) is also called the associated slope function that we investigated in SectionÂ 4.1.4, since at each point \(x\) its value is the slope of the graph of \(f\text{.}\) In addition, the function \(f\,'(x)\) represents the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at every point \(x\text{.}\)

    Subsection 4.2.5 Velocity and Acceleration

    In this section, we give one interpretation of higher order derivatives in terms of position functions. In particular, if \(s(t)\) is the position of an object moving in a straight line, then its instantaneous rate of change is the velocity function \(v(t)=s'(t)\text{.}\) Similarly, the instantaneous rate of change of velocity \(v(t)\) is the acceleration function \(a(t)=v'(t)\text{,}\) which is also the second derivative of the position function \(s(t)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Recall that the height of a falling object is given by the function
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = -16t^2+v_0t+s_0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(v_0\) is the objectâ€™s initial velocity and \(s_0\) is the objectâ€™s initial position.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 4.2.25.

    A water balloon is tossed vertically in the air from a window. The balloonâ€™s height in feet at time \(t\) in seconds after being launched is given by \(s(t)=-16t^2+16t+32\text{,}\) corresponding to an initial velocity of 16 ft/sec and an initial height of 32 ft.
    ðŸ”—
    We can see from the picture (or from factoring \(s(t)\)) that the balloon hits the ground \(t=2\) seconds after it is launched.
    ðŸ”—
    The average velocity of the balloon in its last second is
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[1,2]} = \frac{s(2)-s(1)}{2-1} = \frac{0-32}{1} = -32\text{ feet/second }\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This means that, during the second before it hits the ground, the balloon falls at an average speed of 32 feet each second.
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s find the instantaneous velocity of the balloon at \(t=1\) second. Using the limit definition to compute the instantaneous rate of change of \(s\) with respect to time \(t\text{,}\) at the instant \(t=1\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} s'(1) =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{s(1+h)-s(1)}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} s'(1) =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(-16h^2-16h+32)-32}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-16h^2-16h}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Key ideas: The limit definition of the derivative comes from taking the limit of average rates of change on smaller and smaller intervals. Thus the derivative at a point is the instantaneous rate of change, and also the slope of the tangent line. The derivative is also a function, where the independent variable is the original input \(x\) and the output is the slope of the original function at \(x\text{.}\) We can keep taking derivatives forever (at least until we get a derivative of 0, at which point further derivatives will also be 0).

    Other ideas introduced: The units for derivatives are the same as the units for average rates of change:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\text{ units of output } }{\text{ units of input } }\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Units for higher order derivatives follow a similar pattern. There is an alternative notation for derivatives, called Leibniz notation, that we will use more later.
