    Example 7.4.1. Nicotine in the Blood.

    When a person smokes a cigarette, the nicotine from the cigarette enters the body through the lungs, is absorbed into the blood, and spreads throughout the body. Most cigarettes contain between \(0.5\) and \(2.0\) mg of nicotine; approximately 20% (between \(0.1\) and \(0.4\) mg) is actually inhaled and absorbed into the personâ€™s bloodstream. As the nicotine leaves the blood, the smoker feels the need for another cigarette. The half-life of nicotine in the bloodstream is about 2 hours. The lethal dose is considered to be about \(60\) mg.

    Functions with the behavior exhibited in ExampleÂ 7.4.1 are called surge functions. They have equations of the form \(y=ate^{-bt}\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are positive constants.
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s examine the family of functions \(y=ate^{-bt}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    What effect do the positive parameters \(a\) and \(b\) have on the shape of the graph of \(y=ate^{-bt}\text{?}\) Letâ€™s start by looking at the graph in FigureÂ 7.4.4 with \(a=1\) and \(b=1\text{.}\) We consider the effect of \(b\) on the graph of \(y=ate^{-bt}\) first.     ðŸ”—
    The next example shows that the maximum of the function \(y=te^{-bt}\) occurs at \(t=1/b\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 7.4.7. Formulas for the Maximum of a Surge Function.

    For \(b>0\text{,}\) show that the maximum value of \(y=te^{-bt}\) occurs at \(t=1/b\) and increases as \(b\) decreases.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The maximum must occur at a critical point where \(dy/dt=0\text{.}\) Differentiating gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt} = 1\cdot e^{-bt}+t(-be^{-bt}) = e^{-bt}-bte^{-bt} = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^{-bt}\) is always positive, \(dy/dt=0\) only where
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}1-bt \amp = 0\\ t=\frac{1}{b}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    By the Single Critical Point Theorem (TheoremÂ 5.5.6), we only need to check that \(t=\frac{1}{b}\) is a local maximum in order to know that it is a global maximum, since it is the only critical point on the interval \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\) To do this, we observe that the sign of \(dy/dt = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\) depends only on the sign of \(1-bt\text{,}\) since \(e^{-bt}\) is positive for all values of \(t\text{.}\) When \(t\lt 1/b\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}t \amp \lt 1/b\\ bt \amp \lt 1\\ 0 \amp \lt 1-bt. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, when \(t>1/b\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}t \amp >1/b\\ bt \amp > 1\\ 0 \amp >1-bt. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence \(dy/dt = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\) is positive for \(t\lt 1/b\) and negative for \(t>1/b\text{,}\) thus \(t=1/b\) is the location of a local maximum, and by the Single Critical Point Theorem, also a global maximum.
    ðŸ”—
    The maximum \(y\)-value is found from substituting \(t=1/b\) into \(y=te^{-bt}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} y=\frac{1}{b}e^{-b(1/b)} = \frac{e^{-1}}{b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can see that for \(b>0\text{,}\) as \(b\) increases, the denominator of \(\frac{e^{-1}}{b}\) will increase, making the value of \(y\) decrease overall. Thus the maximum value of \(y\) decreases as \(b\) increases. Similarly, when \(b\) decreases, the maximum value of \(y\) increases.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The surge function \(y=ate^{-bt}\text{,}\) for positive constants \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) increases rapidly and then decreases toward zero with a maximum at \(t=1/b\text{,}\) as we can see in FigureÂ 7.4.8.

    Subsection 7.4.4 Defining Marginal Cost and Revenue

    In this section, we look at some of the functions of interest to decision-makers in a firm or industry. Recall the definitions of cost, revenue, and profit originally given in SectionÂ 2.4.5:
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 7.4.17. Cost, Revenue, and Profit Functions.

    The cost function, \(C(q)\) gives the total cost of producing a quantity \(q\) of some good.
    ðŸ”—
    The revenue function, \(R(q)\text{,}\) gives the total revenue received by a firm from selling a quantity, \(q\) of some good.
    ðŸ”—
    If the good sells for a price of \(p\) per unit, and the quantity sold is \(q\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Revenue } = \text{ Price } \cdot\text{ Quantity } , \text{ so } R=pq \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The profit function, written as \(\pi\) to distinguish it from the price, \(p\text{,}\) is the difference
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Profit } = \text{ Revenue } -\text{ Cost } \text{ so } \pi=R-C \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The break-even point for a company is the point where the profit is zero and revenue equals cost.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The quantity \(q\text{,}\) of an item that is manufactured and sold depends on its price, \(p\text{.}\) As the price increases, manufacturers are usually willing to supply more of the product, whereas the quantity demanded by consumers falls.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 7.4.18. Supply and Demand.

    The supply curve, for a given item, relates the quantity, \(q\text{,}\) of the item that manufacturers are willing to make to make per unit time to the price, \(p\text{,}\) for which the item can be sold.
    ðŸ”—
    The demand curve relates the quantity, \(q\text{,}\) of an item demanded by consumers per unit time to the price, \(p\text{,}\) of the item
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

However, for historical reasons, the economists put price (the independent variable) on the vertical axis and quantity (the dependent variable) on the horizontal axis. (The reason for this state of affairs is that economists originally took price to be the dependent variable and put it on the vertical axis. Later, when the point of view changed, the axes did not.)     Many economics decisions are based on analysis of the costs and revenues "at the margin," meaning the additional cost or revenue generated by producing and selling one additional item. For example, the additional cost "at the margin" of producing \(q\) items is
    \begin{equation*} C(q+1)-C(q) = \frac{C(q+1)-C(q)}{(q+1)-q}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We recognize this as the average rate of change of \(C\) on the interval \([q,q+1]\text{.}\) If the graph of the cost function is not curving too fast near the point, the slope of the secant line is close to the slope of the tangent line. Therefore, the average rate of change is close to the instantaneous rate of change. Since these rates of change are not very different, many economists choose to define marginal cost, \(MC\text{,}\) and marginal revenue, \(MR\text{,}\) as the instantaneous rate of change of cost or revenue with respect to quantity:
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 7.4.21. Marginal Cost and Revenue.

    The marginal cost,
    \begin{equation*} MC=C'(q) \end{equation*}
    is the instantaneous rate of change of the cost of producing a quantity \(q\) of some good.
    ðŸ”—
    The marginal revenue,
    \begin{equation*} MR=R'(q) \end{equation*}
    is the instantaneous rate of change of the revenue from producing a quantity \(q\) of some good.

    Subsection 7.4.5 Maximizing Profit and Revenue

    A fundamental issue for a producer of goods is how to maximize profit. For a quantity, \(q\text{,}\) the profit \(\pi(q)\) is the difference between the revenue, \(R(q)\text{,}\) and the cost, \(C(q)\text{,}\) of supplying that quantity. Thus \(\pi(q) = R(q)-C(q)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    ðŸ”—
    We can get the same result analytically. Global maxima and minima of a function can only occur at critical points of the function or at the endpoints of the interval. To find a critical points of profit \(\pi(q)\text{,}\) look for zeros of the derivative:
    \begin{equation*} \pi'(q) = R'(q)-C'(q) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    \begin{equation*} R'(q) = C'(q)\text{,} \end{equation*}

    ðŸ”—
    \fbox{The maximum (or minimum) profit can occur where
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Marginal profit } = 0 \end{equation*}
    that is, where
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Marginal revenue } = \text{ Marginal cost } \end{equation*}

    Example 7.4.26.

    At a price of $80 for a half-day trip, a white-water rafting company attracts 300 customers. Every $5 decrease in price attracts an additional 30 customers. How can we find the price that will maximize the revenue?
    ðŸ”—
    In order to find revenue \(R=pq\text{,}\) we must first find the demand equation, meaning the equation relating price to demand. If price, \(p\text{,}\) is 80, the number of trips sold, \(q\text{,}\) is 300. If \(p\) is 75, then \(q\) is 330, and so on. Because demand changes by a constant (30 people) for every $5 drop in price, \(q\) is a linear function of \(p\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \text{ Slope } = \frac{300-330}{80-75} = -\frac{30}{5} = -6\text{ people/dollar }\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so the demand equation is \(q=-6p+b\text{.}\) Since \(p=80\) when \(q=300\text{,}\) we have (using the point-slope equation)
    \begin{equation*} q = -6(p-80)+300 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} q= -6p+780 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The demand equation is \(q=-6p+780\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Since revenue \(R=pq\text{,}\) revenue as a function of price is
    \begin{equation*} R(p) = p(-6p+780) = -6p^2+780p \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    To find the maximum of the revenue function, we differentiate:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}R'(p) \amp = -12p+780 = 0\\ p \amp = \frac{780}{12} = 65. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    There is a single critical point on the domain \(p\geq 0\) of \(R(p)\text{,}\) so as long as we verify that the critical point is a local maximum, it must be the global maximum by the Single Critical Point Theorem. Using the second derivative test, we calculate
    \begin{equation*} R''(p) = -12 \end{equation*}
    and so in particular,
    \begin{equation*} R''(65) = -12\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    This tells us that \(R(p)\) is concave down at the critical point of \(p=65\text{,}\) and so \(p=65\) is the location of a local maximum.
    ðŸ”—
    Thus the maximum revenue is achieved when the price is $65.
