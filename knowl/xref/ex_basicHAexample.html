Example 3.4.26. A Horizontal Asymptote.

We use tables of values to find \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x}{x-1}\) and \(\ds\lim_{x\to-\infty}\frac{x}{x-1}\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
To see what happens to \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) as \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) we choose a sequence of values of \(x\) that gets larger and larger, and record the corresponding (rounded) values of \(\frac{x}{x-1}\text{:}\)
ðŸ”—
As \(x\) grows larger, the quantity \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) approaches 1, so assuming that the pattern in the table continues for even larger values of \(x\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{x}{x-1} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We conclude that \(f(x) = \frac{x}{x-1}\) has a right horizontal asymptote at \(y=1\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
To see what happens to \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) as \(x\to-\infty\text{,}\) we choose a sequence of values of \(x\) that get larger and larger and are negative, and record the corresponding (rounded) values of \(\frac{x}{x-1}\text{:}\)
ðŸ”—
As \(x\) approaches negative infinity, the quantity \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) approaches \(0.999999\ldots = 1\text{,}\) so assuming that the pattern in the table continues for even larger values of \(x\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to-\infty}\frac{x}{x-1} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We conclude that \(f(x) = \frac{x}{x-1}\) has a left horizontal asymptote at \(y=1\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Thus we have seen that \(f(x) = \frac{x}{x-1}\) has a horizontal asymptote to both the right and left, and that the function approaches the finite value of \(y=1\) on both sides of its graph.
