Example 3.5.15.

For the function \(f(x) = \frac{x-3}{(x-3)(x-1)}\text{,}\) what form are the limits \(\ds\lim_{x\to 3}f(x)\) and \(\ds\lim_{x\to 1}f(x)\) originally? Does the form change after canceling? How does each limit evaluate in the end, and what visual feature of the graph does it describe?

Solution.
\(\ds\lim_{x\to 3} f(x) = \frac{0}{0}\) before cancelling. However, after cancelling, we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x-3}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \lim_{x\to 3}\frac{1}{x-1} = \frac{1}{3-1} = \frac{1}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This is neither form \(\frac{0}{0}\) nor is it \(\frac{1}{0}\text{.}\) Itâ€™s a finite value. This means that the function has a hole when \(x=3\) and that the \(y\)-value of the hole is \(y=\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) meaning that there is a hole in the graph at the point \(\left(3,\frac{1}{2}\right)\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
\(\ds\lim_{x\to 1} f(x) = \frac{-2}{(-2)(0)}\) has the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{.}\) After cancelling, it still has this form, but is simpler:
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x-3}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{1}{x-1} = \frac{1}{0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Letâ€™s work with the simplified expression \(\frac{1}{x-1}\text{.}\) A quick sign analysis tells us that \(x-1\) is positive to the right of \(x=1\) and negative to the left of \(x=1\text{,}\) so that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-}f(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^-}\frac{1}{x-1} = \frac{1}{0^-} = -\infty \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^+}f(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^+}\frac{1}{x-1} = \frac{1}{0^+} = \infty \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
This tells us that the function has a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\) and that the left side approaches \(-\infty\) while the right side approaches \(\infty\text{.}\)
