Example 6.3.16.

Hawksbill turtles are endangered and are extensively hunted as the traditional source of tortoiseshell. They currently have a population of 25,000 and the population is decreasing at a rate of 12% each year. How long will it be until there are under 5,000 hawksbill turtles left?
ðŸ”—
Solution.
We measure the population of hawksbill turtles with the function
\begin{equation*} P(t) = 25000(0.88)^t\text{,} \end{equation*}
using the growth rate of \(-12\%\) to determine the growth factor of \(b=1-0.12 = 0.88\text{.}\) We wish to solve the equation
\begin{equation*} 25000(0.88)^t = 5000\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
First divide by 25000:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}0.88^t \amp = \frac{5000}{25000}\\ 0.88^t\amp =0.2. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We could convert this to a logarithm with base \(0.88\) in order to solve for \(t\text{,}\) or we could apply any other base logarithm (such as a base-10 logarithm) to both sides and use TheoremÂ 6.3.13 part (c) to bring the \(t\) out of the exponent:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\log_{10}(0.88^t) \amp = \log_{10}(0.2)\\ t\cdot\log_{10}(0.88) \amp = \log_{10}(0.2)\\ t \amp = \frac{\log_{10}(0.2)}{\log_{10}(0.88)} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
The benefit to doing it this way is that most calculators have a \(\log_{10}\) key. Evaluating this, we get
\begin{equation*} t = \frac{\log_{10}(0.2)}{\log_{10}(0.88)}\approx 12.59\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
So in a bit more than 12 and a half years, there will be only 5,000 hawksbill turtles left.
