Example 7.1.14.

Suppose a population of wombats on a small island is growing at a rate proportional to the number of wombats on the island. If there were 12 wombats on the island in 1990 and 37 wombats on the island in 1998, how many wombats were on the island in the year 2010?
ðŸ”—
Solution.
We are given that the rate of change \(W'(t)\) of the population of wombats is proportional to the population \(W(t)\) of wombats at time \(t\text{;}\) in other words,
\begin{equation*} W'(t) = kW(t) \end{equation*}
for some real number \(k\text{.}\) By TheoremÂ 7.1.13, this means that the population \(W(t)\) of wombats on the island must be an exponential function
\begin{equation*} W(t) = Ae^{kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We now only need to find constants \(A\) and \(k\) that match the information given in the problem.
ðŸ”—
If we let \(t=0\) represent 1990 (so that \(t=8\) will represent 1998), then from the information given in the problem, we have
\begin{equation*} W(0) = 12\text{ and } W(8)=37\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We will use these two data points to find \(A\) and \(k\text{.}\) Using the first data point, we have
\begin{equation*} W(0) = Ae^0 = A = 12 \end{equation*}
so \(W(t) = 12e^{kt}\) for some \(k\text{.}\) Using the second data point, we can now solve for the value of \(k\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}W(8) = 12e^{k(8)} \amp =37\\ e^{8k} \amp =\frac{37}{12}\\ \ln(e^{8k}) \amp =\ln\left(\frac{37}{12}\right)\\ 8k \amp = \ln\left(\frac{37}{12}\right)\\ k \amp = \frac{\ln\left(\frac{37}{12}\right)}{8}\approx 0.14 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Thus \(W(t) = 12e^{0.14 t}\text{.}\) Using this function, we can now easily calculate the number of wombats that were on the island in the year 2010. Since 2010 is 20 years after 1990, we need to find
\begin{equation*} W(20) = 12e^{0.14(20)}\approx 197.34\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
In the year 2010, there were approximately 197 wombats on the island, assuming of course that we cannot have "parts" of wombats.
