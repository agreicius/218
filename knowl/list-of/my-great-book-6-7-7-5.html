Example 4.6.17.

Find the derivative of
\begin{equation*} h(x) = (x^2-1)^5(3x-1)^2 \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Solution.
The function \(h(x)\) at its outermost layer is a product of \((x^2-1)^5\) and \((3x-1)^2\text{.}\) We begin by applying the product rule, using Leibniz notation to denote derivatives that we have not yet computed:
\begin{equation*} h'(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\Big[(x^2-1)^5\Big]\cdot (3x-1)^2 + (x^2-1)^5\cdot \frac{d}{dx}\Big[(3x-1)^2\Big] \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We must use the chain rule to find the derivatives \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[(x^2-1)^5\right]\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[(3x-1)^2\right]\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[(x^2-1)^5\right] = 5(x^2-1)^4(2x) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\left[(3x-1)^2\right] = 2(3x-1)^1(3) \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Putting these into the product rule that we already started, we have
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}h'(x) \amp = \frac{d}{dx}\Big[(x^2-1)^5\Big]\cdot (3x-1)^2 + (x^2-1)^5\cdot \frac{d}{dx}\Big[(3x-1)^2\Big]\\ \amp =\left(5(x^2-1)^4(2x)\right)\cdot (3x-1)^2+(x^2-1)^5\cdot \left(2(3x-1)^1(3)\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
This expression can be simplified substantially. To do so, first notice that there are common factors of \(2\text{,}\) \((x^2-1)^4\text{,}\) and \((3x-1)\) in both terms that can be pulled out:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}h'(x) \amp =\left(5(x^2-1)^4(2x)\right)\cdot (3x-1)^2+(x^2-1)^5\cdot \left(2(3x-1)^1(3)\right)\\ \amp = 2(x^2-1)^4(3x-1)\left[5(x)(3x-1)+(x^2-1)(3)\right] \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Lastly, we can simplify the remaining expression inside the brackets, leaving the rest factored:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}h'(x) \amp = 2(x^2-1)^4(3x-1)\left[5(x)(3x-1)+(x^2-1)(3)\right]\\ \amp = 2(x^2-1)^4(3x-1)[15x^2-5x+3x^2-3]\\ \amp = 2(x^2-1)^4(3x-1)(18x^2-5x-3) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
While simplifying to this final form is not essential (WeBWorK will not require you to simplify), it will be necessary for actually being able to use the derivative in the context of an application.
