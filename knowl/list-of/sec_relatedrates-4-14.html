Example 5.2.6.

The circumference of a circular shadow is increasing at a rate of 3 inches per minute. At what rate is the area of the shadow changing when the circumference is 16 inches?
ðŸ”—
Solution.
Meet the Problem: The quantities involved in the problem are the circumference, \(C\text{,}\) and area, \(A\text{,}\) of a circle.
ðŸ”—
ðŸ”—
We are told that the circumference is increasing at a rate of 3 inches per minute, so if we let \(t\) represent time in minutes, we can write \(\frac{dC}{dt} =3\text{.}\) We are asked to find the rate at which the area of the shadow is changing, so we would like to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\) Specifically, we want to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) when \(C=16\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Make a Plan: It is not entirely obvious how to get an equation involving the circumference \(C\) and area \(A\) of a circle. Since the area and radius are related, and the radius and circumference are related, we may reasonably expect the area and the circumference to be related. That means we should be able to find an equation containing both \(A\) and \(C\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
The area and radius are related via
\begin{equation*} A=\pi r^2 \end{equation*}
while the circumference and radius are related via
\begin{equation*} C=2\pi r\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can find an equation involving only \(A\) and \(C\) by solving one equation for \(r\) and substituting it into the other equation, eliminating the variable \(r\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Once we have that equation, we take its derivative and plug in the information that we are given: \(C=16\) and \(\frac{dC}{dt}=3\text{,}\) then solve for \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Carry out Your Plan: The equation \(C=2\pi r\) is easier to solve for \(r\text{,}\) so we solve for \(r\) in terms of \(C\) and substitute into \(A=\pi r^2\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}C \amp =2 \pi r\\ \frac{C}{2\pi} \amp = r \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
and so
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}A \amp = \pi r^2\\ A \amp =\pi \left(\frac{C}{2\pi}\right)^2\\ A \amp = \frac{C^2}{4\pi} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
where we have simplified and cancelled one of the \(\pi\)â€™s in the last step.
ðŸ”—
So we see that, yes indeed, the area and circumference of a circle are related, specifically by the equation
\begin{equation*} A=\frac{C^2}{4\pi} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We take the derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}\) of both sides. Note that we do not actually need to use the quotient rule because \(4\pi\) is a constant, so we rewrite the equation first to make this more obvious:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \frac{d}{dt}\left[\frac{1}{4\pi} \cdot C^2\right]\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4\pi}\cdot 2C\, \frac{dC}{dt}\\ \amp = \frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Plugging in \(C=16\) and \(\frac{dC}{dt}=3\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{C=16} = \frac{16}{2\pi}\cdot 3 = \frac{24}{\pi}\approx 7.6394 \text{ ft } ^2/\text{ min } \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Reflect Back: We can check that our answer makes sense in context. In particular, the area is increasing since the derivative is positive, which it should since the circle is getting larger. The units are correct since area is measured in ft\(^2\) while time is measured in feet. We can even notice that the area is increasing faster than the circumference, which makes sense since area is proportional to the square of the circumference in the original equation we had.
ðŸ”—
Critique and Change the Problem: Here we could think about ways to change the problem. For instance, what if we were given the rate of change of the area and were asked to find the rate of change of the circumference? Our work would be mostly the same, except for the final step when we plug in and solve. If we are additionally given the value of the area instead of the circumference, we would have to do a bit more work.
ðŸ”—
For example: what if we are told that the area is decreasing by 2 ft\(^2\)/min and are asked to find the rate at which the circumference decreases when the area is 9 ft\(^2\text{?}\) Then we can plug in \(\frac{dA}{dt}=-2\) but we would still need a value of \(C\) in order to solve for \(\frac{dC}{dt}\) in
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}=\frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since we are told that \(A=9\text{,}\) we can use the original equation \(A=\frac{C^2}{4\pi}\) to find the value of \(C\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}9 \amp =\frac{C^2}{4\pi}\\ 36\pi \amp = C^2\\ \pm\sqrt{36\pi} \amp = C\\ \pm6\sqrt{\pi} \amp = C \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Circumference is a length and should be positive, so \(C = 6\sqrt{\pi}\approx 10.6347\text{.}\) Now we can plug in and solve for \(\frac{dC}{dt}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -2 \amp = \frac{6\sqrt{\pi}}{4\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -2\cdot \frac{4\pi}{6\sqrt{\pi}} \amp =\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -\frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{\pi}} \amp =\frac{dC}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
And so the circumference is decreasing by \(\frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{\pi}}\approx 2.3633\) ft/min.
