Example 3.6.8.

We could factor the expression \(x^2-x^3\) by dividing out \(x^2\) from both terms:
\begin{equation*} x^2-x^3 = x^2(1-x) \end{equation*}

Now when we take the limit, we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty} x^2-x^3 = \lim_{x\to\infty} x^2(1-x) = (\infty)(-\infty) = -\infty \end{equation*}
since the product of two very large numbers will be very large, and if one number is positive while the other is negative, the product will be negative. This also makes sense if we consider the fact that \(x^3\) will get larger faster than \(x^2\text{,}\) so that the expression \(x^2-x^3\) will become be overwhelmed by the \(x^3\) term for large values of \(x\text{.}\)
