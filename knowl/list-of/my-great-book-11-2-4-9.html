Example 9.1.10. Riding a Ferris Wheel.

A Ferris wheel with a radius of 10 m is rotating at a rate of one revolution every 2 minutes. How fast is a rider rising when their seat is 16 m above ground level?

Solution.
Hopefully this evokes memories of the beginnings of our exploration of trigonometric functions: the vertical movement of a point around the unit circle is described by the sine function. With this in mind, letâ€™s draw a circle of radius 10 representing our Ferris wheel and set our origin at the center of the circle. This means that "ground level" is at \(y=-10\text{:}\)
We are told in the problem that \(\theta\) rotates once around every two minutes, so halfway around every minute, meaning \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi\) rad/min. 16 m above ground level is when \(y=6\text{,}\) hence we are asked to find \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) when \(y=6\text{,}\) given that \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
In this problem, we relate \(y\) and \(\theta\) using sine function and the constant hypotenuse:
\begin{equation*} \sin\theta = \frac{y}{10} \end{equation*}
and solving for \(y\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} y = 10\sin\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
Taking the derivative,
\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt} = 10\cos\theta\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
In order to find \(\cos\theta\) when \(y=6\text{,}\) we use the Pythagorean Theorem to find \(x\text{.}\) We have that \(x^2+6^2 = 10^2\) when \(x = \sqrt{100-36} = \sqrt{64} = 8\text{.}\) Hence \(\cos\theta = \frac{8}{10} = \frac{4}{5}\text{.}\) Now we can simplify
\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt}\Big|_{\substack{y=6\\ \frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi} } = 10\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)(\pi) = 8\pi\approx 25.13 \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Assuming that the rider is on the way up, their seat is rising at a rate of about \(25.13\) meters per minute (equal to about 1.37 feet per second).
