Example 4.2.25.

A water balloon is tossed vertically in the air from a window. The balloonâ€™s height in feet at time \(t\) in seconds after being launched is given by \(s(t)=-16t^2+16t+32\text{,}\) corresponding to an initial velocity of 16 ft/sec and an initial height of 32 ft.
ðŸ”—
We can see from the picture (or from factoring \(s(t)\)) that the balloon hits the ground \(t=2\) seconds after it is launched.
ðŸ”—
The average velocity of the balloon in its last second is
\begin{equation*} AV_{[1,2]} = \frac{s(2)-s(1)}{2-1} = \frac{0-32}{1} = -32\text{ feet/second }\text{.} \end{equation*}
This means that, during the second before it hits the ground, the balloon falls at an average speed of 32 feet each second.
ðŸ”—
Letâ€™s find the instantaneous velocity of the balloon at \(t=1\) second. Using the limit definition to compute the instantaneous rate of change of \(s\) with respect to time \(t\text{,}\) at the instant \(t=1\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} s'(1) =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{s(1+h)-s(1)}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We compute \(s(1) = -16(1)^2+16(1)+32 = 32\) and \(s(1+h) = -16(1+h)^2+16(1+h)+32 = -16h^2-16h+32\text{.}\) Substituting into the formula for \(s'(1)\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} s'(1) =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(-16h^2-16h+32)-32}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-16h^2-16h}{h}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Now we can factor an \(h\) and cancel (notice how that keeps happening?!?), to get
\begin{equation*} s'(1) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{h(-16h-16)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}-16h-16 = -16(0)-16 = -16\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
This tells us that the velocity of the balloon at precisely \(t=1\) second after being launched is \(v(1)=s'(1)=-16\) feet/second. Note that the units for the derivative are the same as the units for the average velocity, since the derivative is the limit of average velocities.
ðŸ”—
This instantaneous velocity at \(t=1\) has a smaller magnitude than the average velocity on the interval \([1,2]\text{.}\) We can intuit that the balloon will speed up as it falls, so it makes sense for the average speed on the interval to be larger than the instantaneous speed at the beginning.
ðŸ”—
In order to confirm this intuition, letâ€™s find the velocity of the balloon the entire time between when it is launched and when it hits the ground. Now, we use the limit definition to compute the derivative for any time \(t\text{.}\) Our calculations will be very similar to those above, with \(1\) replaced by \(t\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}s'(t) \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{s(t+h)-s(t)}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{(-16(t+h)^2+16(t+h)+32)-(-16t^2+16t+32)}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-16(t^2+2th+h^2)+16t+16h+32+16t^2-16t-32}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-16t^2-32th-16h^2+16h+16t^2}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{-32th-16h^2+16h}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{h(-16h-32t+16)}{h}\\ \amp =\lim_{h\to 0}(-16h-32t+16)\\ \amp =-16(0)-32t+16 \\ \amp = -32t+16 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Thus we see that at any time \(t\text{,}\) the velocity of the balloon is \(v(t)=s'(t)=-32t+16\) feet/second. For example, at \(t=1\text{,}\) we confirm our earlier calculation that the velocity is \(v(1) = -32(1)+16 = -16\) ft/sec. We can also see very quickly that
\begin{equation*} v(1.5) = -32(1.5)+16 = -32\text{ ft/sec } \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} v(1.9) = -32(1.9)+16 = -44.8\text{ ft/sec } \end{equation*}
and that
\begin{equation*} v(2) = -32(2)+16 = -48\text{ ft/sec. } \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
These numbers confirm our intuition that the balloon will speed up as it falls.
ðŸ”—
Notice also that there appears to be a point where the velocity is zero. Indeed, \(v(t) = -32t+16 = 0\) when \(t=0.5\) On the graph of \(s(t)\text{,}\) this is the vertex of the parabola where there is a horizontal tangent line. In terms of the motion of the balloon, this is the moment when the balloon transitions from rising to falling, and is momentarily still in the air.
