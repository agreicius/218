Solution.
  1. First observe that if \(r(x) = \frac{g(x)}{f(x)}\text{,}\) then \(r'(x) = \frac{f(x)g'(x)-g(x)f'(x)}{(f(x))^2}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}r'(2) \amp = \frac{f(2)g'(2)-g(2)f'(2)}{(f(2))^2}\\ \amp = \frac{(5)(2)-(-3)(-1/2)}{(5)^2}\\ \amp =\frac{10-3/2}{25}\\ \amp = \frac{17/2}{25}\\ \amp = \frac{17}{50} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

  3. First note that we expect \(r(2.06)\) to be larger than \(r(2)\) since \(r\) is increasing at \(x=2\) by part (b). \(r(2) = \frac{g(2)}{f(2)} = -\frac{3}{5}\text{.}\) The local linearization of \(r\) at the point \((2,r(2))\) is
    \begin{equation*} y=\frac{17}{50}(x-2)-\frac{3}{5}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} r(2.06)\approx \frac{17}{50}(2.06-2)-\frac{3}{5} = \frac{17}{50}(0.06)-\frac{3}{5} = -0.5796\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(r(2) = -\frac{3}{5} = -0.6\text{,}\) \(r(2.06)\) is indeed larger than \(r(2)\text{,}\) as we expected.
