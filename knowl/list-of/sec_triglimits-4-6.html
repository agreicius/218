Example 8.6.11.

Here, we will calculate
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\pi}\csc x \end{equation*}
without relying on the graph of the function in order to verify that the limit has the behavior suggested by the graph.

Solution.
Since \(\csc x = \frac{1}{\sin x}\) and \(\sin(-\pi)=0\text{,}\) \(\ds\lim_{x\to -\pi}\csc x\) is of the form \(\frac{1}{0}\) and therefore the function \(y=\csc x\) will have a vertical asymptote at \(x=-\pi\text{.}\) However, we donâ€™t yet know if the function approaches \(-\infty\) or \(\infty\) from the left and the righta.

From the left, as \(x\to-\pi^-\text{,}\) we are considering angles slightly less than \(-\pi\text{;}\) these angles terminate in the second quadrant just above the negative horizontal-axis (for instance, \(-\pi-0.1\)). For such angles, the sine function is positive, and therefore as \(x\to-\pi^-\text{,}\) we have \(\sin x\to 0^+\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\pi^-}\csc x =\frac{1}{0^+} = \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
Similarly, from the right, as \(x\to -\pi^+\text{,}\) we have \(\sin x\to 0^-\) (these angles terminate in the third quadrant just below the negative horizontal-axis) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\pi^+}\csc x =\frac{1}{0^-} = -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
This means that \(\lim_{x\to -\pi}\csc x\) does not exist, and we have verified that \(\csc x\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x=-\pi\) which approaches \(\infty\) to the left and \(-\infty\) to the right.
