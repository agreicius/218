Theorem 2.18.15. Trigonometry of arbitrary circles.

Let \(r\in (0,\infty)\text{,}\) and let \(C_r\) be the circle \(x^2+y^2=r^2\) of radius \(r\text{.}\) Given \(\theta\in \R\text{,}\) let \(P_{r,\theta}=(x,y)\) be the intersection of the terminal edge of the standard central angle \(\theta\) with the circle \(C_r\text{,}\) as depicted in Figure 2.18.16. We have
\begin{align} x \amp =r\cos\theta \amp y\amp=r\sin\theta\tag{2.84} \end{align}
from which it follows that
\begin{align} \cos\theta \amp =\frac{x}{r} \amp \sin\theta=\frac{y}{r}\tag{2.85}\\ \tan\theta \amp =\frac{y}{x} \amp \cot\theta\amp =\frac{x}{y}\tag{2.86}\\ \sec\theta \amp =\frac{r}{x} \amp \csc\theta\amp =\frac{r}{y}\text{.}\tag{2.87} \end{align}
🔗
🔗

Proof.

This slight generalization of the definitions of the trigonometric functions in terms of the unit circle, is a consequence of the law of similar triangles. As indicated in Figure 2.18.17, we simply relate the reference triangle associated to \(P_{r,\theta}\) to the reference triangle for the point \(P_\theta\) where the terminal edge of the angle \(\theta\) intersects the unit circle.
🔗
The two right triangles in this diagram are similar. As a result, comparing leg lengths to the hypotenuse, we have
\begin{align*} \frac{\cos\theta}{1} \amp =\frac{x}{r} \\ \frac{\sin\theta}{1} \amp =\frac{y}{r} \end{align*}
and hence
\begin{align*} x \amp =r\cos\theta\\ y \amp =r\sin\theta\text{.} \end{align*}
The rest of the identies in the theorem follow from the definitions of the remaining trigonometric functions.
