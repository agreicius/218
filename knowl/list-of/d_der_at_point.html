Definition 1.18.1. Derivative at a point.

Suppose \(a\in \R\) is an interior point or endpoint of the domain of \(f\text{.}\) We say that \(f\) is differentiable at \(a\) if the limit
\begin{equation} \lim\limits_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h+a)-f(a)}{h},\tag{1.83} \end{equation}
or equivalently, the limit
\begin{equation} \lim\limits_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\text{,}\tag{1.84} \end{equation}
exists. When this is the case, we call this limit the derivative of \(f\) at \(a\text{,}\) denoted \(f'(a)\text{:}\) i.e.,
\begin{equation} f'(a)= \lim\limits_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h+a)-f(a)}{h}=\lim\limits_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\text{.}\tag{1.85} \end{equation}
