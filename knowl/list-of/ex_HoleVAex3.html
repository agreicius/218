Example 3.5.17.

For the function \(h(x) = \frac{(x-3)^2}{(x-3)^3(x-1)}\text{,}\) what form are the limits \(\ds\lim_{x\to 3}g(x)\) and \(\ds\lim_{x\to 1}g(x)\) originally? Does the form change after cancelling? How does each limit evaluate in the end, and what visual feature of the graph does it describe?

Solution.
\(\ds\lim_{x\to 3} h(x) = \frac{0}{0}\) before cancelling. However, after cancelling, we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3} \frac{(x-3)^2}{(x-3)^3(x-1)} = \lim_{x\to 3}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(3-3)(3-1)} = \frac{1}{(0)(2)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This has the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{,}\) so is infinite. Splitting up into right and left limits, we have
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3^-}h(x) = \lim_{x\to 3^-}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(0^-)(2)} = -\infty \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 3^+}h(x) = \lim_{x\to 3^+}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(0^+)(2)} = \infty \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
This tells us that the function has a vertical asymptote at \(x=3\) and that the left side approaches \(-\infty\) while the right side approaches \(\infty\text{.}\)

\(\ds\lim_{x\to 1}h(x) = \frac{(-2)^2}{(-2)^3(0)}\) has the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{.}\) After cancelling, it still has this form, but is simpler:
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1} \frac{(x-3)^2}{(x-3)^3(x-1)} = \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(-2)(0)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Letâ€™s work with the simplified expression \(\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)}\text{.}\) We know that \(x-1\) is positive to the right of \(x=1\) and negative to the left of \(x=1\text{,}\) so that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-}h(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^-}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(-2)(0^-)} = \infty \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^+}h(x) = \lim_{x\to 1^+}\frac{1}{(x-3)(x-1)} = \frac{1}{(-2)(0^+)} = -\infty \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
This tells us that the function has a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\) and that the left side approaches \(\infty\) while the right side approaches \(-\infty\text{.}\)
