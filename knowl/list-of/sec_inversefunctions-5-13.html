Example 6.1.7.

For instance, if \(w=f(z) = 2z+1\text{,}\) we can show that the function \(z=g(w)=\dfrac{w-1}{2}\) reverses the effect of \(f\) (and vice versa), and thus \(g=f^{-1}\text{.}\) We observe that
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}w\amp = 2z+1\\ w-1 \amp = 2z\\ \frac{w-1}{2} \amp = z \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation*} w=f(z)=2z+1\qquad \textrm{ and } \qquad z=f^{-1}(w)=\dfrac{w-1}{2} \end{equation*}
are equivalent forms of the same equation, and thus they say the same thing from two different perspectives. The first version of the equation is solved for \(w\) in terms of \(z\text{,}\) while the second equation is solved for \(z\) in terms of \(w\text{.}\)
