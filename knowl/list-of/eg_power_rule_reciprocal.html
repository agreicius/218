Solution.
  1. The key here is to make explicit that \(f\) is a power function using algebra:
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{1}{x^2}=x^{-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now it is a simple matter of applying the power function formula for the derivative:
    \begin{align*} f'(x) \amp =\left( x^{-2}\right)'\\ \amp = -2x^{-2-1} \amp (\text{power func.})\\ \amp = -2x^{-3}\\ \amp =-\frac{2}{x^3}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Note that our answer was technically complete once we had \(f'(x)=-2x^{-3}\text{.}\) However, we usually prefer power expressions that use positive exponents, especially if we wish to evaluate the expression, as in the next part.
