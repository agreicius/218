Since the derivative \(g'(x)\) is positive for \(x\lt -1\text{,}\) we know that the function \(g(x)\) must be increasing for \(x\lt -1\text{.}\) Because the derivative \(g'(x)\) has a zero at \(x=-1\) and switches to negative, we know that the function \(g(x)\) must have a local maximum at \(x=-1\) and decrease from \(x=-1\) to \(x=2\text{.}\) The derivative \(g'(x)\) is positive for \(x>2\text{,}\) so the function \(g(x)\) is increasing for \(x>2\text{.}\) The open dots on the graph at \(x=2\) tell us that the derivative is undefined at \(x=2\text{,}\) so \(g(x)\) is not differentiable at \(x=2\text{,}\) but we do not know in what way \(g(x)\) fails to be differentiable.
