Example 5.2.3. Ripples in a Pond.

A stone dropped in a pond creates circular ripples that spread out from the center where the stone was dropped. If the radius of a particular circle is increasing at a constant rate of half a foot per second, we can ask how fast the area inside the circle is increasing at the moment the radius is 3 feet.
ðŸ”—
Meet the Problem: Having read the problem, we begin by drawing a diagram of a circle with radius \(r\text{,}\) including an arrow indicating that the radius is increasing.
ðŸ”—
ðŸ”—
The problem involves the area \(A\) of the circle as well. The quantities \(A\) and \(r\) are not just variables, but are also functions \(A(t)\) and \(r(t)\) of time since the problem suggests that they are changing. We are told that when \(r=3\text{,}\) the radius is changing at a positive rate of 0.5 ft/sec. We can write this mathematically as \(\frac{dr}{dt}=0.5\text{.}\) Note that the derivative is respect to \(t\) because the rate describes how \(r\) changes over time. We are asked to find how fast the area is changing, again inferring that we are measuring over time, meaning we wish to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Make a Plan: We know that \(A\) and \(r\) are related by \(A=\pi r^2\text{.}\) If we take the derivative with respect to \(t\) (NOT \(r\text{;}\) see emphasis on using \(t\) as the independent variable in the previous paragraph), then we will know how \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\) are related. We expect to need to plug in the information that we are given and then arrive at a number for \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Carry out Your Plan:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{d}{dt}[A] \amp = \frac{d}{dt}[\pi r^2]\\ \frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \pi \left(2r\,\frac{dr}{dt}\right)\\ \frac{dA}{dt} \amp = 2\pi r\, \frac{dr}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Note that we have used that \(A\) and \(r\) are implicit functions of \(t\text{,}\) and so have needed to use the chain rule when taking the derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}[r^2] = 2r\,\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
At this point, we have "related the rates" of changes of the area and the radius, \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\) Then if we remember that we are given \(r=3\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt} = 0.5\text{,}\) we can see that
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt} \Big|_{r=3}= 2\pi (3)(0.5) = 3\pi\approx 9.42\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec } \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Reflect Back: We have successfully found a value for the quantity \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) when \(r=3\text{,}\) as asked for in the problem. Our units are accurate since \(A\) is measured in square feet and \(t\) is measured in seconds. The positive answer makes sense since the area should be increasing as the radius increases. The area is additionally increasing at a larger rate than the radius, which also makes sense since the area of a circle of radius \(r=3\) is proportional to the square of the radius, so should increase more.
ðŸ”—
Critique and Change the Problem: Here is an observation that goes beyond what was asked in the problem: Notice that even though the radius of the circle is changing at a constant rate, the area of the circle will not be changing at a constant rate. As the radius gets larger, the change in the area will increase. When the radius is \(r=5\text{,}\) the change in the area will be
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=5} = 2\pi(5)(0.5) = 5\pi\approx 15.7\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec }\text{.} \end{equation*}
When the radius is \(r=10\text{,}\) the change in the area will be
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=10} = 2\pi(10)(0.5) = 10\pi\approx 31.4\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec }\text{.} \end{equation*}
When the radius of is \(r=20\) feet, the change in the area will be
\begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=20} = 2\pi(20)(0.5) = 20\pi\approx 62.8\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec } \end{equation*}
