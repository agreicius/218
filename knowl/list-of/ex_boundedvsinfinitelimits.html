As \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) \(\sin x\) oscillates between \(-1\) and \(1\text{.}\) Your first thought might be that since \(x\) is approaching \(\infty\text{,}\) the function \(x\sin x\) will also approach infinity. However, because \(\sin x\) oscillates between negative and positive numbers, so will the function \(x\sin x\text{.}\) Note that as \(x\) increases in magnitude, so does \(x\sin x\text{.}\) Therefore as \(x\to \infty\text{,}\) the quantity \(x\sin x\) oscillates without converging to any real number, and therefore \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}x \sin x\) does not exist. One way to express this is that \(x\sin x\) "oscillates with increasing magnitude and without bound" as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\)

Again, as \(x\to \infty\text{,}\) \(\sin x\) oscillates between \(-1\) and \(1\text{.}\) Therefore the numerator \(\sin x\) stays bounded (meaning it never becomes infinitely large) while the denominator \(x\) becomes infinitely large. A bounded quantity divided by an infinite quantity that increases without bound is zero; in other words, loosely speaking,
