Example 6.5.9.

Consider the function \(f(x)=\frac{3}{2+2^{-4x}}\text{.}\) What are the horizontal asymptotes of \(f(x)\text{?}\)
ðŸ”—
Solution.
To find horizontal asymptotes, we must compute the limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\to \infty\) as well as \(x\to-\infty\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to \infty\text{,}\) note that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to \infty} 2^{-4x} = 0 \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}f(x)=\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3}{2+2^{-4x}}=\frac{3}{2+0}=\frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to- \infty\text{,}\) note that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\infty} 2^{-4x} = \infty \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to-\infty}f(x)=\lim_{x\to-\infty}\frac{3}{2+2^{-4x}}=\frac{3}{2+\infty}=\frac{3}{\infty}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
The graph of \(f(x)=\frac{3}{2+2^{-4x}}\) therefore has two different horizontal asymptotes to the right and left. To the right there is an asymptote at \(y=\frac{3}{2}\text{,}\) and to the left there is an asymptote at \(y=0\text{.}\) We can check this by looking at the graph:
