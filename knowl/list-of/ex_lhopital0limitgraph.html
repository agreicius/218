Example 7.2.2. Graphically Comparing Functions in a $\frac{0}{0}$ Limit.

For another example, consider the limit
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to0\text{,}\) we have \(x^2\to0\) and \((2^x-1)\to (1-1)\to0\) and thus this limit is of the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) This indeterminate limit cannot be simplified with algebra. So what can we do?
ðŸ”—
We can approach the problem graphically. The graphs of \(f(x)=x^2\) and \(g(x)=2^x-1\) are shown below. Since we are interested in the limit as \(x\to0\text{,}\) we should focus on what happens as we look at smaller and smaller graphing windows around \(x=0\text{,}\) as shown in the second and third graphs.
ðŸ”—
Near \(x=0\text{,}\) the graph of \(f(x)=x^2\) loks a lot like its horizontal tangent line \(y=0\text{,}\) and the graph of \(g(x)=2^x-1\) looks a lot like its tangent line \(y=x\text{.}\) Thus, we would anticipate the behavior of the quotient \(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}\) as \(x\to0\) to be similar to that of the quotient \(\frac{0}{x}\) of the corresponding tangent lines at \(x=0\text{.}\) From this information it would be reasonable to guess that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0}\frac{x^2}{2^x-1}=\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{0}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}0=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
