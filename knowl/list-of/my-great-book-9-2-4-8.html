Example 7.1.5.

Sketch an accurate labeled graph of the function \(f(x) = \dfrac{6}{4-2^x}\text{.}\) Include complete sign chart analyses of \(f, f'\text{,}\) and \(f''\text{,}\) and calculate any relevant limits.

Solution.
Domain: \(f(x)\) is undefined when its denominator is zero. Solving for \(x\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}4-2^x \amp = 0\\ 4 \amp = 2^{x}\\ 2^2 \amp = 2^x\\ 2 \amp = 2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
we see that \(f(x)\) has domain \((-\infty,2)\cup(2,\infty)\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
\(x\)-intercepts \(f(x)\) has no \(x\)-intercepts because its numerator is \(6\) and is never equal to 0.
ðŸ”—
Limits. Since the domain of \(f\) is \((-\infty,2)\cup(2,\infty)\text{,}\) we must investigate the limits of \(f(x) = \frac{6}{4-2^x}\) as \(x\to\pm\infty\) and as \(x\to 2\) from the left and the right.
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to 2^-\) we have \(4-2^x\to 0^+\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 2^-}\frac{6}{4-2^x} = \frac{6}{0^+} = \infty\text{,} \end{equation*}
and as \(x\to 2^+\) we have \(4-2^x\to 0^-\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 2^+}\frac{6}{4-2^x} = \frac{6}{0^-} = -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus the graph of \(f\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\text{,}\) where the graph approaches \(\infty\) to the left of 2 and approaches \(-\infty\) to the right of \(2\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to\infty\) the denominator \(4-2^x\) of \(f(x)\) approaches \(-\infty\text{,}\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{6}{4-2^x} = \frac{6}{-\infty} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
As \(x\to-\infty\text{,}\) the denominator \(4-2^x\) approaches \(4\text{,}\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to-\infty}\frac{6}{4-2^x} = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This information tells us that the graph of \(f\) has two horizontal asymptotes: at \(y=0\) on the right and at \(y=\frac{3}{2}\) on the left.
ðŸ”—
Derivatives: Letâ€™s begin by finding and simplifying the first and second derivatives of \(f(x)\text{.}\) The first derivative of \(f(x) = 6(4-2^x)^{-1}\) is
\begin{equation*} f'(x) = 6(-1)(4-2^x)^{-2}(-(\ln 2)2^x) = \frac{6(\ln 2)2^x}{(4-2^x)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Differentiating that result using the quotient rule and then factoring to simplify as much as possible so that we can easily identify roots, we find that the second derivative of \(f\) is
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}f''(x) \amp = \frac{6(\ln 2)(\ln 2)2^x(4-2^x)^2-(6(\ln 2)2^x)(2)(4-2^x)^1(-(\ln 2)2^x)}{(4-2^x)^4}\\ \amp =\frac{6(\ln 2)^22^x(4-2^x)((4-2^x)+2\cdot 2^x)}{(4-2^x)^4}\\ \amp =\frac{6(\ln 2)^2 2^x(4+2^x)}{(4-2^x)^3}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
To determine the intervals on which \(f, f'\) and \(f''\) are positive and negative we must first locate the values of \(x\) for which these functions are zero or do not exist. The function
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{6}{4-2^x} \end{equation*}
is never zero, but is undefined where its denominator is zero, at \(x=2\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Looking at the formula for
\begin{equation*} f'(x) \frac{6(\ln 2)2^x}{(4-2^x)^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}
we can see that it is never zero (since \(2^x\) is never zero), but is also undefined at \(x=2\text{.}\) Thus \(x=2\) is the only critical point of \(f\) and the only point we will mark on the number line for \(f'\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Similarly, from the formula for
\begin{equation*} f''(x) = \frac{6(\ln 2)^2 2^x(4+2^x)}{(4-2^x)^3}\text{,} \end{equation*}
we can see that \(f''(x)\) is never zero (since neither \(2^x\) nor \(4+2^x\) can ever be zero), but it is undefined at \(x=2\text{.}\)

The graph of \(f\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x=2\text{,}\) where the graph approaches \(\infty\) to the left of 2 and approaches \(-\infty\) to the right of \(2\text{.}\) The graph of \(f\) has two horizontal asymptotes: at \(y=0\) on the right and at \(y=\frac{3}{2}\) on the left.
