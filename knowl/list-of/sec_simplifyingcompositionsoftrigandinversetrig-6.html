Example 8.5.5. Testing Values.

To verify the remarkable fact that \(\cos(\sin^{-1}x) = \sqrt{1-x^2}\text{,}\) we evaluate both sides at some simple \(x\)-values. While looking at just a few \(x\)-values will not prove that the two expressions are equal for all \(x\text{,}\) it will at least give us some evidence that the equality is reasonable.
ðŸ”—
For example, at \(x=0\) we have
\begin{equation*} \cos(\sin^{-1} 0) = \cos 0 = 1\text{ and } \sqrt{1-0^2} =\sqrt{1} = 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
and at \(x=1\) we have
\begin{equation*} \cos(\sin^{-1} 1) = \cos \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = 0\text{ and } \sqrt{1-1^2} =\sqrt{0} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

As a less trivial example, consider \(x=\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) At this value we have
\begin{equation*} \cos\left(\sin^{-1} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\right) = \cos \left(\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\text{ and } \sqrt{1-\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2} =\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{4}} =\sqrt{\frac{3}{4}} =\frac{ \sqrt{3}}{2} \end{equation*}
