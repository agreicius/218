Solution.
{a.} If we let \(x=-5\) in \(y^3+xy+2=0\text{,}\) We get the cubic equation
\begin{equation*} y^3-5y+2=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
It just so happens that \(y^3-5y+2\) factors as \((y-2)(y^2+2y-1)\text{.}\) This is not a factorization that we can do by hand, but you can verify that it is true by simplifying the factored form.
ðŸ”—
If \((y-2)(y^2+2y-1)=0\) we must have either \(y-2=0\) or \(y^2+2y-1=0\text{.}\) The first has solution \(y=2\) and the second has solutions \(y=-1+\sqrt{2}\) and \(y=-1-\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) by the quadratic formula.
ðŸ”—
Thus \((-5,2)\text{,}\) \((-5,-1+\sqrt{2})\text{,}\) and \((-5,-1-\sqrt{2})\) are points on the graph of \(y^3+xy+2=0\text{.}\) Note that this alone shows that \(y\) could not possibly be written as an explicit function of \(x\text{,}\) since the curve does not satisfy the vertical line test.
ðŸ”—
{b.} Next, we find the slope of the tangent line to \(y^3+xy+2=0\) at the point \((-5,2)\text{.}\) We must first calculate \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\) Using implicit differentiation and the chain rule, with the product rule for the middle term, we have
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}y^3+xy+2 \amp = 0\\ \frac{d}{dx}[y^3+xy+2] \amp = \frac{d}{dx}[0]\\ \frac{d}{dx}[y^3]+\frac{d}{dx}[xy]+\frac{d}{dx}[2] \amp = 0\\ 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx}+\left((1)(y)+(x)\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)\right)+0 \amp = 0\\ 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx}+y+x\,\frac{dy}{dx} \amp = 0\\ 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx}+x\,\frac{dy}{dx} \amp = -y\\ \frac{dy}{dx}(3y^2+x) \amp = -y\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = \frac{-y}{3y^2+x} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Therefore the slope of the tangent line at the point \((a,b) = (-5,2)\) is
\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{(-5,2)} = \frac{-2}{3(2)^2+(-5)} = \frac{-2}{12-5} = -\frac{2}{7} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
{c.} The equation of the tangent line at the point \((-5,2)\) with slope \(-\frac{2}{7}\) is
\begin{equation*} y = -\frac{2}{7}(x+5)+2\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
