Example 7.3.19.

For time \(t\geq 0\text{,}\) in days, the rate at which photosynthesis takes place in the leaf of a plant, represented by the rate at which oxygen is produced, is approximated by
\begin{equation*} p(t) = 100(e^{-0.02t}-e^{-0.1t})\text{.} \end{equation*}
When is photosynthesis occurring the fastest? What is the rate at that time?

Solution.
Photosynthesis is fastest at the global maximum of \(p(t)\text{.}\) To find this maximum value, we first find critical points. We differentiate, set equal to zero, and solve for \(t\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}p'(t) = 100(-0.02e^{-0.02t}+0.1e^{-0.1t}) \amp = 0\\ -0.02e^{-0.02t} \amp =-0.1e^{-0.1t}\\ \frac{e^{-0.02t}}{e^{-0.1t}} \amp = \frac{0.1}{0.02}\\ e^{-0.02t+0.1t} \amp = 5\\ e^{0.08t} \amp = 5\\ 0.08t \amp = \ln 5\\ t \amp =\frac{\ln 5}{0.08}\approx 20.12\text{ days } \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
We are working on the interval \(t\geq 0\text{,}\) as so we compute \(p(0)\text{,}\) \(p(20.12)\text{,}\) and \(\ds\lim_{t\to\infty}p(t)\) to determine whether \(t\approx 20.12\) gives us the global maximum value we are looking for:
ðŸ”—
\(t\) \(p(t)=100(e^{-0.02t}-e^{-0.1t})\)
0 \(p(0) = 0\)
\(20.12\) \(p(20.12)\approx 53.50\)
\(\to\infty\) \(\lim_{t\to\infty}p(t) = 0\)
So we see that \(t\approx 20.12\) days does indeed give us the maximum value of \(p(t)\text{,}\) meaning the maximum rate at which oxygen is produced. We already calculated the maximum rate. It is
\begin{equation*} p(20.12) = 100(e^{-0.02(20.12)}-e^{-0.1(20.12)})\approx 53.50\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Photosynthesis occurs fastest after about 20 days. The maximum rate is about 53.5 units of oxygen per day.
