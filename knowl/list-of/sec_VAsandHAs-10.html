Example 3.4.24. A Vertical Asymptote.

The function \(f(x) = \frac{x}{x-1}\) is undefined at \(x=1\text{.}\) We create a table of values for \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches 1 from the left and right:
ðŸ”—
We can see two distinct patterns begin to emerge from the left and right of \(x=1\text{.}\) As \(x\) approaches 1 from the left, the values of \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) are negative and are getting bigger and bigger (we expect the next few numbers to be -9999, -99999, etc.). On the other hand, as \(x\) approaches 1 from the right, the values of \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) are positive and are getting bigger and bigger (on this side, we expect the next few numbers to be 10001, 100001, etc.). The common feature is that the function is getting larger on both sides, one negative and one positive, and the function will continue to get bigger and bigger the closer and closer we get to 1.
ðŸ”—
There is no limit (pun intended) to how large \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) will become near \(x=1\text{!}\)
ðŸ”—
We summarize this behavior as
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-} f(x) = -\infty\text{ and } \lim_{x\to 1^+} f(x) = \infty \end{equation*}
and conclude that \(f(x) = \frac{x}{x-1}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\text{.}\)
