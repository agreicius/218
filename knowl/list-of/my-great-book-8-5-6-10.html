Example 6.4.24.

An earthquake on July 30, 2018 in California measured 3.7 on the Richter scale. If an earthquake is 16,550 times more powerful than the California earthquake, what would be its magnitude on the Richter scale?
ðŸ”—
Solution.
Suppose that the California earthquake had maximum wave amplitude of \(C\text{.}\) Then the Richter scale magnitude of the California earthquake tells us that
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}3.7 \amp = \log_{10}\left(\frac{C}{A_0}\right)\\ \amp = \log_{10}C - \log_{10}A_0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
The magnitude \(A\) of the earthquake we wish to measure is 16,550 times more powerful, meaning that
\begin{equation*} A = 16550C\text{.} \end{equation*}
Substituting this into the Richter scale measurement for \(A\text{,}\) we are able to expand via the logarithm rules:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}R \amp = \log_{10}\left(\frac{A}{A_0}\right) \\ \amp =\log_{10}\left(\frac{16550C}{A_0}\right)\\ \amp = \log_{10}(16550)+\log_{10}C-\log_{10}A_0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
However we already know that \(\log_{10}C-\log_{10}A_0=3.7\text{,}\) so in measuring \(R\text{,}\) we need only add \(\log_{10}(16550)\) to the previous measurement of 3.7:
\begin{equation*} R = \log_{10}(16550)+3.7\approx 4.2+3.7 = 7.9\text{.} \end{equation*}
