Example 7.4.7. Formulas for the Maximum of a Surge Function.

For \(b>0\text{,}\) show that the maximum value of \(y=te^{-bt}\) occurs at \(t=1/b\) and increases as \(b\) decreases.
ðŸ”—
Solution.
The maximum must occur at a critical point where \(dy/dt=0\text{.}\) Differentiating gives
\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt} = 1\cdot e^{-bt}+t(-be^{-bt}) = e^{-bt}-bte^{-bt} = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(e^{-bt}\) is always positive, \(dy/dt=0\) only where
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}1-bt \amp = 0\\ t=\frac{1}{b}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
By the Single Critical Point Theorem (TheoremÂ 5.5.6), we only need to check that \(t=\frac{1}{b}\) is a local maximum in order to know that it is a global maximum, since it is the only critical point on the interval \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\) To do this, we observe that the sign of \(dy/dt = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\) depends only on the sign of \(1-bt\text{,}\) since \(e^{-bt}\) is positive for all values of \(t\text{.}\) When \(t\lt 1/b\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}t \amp \lt 1/b\\ bt \amp \lt 1\\ 0 \amp \lt 1-bt. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Similarly, when \(t>1/b\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}t \amp >1/b\\ bt \amp > 1\\ 0 \amp >1-bt. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Hence \(dy/dt = e^{-bt}(1-bt)\) is positive for \(t\lt 1/b\) and negative for \(t>1/b\text{,}\) thus \(t=1/b\) is the location of a local maximum, and by the Single Critical Point Theorem, also a global maximum.
ðŸ”—
The maximum \(y\)-value is found from substituting \(t=1/b\) into \(y=te^{-bt}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} y=\frac{1}{b}e^{-b(1/b)} = \frac{e^{-1}}{b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can see that for \(b>0\text{,}\) as \(b\) increases, the denominator of \(\frac{e^{-1}}{b}\) will increase, making the value of \(y\) decrease overall. Thus the maximum value of \(y\) decreases as \(b\) increases. Similarly, when \(b\) decreases, the maximum value of \(y\) increases.
