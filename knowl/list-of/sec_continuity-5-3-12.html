For example, since \(f(x)=x^2\) and \(g(x) = x^3\) are continuous at \(x=2\text{,}\) we know that \((f+g)(x) = f(x)+g(x) = x^2+x^3\) must also be continuous at \(x=2\text{.}\) This makes sense given TheoremÂ 3.2.33 since
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 2} (f+g)(x) = \lim_{x\to 2} (x^2+x^3) = \lim_{x\to 2} x^2+\lim_{x\to 2} x^3 = 2^2+2^3 = 4+8=12=(f+g)(2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
The key equality here is at either end: \(\ds\lim_{x\to 2} (f+g)(x) = (f+g)(2)\text{.}\) Remember, this is the definition of continuous from DefinitionÂ 3.2.5.
