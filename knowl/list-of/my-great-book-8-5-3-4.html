Example 6.4.2.

As another example, consider a savings account with an initial balance of $1000.00 that earns 3% interest each month. Let \(B(t)\) represent the balance of the savings account \(t\) months after the initial deposit. After one month, the amount in the savings account will be
\begin{equation*} B(1) = 1000+0.03(1000) = \dollar1030.00\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Since \(1000+0.03 = 1.03(1000)\text{,}\) we could also write this equation as:
\begin{equation*} B(1) = 1000(1.03) = \dollar1030.00\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
To find the balance after two months, we need to multiply \(B(1)\) by \(1.03\) (since the balance should grow by 3 percent more). The balance of the savings account after two months will be \(B(1)(1.03) = (1000(1.03))(1.03) = 1000(1.03)^2\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} B(2) = 100(1.03)^2 = \dollar1060.90\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Similarly, after three months the savings account balance will be
\begin{equation*} B(3) = 1000(1.03)^3 = \dollar1092.73\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Each month the balance in the savings account will be \(1.03\) times the amount in the savings account the previous month. The savings account balance \(B(t)\) after \(t\) months is the function \(B(t) = 1000(1.03)(1.03)\dots(1.03)(1.03)\text{,}\) with \(t\) copies of \(1.03\text{.}\) This is an exponential function:
\begin{equation*} B(t) = 1000(1.03)^t \end{equation*}
