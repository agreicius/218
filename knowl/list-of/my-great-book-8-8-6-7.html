Example 6.7.22.

Use logarithmic differentiation to find \(\frac{d}{dx}((x^2-3)^x)\text{.}\)

Solution.
We want to find the \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) for \(y=(x^2-3)^x\text{.}\) Lets start by taking the natural log of both sides:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\ln(y) \amp =\ln\left((x^2-3)^x\right)\\ \amp =x\ln(x^2-3). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Now, lets take the derivative of both sides:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{d}{dx}(\ln(y) )\amp =\frac{d}{dx}\left(x\ln(x^2-3)\right)\\ \frac{1}{y} \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)\amp = x\left(\frac{2x}{x^2-3}\right)+\ln(x^2-3) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
So, multiplying both sides by \(y\text{,}\) we obtain:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{dy}{dx}\amp =y\left( x\left(\frac{2x}{x^2-3}\right)+\ln(x^2-3)\right)\\ \amp =(x^2-3)^x\left( x\left(\frac{2x}{x^2-3}\right)+\ln(x^2-3)\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
