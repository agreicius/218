Where is profit maximized? We know that the maximum profit can occur when Marginal revenue = Marginal cost, that is where the curves above cross at \(q_1\) and \(q_2\text{.}\) Do these points give the maximum profit?
ðŸ”—
We first consider \(q_1\text{.}\) To the left of \(q_1\text{,}\) we have \(MR\lt MC\text{,}\) so \(\pi' = MR-MC\) is negative and the profit function is decreasing there. To the right of \(q_1\text{,}\) we have \(MR>MC\text{,}\) so \(\pi'\) is positive and the profit function is increasing. This behavior, decreasing and then increasing, means that the profit function has a local minimum at \(q_1\text{.}\) This is certainly not the production level we want.
ðŸ”—
What happens at \(q_2\text{?}\) To the left of \(q_2\text{,}\) we have \(MR>MC\text{,}\) so \(\pi'\) is positive and the profit function is increasing. To the right of \(q_2\text{,}\) we have \(MR\lt MC\text{,}\) so \(\pi'\) is negative and the profit function is decreasing. This behavior, increasing and then decreasing, means that the profit function has a local maximum at \(q_2\text{.}\) The global maximum profit occurs either at the production level \(q_2\) or at an endpoint (the largest and smallest possible production levels). Since the profit is negative at the endpoints (because \(R\lt C\) at the endpoints), the global maximum occurs at \(q_2\text{.}\) A graph of \(\pi(q)\) is below:
