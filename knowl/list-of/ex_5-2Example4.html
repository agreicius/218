Example 3.6.25.

Use limits to determine the horizontal asymptotes of
\begin{equation*} k(x) = \frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Again, note that
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}k(x) = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x} = \frac{\infty}{\infty} \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to-\infty}k(x) = \lim_{x\to-\infty} \frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x} = \frac{\infty}{-\infty} \end{equation*}
are both indeterminate. Once again, we work through both methods to provide you with more examples. This time, try to decide which method you prefer while reading, and plan to use that method in problems like this from now on.
ðŸ”—
Method 1: Divide the numerator and denominator by \(x^3\text{,}\) the highest power of \(x\) that appears in the denominator. This is equivalent to multiplying the numerator and denominator by \(1/x^3\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x} \amp = \lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x}\cdot\frac{1/x^3}{1/x^3}\\ \amp = \lim_{x\to \pm\infty}\frac{\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{x^3}}{1-\frac{4}{x^2}}\\ \amp = \frac{0-0}{1-0}\\ \amp =\frac{0}{1}\\ \amp =0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Method 2: Factor \(x^2\) from the numerator and factor \(x^3\) from the denominator. Notice in this case that not all of the \(x\)â€™s cancel.
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2-1}{x^3-4x} \amp = \lim_{x\to\pm\infty}\frac{x^2(1-\frac{1}{x^2})}{x^3(1-\frac{4}{x^2})}\\ \amp = \lim_{x\to \pm\infty}\frac{1-\frac{1}{x^2}}{x(1-\frac{4}{x^2})}\\ \amp = \frac{1-0}{\pm\infty(1-0)}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{\pm\infty}\\ \amp =0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
From both methods, we can see that there is a right and left horizontal asymptote of \(y=0\text{.}\)
