Example 6.3.8.

If we rearrange the statements of the facts in \(10^2 = 100\) and \(\log_{10}(100) = 2\text{,}\) we can see yet another important relationship between the powers of 10 and base-10 logarithm function. Noting that \(\log_{10}(100) = 2\) and \(100 = 10^2\) are equivalent statements, and substituting the latter equation into the former, we see that
\begin{equation*} \log_{10}(10^2) = 2\text{.} \end{equation*}

In a similar way, if we rearrange the statements in \(10^{-3} = \frac{1}{1000}\) and \(\log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{1000}\right) = -3\text{,}\) we can observe that by replacing \(-3\) with \(\log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{1000}\right)\) we have
\begin{equation*} 10^{\log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{1000}\right)} = \frac{1}{1000}\text{.} \end{equation*}
In words, this equation says that "when \(10\) is raised to the power to which we raise \(10\) in order to get \(\frac{1}{1000}\text{,}\) we get \(\frac{1}{1000}\text{.}\)"
