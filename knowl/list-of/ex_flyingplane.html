Example 9.1.6. Tracking a Plane.

A plane flies horizontally at an altitude of 5 km and passes directly over a tracking beacon on the ground. When the angle between the beaconâ€™s line of sight to the plane and the ground is \(\pi/3\text{,}\) this angle is decreasing at a rate of \(\pi/6\) rad/min. How fast is the plane traveling at that time?

Solution.

The problem asks us to find \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) when \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\) and \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\frac{\pi}{6}\text{,}\) given that the height of the triangle is 5. Again, we have no information about the hypotenuse so we would like to relate the two legs of the triangle directly. In order to see how to do this, notice that there is another triangle in the picture that also has height of 5 and that the planeâ€™s distance along the ground is the same as its distance in the air:
Thus we see that
\begin{equation*} \tan\theta = \frac{5}{x} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Solving for \(x\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} x = \frac{5}{\tan\theta} = 5\cot\theta \end{equation*}
and so taking the derivative to relate \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) to \(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = -5\csc^2\theta\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Finally we plug in the given quantities:
\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt}\Big|_{\substack{\theta=\pi/3\\ \frac{d\theta}{dt}=-\frac{\pi}{6}} } = -5\csc^2(\pi/3)\cdot\left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right) \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
In order to simplify \(\csc(\pi/3)\text{,}\) we first remember that \(\sin(\pi/3) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) and so \(\csc(\pi/3) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\text{,}\) hence \((\csc(\pi/3))^2 = \frac{4}{3}\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt}\Big|_{\substack{\theta=\pi/3\\ \frac{d\theta}{dt}} } = -5\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)\left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{10}{9}\pi\approx 3.49\text{.} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Hence the plane is flying at a velocity of about \(3.49\) km/min.
