Example 8.5.14.

Prove the following double-angle identity for tangent:
\begin{equation*} \tan2\theta=\frac{2\tan\theta}{1-\tan^2\theta} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Solution.
Since most of the identities we know at this point involve sines and cosines, it is a good idea to recast this identity in terms of those functions. We begin by simplifying the right side of the identity:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{2\tan\theta}{1-\tan^2\theta}\amp =\frac{2\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}}{1-\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right)^2}\\ \amp =\left(\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta}\right)\left(\frac{2\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}}{1-\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right)^2}\right)\\ \amp =\frac{\cos^2\theta\left(2\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right)}{\cos^2\theta\left(1-\left(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right)^2\right)}\\ \amp =\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Now we simplify the left side:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\tan2\theta\amp =\frac{\sin2\theta}{\cos2\theta}\\ \amp =\frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Combining these two equations, it follows that \(\tan2\theta=\frac{2\tan\theta}{1-\tan^2\theta}\text{.}\)
