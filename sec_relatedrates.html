    Subsection 5.2.3 Rates of Change that are Related

    In this section, we consider situations where several changing quantities are related, but where each quantity is implicitly a function of time, which will be represented by the variable \(t\text{.}\) If two quantities that change over time are related to each other, then their rates of change over time will also be related to each other. In particular, this is true even when one of the quantities is not an explicit function of the other, and so we can use the ideas developed from implicit differentiation in the previous section to study the derivatives of related quantities.
    ðŸ”—
    What do we mean when we say that two quantities are related? This simply means that there is some equation that they satisfy.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.2.1.

    For example, the area \(A\) and the radius \(r\) of a circle are related by the equation
    \begin{equation*} A=\pi r^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The circumference \(C\) and the radius of a circle are also related, and satisfy the equation
    \begin{equation*} C=2\pi r \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.2.2.

    The lengths \(a\) and \(b\) of the legs of a right triangle with hypotenuse of length 7 inches are also related to each other. In particular, they are related via the Pythagorean Theorem:
    \begin{equation*} a^2+b^2 = 7^2 \end{equation*}
    where since we are told the length of the hypotenuse, we include that information in the relationship.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Through knowing how quantities are related, we are interested in determining how their respective rates of change with respect to time are related.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.2.3. Ripples in a Pond.

    A stone dropped in a pond creates circular ripples that spread out from the center where the stone was dropped. If the radius of a particular circle is increasing at a constant rate of half a foot per second, we can ask how fast the area inside the circle is increasing at the moment the radius is 3 feet.
    ðŸ”—
    Meet the Problem: Having read the problem, we begin by drawing a diagram of a circle with radius \(r\text{,}\) including an arrow indicating that the radius is increasing.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The problem involves the area \(A\) of the circle as well. The quantities \(A\) and \(r\) are not just variables, but are also functions \(A(t)\) and \(r(t)\) of time since the problem suggests that they are changing. We are told that when \(r=3\text{,}\) the radius is changing at a positive rate of 0.5 ft/sec. We can write this mathematically as \(\frac{dr}{dt}=0.5\text{.}\) Note that the derivative is respect to \(t\) because the rate describes how \(r\) changes over time. We are asked to find how fast the area is changing, again inferring that we are measuring over time, meaning we wish to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Make a Plan: We know that \(A\) and \(r\) are related by \(A=\pi r^2\text{.}\) If we take the derivative with respect to \(t\) (NOT \(r\text{;}\) see emphasis on using \(t\) as the independent variable in the previous paragraph), then we will know how \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\) are related. We expect to need to plug in the information that we are given and then arrive at a number for \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Carry out Your Plan:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{d}{dt}[A] \amp = \frac{d}{dt}[\pi r^2]\\ \frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \pi \left(2r\,\frac{dr}{dt}\right)\\ \frac{dA}{dt} \amp = 2\pi r\, \frac{dr}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Note that we have used that \(A\) and \(r\) are implicit functions of \(t\text{,}\) and so have needed to use the chain rule when taking the derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}[r^2] = 2r\,\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    At this point, we have "related the rates" of changes of the area and the radius, \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt}\text{.}\) Then if we remember that we are given \(r=3\) and \(\frac{dr}{dt} = 0.5\text{,}\) we can see that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt} \Big|_{r=3}= 2\pi (3)(0.5) = 3\pi\approx 9.42\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Reflect Back: We have successfully found a value for the quantity \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) when \(r=3\text{,}\) as asked for in the problem. Our units are accurate since \(A\) is measured in square feet and \(t\) is measured in seconds. The positive answer makes sense since the area should be increasing as the radius increases. The area is additionally increasing at a larger rate than the radius, which also makes sense since the area of a circle of radius \(r=3\) is proportional to the square of the radius, so should increase more.
    ðŸ”—
    Critique and Change the Problem: Here is an observation that goes beyond what was asked in the problem: Notice that even though the radius of the circle is changing at a constant rate, the area of the circle will not be changing at a constant rate. As the radius gets larger, the change in the area will increase. When the radius is \(r=5\text{,}\) the change in the area will be
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=5} = 2\pi(5)(0.5) = 5\pi\approx 15.7\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec }\text{.} \end{equation*}
    When the radius is \(r=10\text{,}\) the change in the area will be
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=10} = 2\pi(10)(0.5) = 10\pi\approx 31.4\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec }\text{.} \end{equation*}
    When the radius of is \(r=20\) feet, the change in the area will be
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{r=20} = 2\pi(20)(0.5) = 20\pi\approx 62.8\text{ ft } ^2\text{ /sec } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

These rates will be related in some way that is determined by the way the corresponding quantities are related. Translating a related-rates problem from words to equations should result in an equation that relates the two quantities whose derivatives you either know or want to find. You can then implicitly differentiate the equation relating the quantities to get an equation relating the derivatives, as we did in the previous example.

    Example 5.2.6.

    The circumference of a circular shadow is increasing at a rate of 3 inches per minute. At what rate is the area of the shadow changing when the circumference is 16 inches?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Meet the Problem: The quantities involved in the problem are the circumference, \(C\text{,}\) and area, \(A\text{,}\) of a circle.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    We are told that the circumference is increasing at a rate of 3 inches per minute, so if we let \(t\) represent time in minutes, we can write \(\frac{dC}{dt} =3\text{.}\) We are asked to find the rate at which the area of the shadow is changing, so we would like to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\) Specifically, we want to find \(\frac{dA}{dt}\) when \(C=16\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Make a Plan: It is not entirely obvious how to get an equation involving the circumference \(C\) and area \(A\) of a circle. Since the area and radius are related, and the radius and circumference are related, we may reasonably expect the area and the circumference to be related. That means we should be able to find an equation containing both \(A\) and \(C\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The area and radius are related via
    \begin{equation*} A=\pi r^2 \end{equation*}
    while the circumference and radius are related via
    \begin{equation*} C=2\pi r\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can find an equation involving only \(A\) and \(C\) by solving one equation for \(r\) and substituting it into the other equation, eliminating the variable \(r\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Once we have that equation, we take its derivative and plug in the information that we are given: \(C=16\) and \(\frac{dC}{dt}=3\text{,}\) then solve for \(\frac{dA}{dt}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Carry out Your Plan: The equation \(C=2\pi r\) is easier to solve for \(r\text{,}\) so we solve for \(r\) in terms of \(C\) and substitute into \(A=\pi r^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}C \amp =2 \pi r\\ \frac{C}{2\pi} \amp = r \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and so
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}A \amp = \pi r^2\\ A \amp =\pi \left(\frac{C}{2\pi}\right)^2\\ A \amp = \frac{C^2}{4\pi} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    where we have simplified and cancelled one of the \(\pi\)â€™s in the last step.
    ðŸ”—
    So we see that, yes indeed, the area and circumference of a circle are related, specifically by the equation
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{C^2}{4\pi} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We take the derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}\) of both sides. Note that we do not actually need to use the quotient rule because \(4\pi\) is a constant, so we rewrite the equation first to make this more obvious:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \frac{d}{dt}\left[\frac{1}{4\pi} \cdot C^2\right]\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4\pi}\cdot 2C\, \frac{dC}{dt}\\ \amp = \frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Plugging in \(C=16\) and \(\frac{dC}{dt}=3\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}\Big|_{C=16} = \frac{16}{2\pi}\cdot 3 = \frac{24}{\pi}\approx 7.6394 \text{ ft } ^2/\text{ min } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Reflect Back: We can check that our answer makes sense in context. In particular, the area is increasing since the derivative is positive, which it should since the circle is getting larger. The units are correct since area is measured in ft\(^2\) while time is measured in feet. We can even notice that the area is increasing faster than the circumference, which makes sense since area is proportional to the square of the circumference in the original equation we had.
    ðŸ”—
    Critique and Change the Problem: Here we could think about ways to change the problem. For instance, what if we were given the rate of change of the area and were asked to find the rate of change of the circumference? Our work would be mostly the same, except for the final step when we plug in and solve. If we are additionally given the value of the area instead of the circumference, we would have to do a bit more work.
    ðŸ”—
    For example: what if we are told that the area is decreasing by 2 ft\(^2\)/min and are asked to find the rate at which the circumference decreases when the area is 9 ft\(^2\text{?}\) Then we can plug in \(\frac{dA}{dt}=-2\) but we would still need a value of \(C\) in order to solve for \(\frac{dC}{dt}\) in
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dA}{dt}=\frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since we are told that \(A=9\text{,}\) we can use the original equation \(A=\frac{C^2}{4\pi}\) to find the value of \(C\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}9 \amp =\frac{C^2}{4\pi}\\ 36\pi \amp = C^2\\ \pm\sqrt{36\pi} \amp = C\\ \pm6\sqrt{\pi} \amp = C \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Circumference is a length and should be positive, so \(C = 6\sqrt{\pi}\approx 10.6347\text{.}\) Now we can plug in and solve for \(\frac{dC}{dt}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{dA}{dt} \amp = \frac{C}{2\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -2 \amp = \frac{6\sqrt{\pi}}{4\pi}\,\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -2\cdot \frac{4\pi}{6\sqrt{\pi}} \amp =\frac{dC}{dt}\\ -\frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{\pi}} \amp =\frac{dC}{dt} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    And so the circumference is decreasing by \(\frac{4\pi}{3\sqrt{\pi}}\approx 2.3633\) ft/min.

    \begin{equation*} \frac{dr}{dt}\Big|_{r=4} = \frac{44}{4\pi (4)^2} \approx 0.2188 \text{ inches per second } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Part (b): We wish to find \(\frac{dr}{dt}\) at the "instant that the balloon contains 100 cubic inches of air," meaning when \(V=100\text{.}\) This we cannot just plug in, since we need to know the value of \(r\) for this value of \(V\text{.}\) Hence we use the original equation \(V=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3\) to find the corresponding \(r\text{:}\)

    Subsection 5.2.6 Summary

    Key ideas: If two quantities are related, then their rates of change are also related. We can take the derivative of an equation relating the quantities in order to find the relationship between the rates of change. Such "related rates problems" have a predictable format that you should learn to recognize in order to not get lost while solving these types of problems.
    ðŸ”—
    Other ideas reinforced: A derivative is a rate of change. Units are extremely useful for identifying the meaning of a given number in the context of a word problem. Word problem should be read at least three times while completing the problem, twice at the beginning and once again at the end.
