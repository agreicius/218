    Theorem 8.3.4. Domain, Range, and Period of the Six Trigonometric Functions.

    The six trigonometric functions \(\sin t, \cos t, \tan t, \csc t, \sec t\text{,}\) and \(\cot t\) have the following domain, range, and period:
    • The domain of both \(\sin t\) and \(\cos t\) is the set of all real numbers and their range is \([-1,1]\text{.}\) They are periodic with period \(2\pi\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The domain of \(\tan t\) is the set of all real numbers \(t\) except for odd multiples of \(\pi/2\text{:}\) \(t\neq \ldots -\frac{3\pi}{2}, -\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2},\ldots\) and its range is \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\) It is periodic with period \(\pi\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

We have already explored how \(\sin t\) and \(\cos t\) are defined for all values of \(t\text{,}\) take on values between \(-1\) and \(1\) inclusive, and are periodic with period \(2\pi\text{.}\) These all come from their definition as the vertical/horizontal value of a point moving around the unit circle.

    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{(2k+1)\pi}{2}\text{ where } k\text{ is a positive or negative whole number. } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    At those points, \(\tan t\) has vertical asymptotes since \(\sin t = \pm1\) where \(\cos t = 0\text{,}\) thus \(\ds\lim_{t\to (2k+1)\pi/2}\tan t\) has the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{.}\) In particular, when \(t\to\pi/2\text{,}\) \(\sin t\to 1\) and \(\cos t\to 0\) from both above (when \(t\to 0^-\)) and below (when \(t\to 0^+\)), so we see that the asymptotes go to both \(\infty\) and \(-\infty\text{,}\) so \(\tan t\) has range \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\) We cannot yet prove rigorously that \(\tan t\) has period \(\pi\text{,}\) rather than \(2\pi\text{,}\) but it is clear to see from the graph, and is due to the fact that the sign of \(\frac{\sin t}{\cos t}\) is positive for \(t\) in the first quadrant, negative for \(t\) in the second quadrant, then positive again for \(t\) in the third quadrant, and negative for \(t\) in the fourth quadrant. (The period of \(\tan t\) will be the last thing we examine in this section.)
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s take a closer look at the graph of \(\csc t = \frac{1}{\sin t}\text{.}\) It is undefined at all values of \(t\) where \(\sin t=0\text{.}\) These are the angles corresponding to the horizontal axis of the unit circle, which are the multiples of \(\pi\text{,}\) and can be written
    \begin{equation*} t = k\pi\text{ where } k\text{ is a positive or negative whole number. } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    At those points, \(\csc t\) has vertical asymptotes since \(\ds\lim_{t\to k\pi}\csc t\) has the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{,}\) and we can see that for each such \(t\text{,}\) \(\sin t\) approaches \(0^+\) on one side and \(0^-\) on the other. The range of \(\csc t\) is not all real numbers, however, since the values of \(\csc t\) cannot be between \(-1\) and \(1\text{.}\) This is because \(\sin t\leq1\) and \(\sin t\geq-1\text{,}\) so either \(\csc t = \frac{1}{\sin t}\geq1\) or \(\csc t = \frac{1}{\sin t}\leq-1\text{.}\) The period of \(\csc t\) is \(2\pi\) because the period of \(\sin t\) is \(2\pi\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 8.3.3 Solving Equations Involving Trigonometric Functions

    Now that we know what the trigonometric functions look like, have a table of values that we could memorize (or look up), and have constant access to a calculator for all of the other values, it might be tempting to forget all about the unit circle definitions of sine and cosine. However, the unit circle provides a crucial perspective that allows us to solve equations involving trigonometric functions, and also derive further useful properties of trigonometric functions.
    ðŸ”—
    In the next example, we demonstrate how to use the unit circle to solve fairly basic equations involving sine and cosine.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 8.3.6.

    Find all of the solutions of the equation \(\sin\theta=\cos\theta\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    If \(\sin\theta=\cos\theta\text{,}\) then \(\theta\) is an angle whose terminal edge intersects the unit circle at a point \((x,y)\) with \(x=y\text{,}\) since the coordinates \((x,y)\) on the unit circle correspond to the points \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\text{.}\) The only such points on the unit circle are \(\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\) and \(\left(-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\text{,}\) as shown in FigureÂ 8.3.7 below.
    ðŸ”—
    The angles that end at these points are all of the form \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}+\pi k\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) Thus, the solution set for the equation is \(\left\{\dots,-\frac{3\pi}{4},\frac{\pi}{4},\frac{5\pi}{4},\frac{9\pi}{4},\dots\right\}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Below is another application of our understanding of the unit-circle origins of sine and cosine. We can often find the value of one function if we are given the value of another function, without having to know the precise angle involved.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 8.3.8.

    Given that \(\cos\theta=-\frac{1}{3}\) and \(\theta\) is an angle that terminates in the third quadrant, find \(\tan\theta\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since \(\cos\theta=-\frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) the angle \(\theta\) must intersect the unit circle at a point whose \(x\)-coordinate is \(-\frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) as shown next. Only one of these points is in the third quadrant, and it is of the form \(\left(-\frac{1}{3},y\right)\) where \(y\) is some negative number.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    To find the tangent of \(\theta\text{,}\) we need to know the \(y\)-coordinate of the point we have identified in the third quadrant. Because the radius of the circle is 1 and is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with base \(x=\frac{1}{3}\) and height \(y\text{,}\) by the Pythagorean Theorem, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}y^2+\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 \amp =1 \\ y^2+\frac{1}{9}\amp =1\\ y \amp =\pm\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{9}}\\ \amp =\pm\sqrt{\frac{8}{9}}\\ \amp =\pm\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Since \(\theta\) terminates in the third quadrant, the \(y\)-coordinate corresponding to \(\theta\) is the negative choice:
    \begin{equation*} y= -\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    As a reality check, \(-\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\approx -0.9428\) is a reasonable answer, since the point in question has a \(y\) coordinate very close to \(-1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We can now say that
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}=\frac{-\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}}{-\frac{1}{3}}=\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{1}=2\sqrt{2} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    It is interesting to note that we never actually had to determine the angle measure of \(\theta\text{,}\) we only needed to know the \((x,y)\) coordinates of the corresponding point on the unit circle.

    Subsection 8.3.5 A Few Trigonometric Identities

    Another reason to remember the unit-circle definitions of sine and cosine is in establishing the many identities that these and the other trigonometric functions satisfy. An identity is an equation that is always true.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 8.3.12.

    For example, the equation \(x^2=4\) is not an identity because it is true only for the values \(x=2\) and \(x=-2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    By contrast, the equation \(3^{2x}=9^x\) is true for all values of \(x\) and thus is an identity.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Because trigonometric functions are closely related to each other, there are a LOT of trigonometric identities that describe relationships between two seemingly different trigonometric expressions that are in fact equal for all relevant values of \(x\text{.}\) These identities will be the key to rewriting and simplifying trigonometric expressions, much like the logarithms rules of previous chapters.
    â€‰2â€‰
    Please try not to forget them as they will be on the final exam.
    ðŸ”—
    For instance, the sine and cosine of an angle \(\theta\) are the \(y\)-coordinate and \(x\)-coordinates, respectively, of the point where the terminal edge of \(\theta\) meets the unit circle. Since the unit circle has equation \(x^2+y^2 = 1\text{,}\) the quantities \(x=\cos \theta\) and \(y=\sin \theta\) are related by the trigonometric identity
    \begin{equation*} (\cos \theta)^2+(\sin \theta)^2 = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    This equation is known as a Pythagorean Identity, since, for acute angles, it follows from the Pythagorean Theorem as follows: If \(\theta\) is an angle in a right triangle with hypotenuse \(1\text{,}\) then \(\sin \theta = \frac{\text{ opp } }{1}\) is the length of the side opposite \(\theta\) and \(\cos\theta = \frac{\text{ adj } }{1}\) is the length of the side adjacent to \(\theta\text{.}\) By the Pythagorean Theorem, the sum of the squares of those lengths must be equal to the square of the hypotenuse, again giving us the identity just listed. The following Figure illustrates these two ways of obtaining that identity:
    ðŸ”—
    For notational convenience, people often use the abbreviation
    \begin{equation*} \sin^2\theta\text{ or } \cos^2\theta \end{equation*}
    to represent \((\sin \theta)^2\) or \((\cos\theta)^2\text{,}\) respectively. We will use exponents similarly for the other trigonometric functions and for higher integer powers.
    ðŸ”—
    Thus the Pythagorean Identity is typically written
    \begin{equation*} \cos^2 \theta+\sin^2 \theta = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 8.3.14.

    The notation \(\sin^2\theta\text{ or } \cos^2\theta\) should not be taken to mean \(\sin(\sin(\theta))\) or \(\cos(\cos(\theta))\text{.}\) This is confusing and I apologize on behalf of the mathematical community.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    From the preceding discussion, we get the following three trigonometric identities:
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 8.3.15. Pythagorean Identities.

    For every angle \(\theta\) for which the expressions that follow are defined,
    1. \(\displaystyle \sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta = 1\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. \(\displaystyle \tan^2\theta+1 = \sec^2\theta\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    3. \(\displaystyle 1+\cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    We have already proved (a). To prove (b), we simply divide both sides of (a) by \(\cos^2\theta\) and then simplify:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\sin^2\theta +\cos^2\theta \amp = 1\\ \frac{\sin^2\theta +\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} \amp = \frac{1}{\cos^2\theta}\\ \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} +\frac{\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} \amp = \frac{1^2}{\cos^2\theta}\\ \left(\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}\right)^2+1 \amp = \left(\frac{1}{\cos\theta}\right)^2\\ \tan^2\theta +1 \amp =\sec^2\theta \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
