    Subsection 5.6.2 Inflection Points: Where Concavity Changes

    We begin by reviewing the type of information we can determine using the first and second derivatives, and see two more characterizations of concavity.
    ðŸ”—
    Recall how the sign of the first derivative (TheoremÂ 5.4.1) and the definition of concavity (DefinitionÂ 5.4.15) give us information about the shape of a function:
    ðŸ”—
    Derivative Sign The function \(f(x)\)
    First derivative \(f'\) is positive \(f\) is increasing
    First derivative \(f'\) is negative \(f\) is decreasing
    Second derivative \(f''\) is positive \(f\) is concave up
    Second derivative \(f''\) is negative \(f\) is concave down
    From the First Derivative Test (TheoremÂ 5.4.3), a change in the sign of the first derivative at a critical point indicates a local extrema, a.k.a. a local maximum or minimum (for a function \(f(x)\) that is continuous and differentiable near \(x=c\)):
    ðŸ”—
    Left of \(x=c\) Right of \(x=c\) The function \(f(x)\) has
    \(f'\) positive \(f'\) negative a local maximum at \(x=c\)
    \(f'\) negative \(f'\) positive a local minimum at \(x=c\)
    \(f'\) positive \(f'\) positive neither a min nor max at \(x=c\)
    \(f'\) negative \(f'\) negative neither a min nor max at \(x=c\)
    There is also a special name for the points on the curve at which the second derivative changes sign: inflection points
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 5.6.1.

    If \(x=c\) is a value in the domain of a continuous function \(f(x)\) at which \(f(x)\) changes concavity, then we say that \((c,f(c))\) is an inflection point or point of inflection of \(f\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Just as we look for locations where \(f\) changes from increasing to decreasing at points where \(f'(c)=0\) or \(f'(c)\) is undefined, so too we find where \(f''(c)=0\) or \(f''(c)\) is undefined to see if there are points of inflection at these locations.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.6.2.

    For example, consider the function \(f(x) = x^3\text{.}\) Calculating the first two derivatives,
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = 3x^2 \text{ and } f''(x) = 6x\text{.} \end{equation*}
    \(f''(x)=6x=0\) only when \(x=0\) and is defined for all \(x\)-values, hence \(f''(x)\) can only change sign at \(x=0\text{.}\) Completing a sign analysis for \(f''(x) = 6x\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    we see the sign of \(f''\) changes from negative to positive at \(x=0\text{,}\) and therefore the concavity of \(f\) changes from down to up at \(x=0\text{.}\) Hence \(x=0\) is an inflection point of \(f(x)\text{.}\) (It is also correct to say that \(x=0\) is a critical point for \(f'(x)\) since the derivative of \(f'(x)\) is equal to zero at \(x=0\text{.}\))
    ðŸ”—
    We can see this on the graph of \(f(x)=x^3\) below, where the function changes from concave down to concave up at the point \((0,0)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Here is another interesting characterization of concavity: when a function is concave up, the tangent lines are below the curve, while when a function is concave down, the tangent lines are above the curve. At an inflection point, the tangent line will pass through the curve, and will be above on one side of the point of inflection and below on the other side of the point of inflection. This sometimes helps people see inflection points more easily on the graph of a function.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.6.4.

    For example, we can see on the graph of \(f(x)=x^3\) below that at \(x=0\text{,}\) the tangent line to the graph passes through the curve. To the left of \(x=0\text{,}\) the tangent lines are above the curve, while to the right of \(x=0\text{,}\) the tangent lines are below the curve:
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    If \(x=c\) is an inflection point of \(f\) and \(f''(c)=0\text{,}\) then the graph of \(f\) could look one of the following four ways near \(x=c\text{,}\) depending on how \(f\) changes concavity and whether \(f\) is increasing or decreasing:
    ðŸ”—
    Here is one last interpretation of inflection points in terms of the shape of the graph of \(f'(x)\text{.}\) We know that at an inflection point of a function \(f\text{,}\) the function changes concavity and the second derivative \(f''\) changes sign. What happens to the first derivative \(f'\text{?}\) The answer lies in the fact that the second derivative \(f''\) is the derivative of the first derivative \(f'\text{.}\) Thus if we know that if \(f''\) is positive, then \(f'\) is increasing, and if \(f''\) is negative then \(f'\) is decreasing. (Recall DefinitionÂ 5.4.15.) Hence when the concavity of \(f(x)\) changes and \(f''(x)\) changes sign, the first derivative \(f'(x)\) has either a local minimum or maximum.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.6.7.

    Below is the graph of \(f(x)=x^3\) in blue, \(f'(x)=3x^2\) in red, and \(f''(x)=6x\) in green. We can see that when \(f''(x) = 6x\) changes sign from negative to positive, not only does \(f(x) = x^3\) change concavity, but also \(f'(x) = 3x^2\) has a local minimum.

    Subsection 5.6.5 Summary

    The following terms were introduced in this section:
    ðŸ”—
    Key ideas: Inflection points are where the concavity of a graph changes. They are also where the first derivative has a local maximum or minimum. We can use first and second derivative information, along with the four shapes of nonlinear functions, to play "connect-the-dots" between intercepts, local minima and maxima, and inflection points, on a graph.
    ðŸ”—
    Other ideas reinforced/introduced: If we are not given an explicit function, we cannot determine precise \(y\)-values for points on the graph of the function. However, we can still sketch a remarkably accurate graph of a function based only on the first, and maybe also second, derivatives.
