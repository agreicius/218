    Section 9.3 L’Hôpital’s Rule with Trigonometric Functions

    Subsection 9.3.1 Learning Goals

    • Practice using L’Hôpital’s Rule with trigonometric functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Practice connecting limit computations with the visual behavior of a function on a graph.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 9.3.2 Examples of applying L’Hôpital’s Rule with Trigonometric Functions

    Having now formally defined the derivatives of the sine and cosine functions via limits,
     1 
    The derivatives of tangent, secant, cosecant, and cotangent were all derived from sine and cosine using standard derivative rules (quotient and chain rules) so we do not have to go back and recalculate those with limits, thank goodness!
    we can use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate limits of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) and \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) for functions involving trigonometric functions. Let’s start with two limits calculated via the Squeeze Theorem in the last section:
    🔗

    Example 9.3.1.

    Since \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = \frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) we may use L’Hôpital’s rule immediately:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cos x}{1}\\ \amp = \frac{\cos 0}{1}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{1}\\ \amp = 1 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 9.3.2.

    Similarly, \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x} = \frac{0}{0}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x} \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{1}\\ \amp = \frac{\sin 0}{1}\\ \amp = \frac{0}{1}\\ \amp = 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 9.3.3.

    Use L’Hôpital’s rule to calculate limits below:
    1. \(\displaystyle \ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin 3x}{2x}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{3\cos(2x)-3}{5x}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Of course, we can use L’Hôpital’s rule with any trigonometric functions, not just sine and cosine, provided that the limit has the form of either \(\frac{0}{0}\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{:}\)
    🔗

    Example 9.3.4.

    Determine whether \(f(x) = \frac{2x}{\tan^2x}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x=0\text{,}\) and if so, the precise behavior of the function to the right and left of the vertical asymptote.
    🔗
    Solution.
    In calculating \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2x}{\tan^2x}\text{,}\) we observe that \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}2x = 0\) and \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\tan^2x = (\tan 0)^2 = 0^2=0\text{,}\) so the limit is indeed of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2x}{\tan^2x} \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2}{2\tan x\sec^2 x}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{2(0)(1)^2}\\ \amp = \frac{2}{0} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We know that this limit is infinite, but we need to examine both sides of the limit to see if the approach \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\text{.}\) Before doing this, let’s cancel the 2’s and rewrite the limit in terms of sines and cosines. This will allow us to use our knowledge of the unit circle and/or the graphs of sine and cosine more easily:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{2\tan x\sec^2 x} = \frac{1}{\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}\left(\frac{1}{\cos x}\right)^2} = \frac{\cos^3x}{\sin x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \(\cos x>0\) for \(x\approx 0\) so it is the \(\sin x\) in the denominator that will determine whether the limit goes to \(\infty\) or \(\infty\text{.}\) As it happens, \(\sin x>0\) for \(x\) a little larger than 0 and \(\sin x\lt 0\) for \(x\) a little smaller than 0 (graph \(y=\sin x\) if you don’t believe me, or take a look at the unit circle near an angle of 0 radians). Thus
    \begin{equation*} \ds\lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\cos^3 x}{\sin x} = \frac{1}{0^+} = \infty \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \ds\lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{\cos^3x}{\sin x} = \frac{1}{0^-} = -\infty \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Overall, \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2x}{\tan^2x}\) DNE, but we know that \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{2x}{\tan^2x} = \infty\) while \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{2x}{\tan^2x} = -\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus the function \(f(x) = \frac{2x}{\tan^2x}\) has a vertical asymptote to \(\infty\) to the right of \(x=0\text{,}\) and a vertical asymptote to \(-\infty\) to the left of \(x=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 9.3.5.

    We calculated \(\ds\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 0\) in Example 8.6.13 of Section 8.6, so we know the calculation below is wrong. Identify which steps are incorrect and explain why they do not follow from the previous step.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\sin x}{x} \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{\cos x}{1}\\ \amp = \lim_{x\to\infty}\cos x\\ \amp = \text{ DNE since cosine oscillates between } -1\text{ and } 1 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 9.3.6.

    Calculate each of the following limits:
    1. \(\displaystyle \ds\lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\csc x}{\ln x}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \ds\lim_{x\to \pi/2}(\tan x-\sec x)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. As \(x\to 0^+\text{,}\) \(\csc x\) approaches \(\infty\) and \(\ln x\) approaches \(-\infty\text{;}\) therefore this is a limit of the form \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\text{,}\) which is indeterminate. L’Hôpital’s rule applied and gives us
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{\csc x}{\ln x} \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{-\csc x\cot x}{1/x} = \lim_{x\to 0^+}(-x\csc x\cot x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      As \(x\to 0^+\) we have \(-x\to 0\text{,}\) \(\csc x\to \infty\text{,}\) and \(\cot x\to \infty\text{,}\) and therefore this limit is of the indeterminate form \(0\cdot \infty\text{.}\) After converting to sines and cosines and simplifying, we will be able to apply L’Hôpital’s rule again:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to 0^+}(-x\csc x\cot x) \amp = \lim_{x\to 0^+} (-x)\left(\frac{1}{\sin x}\right)\left(\frac{\cos x}{\sin x}\right)\amp \leftarrow\text{ trigonometric identities } \\ \amp = \lim_{x\to 0^+} \frac{-x\cos x}{\sin^2 x}\amp \leftarrow\text{ algebra, limit now of form } \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{(-1)\cos x+(-x)(-\sin x)}{2\sin x\cos x}\amp \leftarrow\text{ note use of product and chain rules } \\ \amp = \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{-\cos x+x\sin x}{2\sin x\cos x}\amp \leftarrow\text{ simplify } \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      Now as \(x\to 0^+\) we have \(\sin x\to 0^+\) and \(\cos x\to 1\text{,}\) and therefore the denominator in the remaining expression approaches \(0^+\text{.}\) Since the numerator approaches \(-\cos 0+0(\sin 0) = -1\text{,}\) the limit in question is of the form \(\frac{-1}{0^+}\) and therefore
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\csc x}{\ln x} = \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{-\cos x+x\sin x}{2\sin x\cos x} = -\infty \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. At first glance this does not look like a L’Hôpital’s rule problem, since \(\tan x-\sec x\) is not in the form of a quotient. Worse, the limit is of the indeterminate form \(\infty-\infty\text{,}\) so we can’t solve it simply by evaluating the function at \(x=\frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) After using a bit of algebra, we see that L’Hôpital’s rule does apply here:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\lim_{x\to \pi/2} (\tan x-\sec x) \amp = \lim_{x\to \pi/2}\left(\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}-\frac{1}{\cos x}\right)\amp \leftarrow\text{ definition of tangent and secant } \\ \amp = \lim_{x\to \pi/2}\frac{\sin x-1}{\cos x}\amp \leftarrow\text{ this is now in the form } \frac{0}{0}\\ \amp \stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to \pi/2}\frac{\cos x}{-\sin x}\amp \leftarrow\text{ L'H\^opital's rule } \\ \amp =\frac{\cos(\pi/2)}{-\sin(\pi/2)} = \frac{0}{-1} = 0 \amp \leftarrow\text{ evaluate limit } \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      🔗

    Example 9.3.9.

    Calculate the limit \(\ds\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin^{-1}x}{x^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    As \(x\to 0\text{,}\) the numerator \(\sin^{-1}x\) and the denominator \(x^2\) both approach zero. Therefore this limit is initially in the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\text{,}\) and L’Hôpital’s rule applies. This gives us
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin^{-1}x}{x^2}\stackrel[L'H]{}= \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1/\sqrt{1-x^2}}{2x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1}{2x\sqrt{1-x^2}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(2x\sqrt{1-x^2}\) approaches 0 as \(x\to 0\text{,}\) the preceding limit is of the form \(\frac{1}{0}\text{.}\) From the left, as \(x\to 0^-\text{,}\) the denominator approaches \(0^-\text{,}\) and thus the limit from the left is of the form \(\frac{1}{0^-}\text{.}\) Similarly, from the right, as \(x\to 0^+\text{,}\) the limit is of the form \(\frac{1}{0^+}\text{.}\) Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{\sin^{-1}x}{x^2} = \lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{1}{2x\sqrt{1-x^2}} = -\infty \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{\sin^{-1}x}{x^2} = \lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{1}{2x\sqrt{1-x^2}} = \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus the left and right limits as \(x\to 0\) are not equal to each other, and the limit as \(x\to 0\) does not exist.

    Subsection 9.3.3 Summary

    Key ideas: We can use L’Hôpital’s rule for limits of the form \(\frac{0}{0}\) and \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) that involve trigonometric and inverse trigonometric functions, now that we know how to compute the derivatives of these types of functions.
    🔗
    Other ideas reinforced: It is often useful to rewrite tangent, secant, cosecant, and cotangent in terms of sine and cosine in order to compute with them. Limits computed with L’Hôpital’s rule often require an algebraic simplification after applying L’Hôpital’s rule. Limits at finite values of \(x\) that approach positive or negative infinity demonstrate that the function has a vertical asymptote at that \(x\)-value. Limits of the form \(\frac{1}{0}\) are infinite (so are NOT indeterminate) but often need to be computed separately from the right and left in order to determine whether they approach \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\text{.}\)
