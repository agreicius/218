    Subsection Definition of Average Rate of Change of a Function

    In the previous section, we looked at linear and quadratic functions in some depth. We now know that each type of function has a predictable shape. In particular, linear function have a constant slope, while quadratic functions do not. Another way to say this is that linear functions change at a constant rate. In this section, we learn one way to describe how functions change when they do not increase or decrease at a constant rate.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.3.1.

    TableÂ 1.3.2 below shows the height of the winning pole vault at the Olympics
    â€‰10â€‰
    The World Almanac and Book of Facts, 2005,p. 866 (New York). As found in Applied Calculus by Hughes Hallet et al.
    during the 1960s and more recently. Find the rate of change of the winning height between 1960 and 1968, and between 2008 and 2016. In which of these two periods did the height increase faster?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    To compare the 1960s and the more recent period, we calculate the average change over each time period, similar to how we calculate the slope of a line as \(\frac{\text{ rise } }{\text{ run } }\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \substack{\text{{Average rate of change of height}} \\ \text{{1960 to 1968}} } = \frac{\text{ Change in height } }{\text{ Change in time } } = \frac{540-470}{1968-1960} = 8.75 \text{ cm/year } \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \substack{\text{{Average rate of change of height}} \\ \text{{2008 to 2016}} } = \frac{\text{ Change in height } }{\text{ Change in time } } = \frac{603-596}{2016-2008} = 0.88 \text{ cm/year } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, on average, the height was increasing more quickly during the 1960s.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The data in TableÂ 1.3.2 does not have a constant rate of change (it is not linear). However, we can compute an average rate of change over any interval. The word average is used because the rate of change may vary within the interval.
    ðŸ”—
    In general, given a function that models a certain phenomenon, itâ€™s natural to ask such questions as "how is the function changing on a given interval" or "on which interval is the function changing more rapidly?" The concept of average rate of change enables us to make these questions more mathematically precise.
    ðŸ”—
    We use the symbol \(\Delta\) (the Greek letter capital delta) to mean "change in," so \(\Delta x\) means "change in \(x\) and \(\Delta y\) means "change in y." The average rate of change of a dependent quantity is then expressed as a fraction of the change in the dependent variable divided by the amount of change in the independent variable:
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.3.3. Average Rate of Change.

    If \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{,}\) so \(y=f(x)\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \substack{\text{{\textbf{Average rate of change} of \(y\)}} \\\text{{between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\)}} } = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The units of average rate of change of a function are units of \(y\) per units of \(x\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We will often abbreviate the average rate of change on the interval \([a,b]\) as
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[a,b]} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The quantity \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\) is called a difference quotient because it is the quotient of two differences. This formula may look familiar to you; it resembles the formula we use in SectionÂ 1.2 to compute the slope of the line between two points. As we will explore later, the average rate of change of a function \(f\) on an interval \([a,b]\) can be visualized as the slope of the line from \((a,f(a))\) to \((b,f(b))\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.3.4.

    For example, the function graphed below in FigureÂ 1.3.5 is increasing until \(x=-2\text{,}\) decreasing from \(x=-2\) to \(x=1\text{,}\) increasing again from \(x=1\) until \(x=3\text{,}\) and then decreasing after \(x=3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The function is not linear and so has no "slope." In order to discuss how much it increases or decreases, we can look at each interval separately.
    ðŸ”—
    On the interval \([-2,1]\text{,}\) we calculate the average rate of change of \(y\) between \(x=-2\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) First, we observe that \(f(-2)=4\) and \(f(1)=-2\) in order to use these quantities in the formula for Average Rate of Change with \(a=-2\) and \(b=1\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[-2,1]} = \frac{f(1)-f(-2)}{1-(-2)} = \frac{-2-4}{1+2} = \frac{-6}{3} = -2 \end{equation*}
    and so we see that, on average, the function is changing by \(-2\) units of \(y\) per unit of \(x\) between \(x=-2\) and \(x=1\text{.}\) We could also say that the function is decreasing by 2 units of \(y\) per unit of \(x\text{,}\) which matches what we see in the picture.
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, we calculate the average rate of change of \(y\) on the interval \([1,3]\text{.}\) Observe that \(f(1)=-2\) and \(f(3)=0\) will be used in the formula for Average Rate of Change, using \(a=1\) and \(b=3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[1,3]} = \frac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1} = \frac{0-(-2)}{3-1} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \end{equation*}
    so, on average, the function is increasing by 1 unit of \(y\) per unit of \(x\) between \(x=1\) and \(x=3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We can also measure average rate of change over intervals where the function both increases and decreases. For example, on the interval \([-1,2]\) the function first decreases and then increases. Note that \(f(-1) = 2\) and \(f(2) = -1\text{.}\) Computing the average rate of change from \(x=-1\) to \(x=2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[-1,2]} = \frac{f(2)-f(-1)}{2-(-1)} = \frac{-1-2}{2+1} = \frac{-3}{3} = -1 \end{equation*}
    so, on average, the function is decreasing by 1 (or changing by \(-1\)) unit of \(y\) per unit of \(x\) between \(x=-1\) and \(x=2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    As another example, the average rate of change on the interval \([0,2]\) is
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[0,2]} = \frac{f(2)-f(0)}{2-0} = \frac{-1-(-1)}{2} = \frac{0}{2} = 0 \end{equation*}
    where we observe that since \(f(0) = -1\) and \(f(2) = -1\text{,}\) the function has not changed on average from \(x=0\) to \(x=2\text{.}\)

    Subsection Average Change of a Linear Function

    Letâ€™s examine the average change of linear functions using the function \(f(x) = 7-3x\text{.}\) Before we begin and without doing any calculations, think about what the average change of a line should be.
    ðŸ”—
    Now letâ€™s compute the average change of \(f(x) = 7-3x\) on three different, random intervals: \([-3,-1]\text{,}\) \([2,5]\text{,}\) and \([4,10]\text{.}\) Before reading the calculations below, you should evaluate the values of \(f\) at each of the endpoints of the intervals (\(f(-3), f(-1), f(2), f(4), f(5)\text{,}\) and \(f(10)\)) to make following it less confusing.
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[-3,-1]}=\frac{f(-1)-f(-3)}{-1-(-3)} = \frac{10-16}{2} = \frac{-6}{2} = -3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[2,5]} = \frac{f(5)-f(2)}{5-2} = \frac{-8-1}{3} = \frac{-9}{3} = -3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[4,10]}= \frac{f(10)-f(4)}{10-4} = \frac{-23-(-5)}{6} = \frac{-18}{6} = -3 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We notice that we have gotten the same average change of \(-3\) for all three intervals. We wonder if this is a coincidence. In order to confirm that this is not a coincidence, we calculate the average change of \(f(x) = 7-3x\) on any interval \([a,b]\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}AV_{[a,b]} \amp = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\\ \amp = \frac{(7-3(b))-(7-3(a))}{b-a}\\ \amp =\frac{7-3b-7+3a}{b-a}\\ \amp = \frac{-3b+3a}{b-a}\\ \amp = \frac{-3(b-a)}{b-a}\\ \amp =-3 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    So now we have confirmed that the average rate of change of \(f(x) = 7-3x\) on any interval is \(-3\text{,}\) which is the same as the slope \(m=-3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    There was nothing special about this function. The average change of any linear function \(f(x) = mx+b\) on any interval is the slope \(m\) of the function. In fact, we may take this to be the definition of a linear function, if we want:
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 1.3.7. Alternate Definition of a Linear Function.

    A function \(f\) is linear provided that its average rate of change is constant on every choice of interval in its domain.

    Subsection Average Change in Context

    Letâ€™s now return to the question of describing the rate of change of a quantity in context, paying special attention to the units and interpretation for these calculations.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 1.3.13.

    Let the height function for a ball tossed vertically be given by
    \begin{equation*} s(t) = 64-16(t-1)^2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(t\) is measured in seconds since the ball was thrown and \(s\) is measured in feet above the ground.
    ðŸ”—
    Plotting the function \(s(t)\) using Desmos in FigureÂ 1.3.14, it looks like the ball is thrown from a height of 48 feet, reaches its maximum height of 64 feet after 1 second, and hits the ground after 3 seconds:
    ðŸ”—
    The average rate of change of \(s(t)\) on the interval \([1.5,2.5]\) is calculated below, using the fact that \(s(1.5) = 64-16(1.5-1)^2=60\) and \(s(2.5)=64-16(2.5-1)^2 = 28\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[1.5,2.5]} = \frac{s(2.5)-s(1.5)}{2.5-1.5} = \frac{28-60}{1} = -32 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    According to Definition DefinitionÂ 1.3.3, the units for the average rate of change of \(s(t)\) should be "units of \(s\) per units of \(t\text{.}\)" Since the units of \(s\) are feet and the units of \(t\) are seconds, the units for \(AV_{[1.5,2.5]} = -32\) are "feet per second." In the context of the ball, this means that from 1.5 seconds to 2.5 seconds after the ball is thrown, it falls at an average speed of 32 feet per second toward the ground.
    ðŸ”—
    We can visualize the quantity \(-32\) as the slope of the line connecting the points \((1.5,60)\) and \((2.5,28)\) on the graph of \(s(t)\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In the context of a function that measures height or position of a moving object at a given time, such as in Example ExampleÂ 1.3.13, the meaning of the average rate of change of the function on a given interval is the average velocity of the moving object because it is the ratio of \(\Delta s=\) change in position to \(\Delta t=\) change in time:
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[a,b]} = \frac{s(b)-s(a)}{b-a} =\frac{ \Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{\text{ change in position } }{\text{ change in time } } = \text{ average velocity } \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    For example, in Exercise ExampleÂ 1.3.13, the units on \(AV_{[1.5,2.5]}=-32\) are "feet per second" since the units on the numerator are "feet" and on the denominator "seconds." The fact that the average rate of change is negative in this example indicates that the ball is falling. In every situation, the units on the average rate of change help us interpret its meaning, and those units are always "units of output per unit of input."

    Subsection Concavity and Average Change of a Quadratic Function

    In contrast to linear functions, quadratic functions do not have a constant rate of change. Instead, their rates of change follow a different pattern. Weâ€™ll begin to see this pattern by looking at the graphs of functions whose rates of change are increasing throughout an interval or decreasing throughout an interval.
    ðŸ”—
    [cross-reference to target(s) "fig_TaalmanPage250Increasing" missing or not unique] below shows graphs that are concave up: they are bending upward because the rate of change of the function is increasing.
    ðŸ”—
    On the other hand, [cross-reference to target(s) "fig_TaalmanPage250Decreasing" missing or not unique] below shows graphs that are concave down: they are bending downward because the rate of change of the function is decreasing.
    ðŸ”—
    We know that quadratic functions are always either concave up or concave down, so we can expect their rates of change to increase (if the quadratic function in concave up) or for them to decrease (if the quadratic function is concave down). Note that a line is neither concave up nor concave down, and its rate of change is constant.
    ðŸ”—

    ðŸ”—
    \(\Delta\)
    Key ideas: The average rate of change of a function on an intervals describes how much, on average, that function is increasing or decreasing on the interval. Linear functions have constant average rate of change. Quadratic functions do not have a constant average rate of change. Average rate of change can be visualized as slope between points on the graph. The units for average rate of change are units of output per unit of input.
    ðŸ”—
    Other ideas introduced: Functions that are concave up have increasing rates of change while functions that are concave down have decreasing rates of change. Quadratic functions have rates of change that appear to be changing linearly.
