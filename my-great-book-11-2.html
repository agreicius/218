    Subsection 9.1.2 Trigonometric Relationships

    In this section, we look at a particular type of related rates problem that requires trigonometric functions. Recall from SectionÂ 5.2 that two quantities are related if there is an equation containing the two quantities. If those quantities change over time, then their rates of change are also related. We can find the relationship between the rates of change by taking the derivative of the equation involving the two quantities.
    ðŸ”—
    The legs \(a\) and \(b\) and hypotenuse \(c\) of a right triangle are all related to each other via the different trigonometric functions. Moreover, both of the angles \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) opposite the legs are also related to the various sides.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    For example, the hypotenuse \(c\) is related to both \(a\) and \(b\) via
    \begin{equation*} \sin\theta_1 = \frac{a}{c}\qquad\text{ and } \qquad \sin\theta_2 = \frac{b}{c}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We could alternately use the cosine function instead to relate \(c\) to \(a\) and \(b\) via
    \begin{equation*} \cos\theta_1 = \frac{b}{c}\qquad\text{ and } \qquad \cos\theta_2 = \frac{a}{c}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Each of the legs of the triangle are also related to each other via
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta_1 = \frac{a}{b}\qquad\text{ or } \qquad\tan\theta_2 = \frac{b}{a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The two angles \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) are also related to each other. We already observed that \(\sin\theta_2 = \frac{b}{c}\) and that \(\cos\theta_1 = \frac{b}{c}\text{.}\) Thus we must have
    \begin{equation*} \sin\theta_2 = \cos\theta_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    In general, whenever we are solving a related rates problem that involves an angle and a triangle, we want to pick one of the equations listed above as our "relationship." Should you memorize these? Absolutely not! You should use the problem solving framework from Chapter 0 to help you think of what equation is most appropriate.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 9.1.1.

    For example, suppose that the height \(a\) of a right triangle is increasing but that the base \(b\) stays the same length. As the height increases, the length of the hypotenuse \(c\) must also be increasing. Moreover, both of the angles \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) opposite the base and height must be changing.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose we measure that the base \(b\) is 4 units and the height \(a\) is increasing by 2 units per second at the moment when \(a=3\text{.}\) Letâ€™s find the rate of change of \(\theta_1\text{,}\) the angle opposite \(a\text{,}\) at this moment. In other words, we are are given \(b=4\) and wish to find \(\frac{d\theta_1}{dt}\) when \(a=3\text{,}\) and \(\frac{da}{dt} = 2\text{.}\) First, letâ€™s note that we have information about \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) and wish to know something about \(\theta_1\text{,}\) so out of all of the different relationships listed above, the most convenient one will be
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta_1 = \frac{a}{b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, since we wish to find \(\frac{d\theta_1}{dt}\text{,}\) it will be additionally convenient to solve for it using the arctangent function:
    \begin{equation*} \theta_1 = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We also remember that \(b=4\) is a constant, not a changing quantity, so we can substitute it in before taking the derivative:
    â€‰1â€‰
    Alternately, we can work with the variable \(b\) and remember that \(\frac{db}{dt}=0\) when taking the derivative, but that will involve using the quotient rule when taking the derivative of \(\frac{a}{b}\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \theta_1 = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a}{4}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Taking the derivative \(\frac{d}{dt}\) of both sides,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\frac{d\theta_1}{dt} \amp = \frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{a}{4}\right)^2}\cdot\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{a}{4}\right)\\ \amp =\frac{1}{1+\frac{a^2}{16}}\cdot\frac{1}{4}\frac{da}{dt}\\ \amp =\frac{\frac{da}{dt}}{4+\frac{a^2}{4}} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Lastly, we remember that we want to find \(\frac{d\theta_1}{dt}\) when \(a=3\) and \(\frac{da}{dt} = 2\) and so we compute:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d\theta_1}{dt}\Big|_{\substack{a=3\\ \frac{da}{dt}=2} } = \frac{2}{4+\frac{3^2}{4}} = \frac{2}{25/4} = \frac{8}{25}=0.32 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence \(\theta_1\) is increasing at a rate of \(0.32\) radians per second.

    Example 9.1.4. Watching a Rocket.

    A SpaceX scientist uses a telescope to track a rocket launched vertically from a launching pad 6km away. At a certain moment, the angle \(\theta\) between the telescope and the ground is equal to \(\frac{\pi}{3}\) and is changing at a rate of \(0.9\) radians per minute. What is the rocketâ€™s velocity at that moment?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    As in the picture above, call the vertical height of the rocket \(y\text{.}\) The problem tells us to find \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) when \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\) and \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = 0.9\text{,}\) given that the base of the triangle is \(6\text{.}\) Note that we have no information at all about the hypotenuse, so do not wish to use either the sine or cosine function. Thus we relate \(y\) and \(\theta\) with the tangent function:
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta = \frac{y}{6}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(y\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} y = 6\tan\theta \end{equation*}
    and taking the derivative in order to relate \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) to \(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{,}\) we get

    Example 9.1.6. Tracking a Plane.

    A plane flies horizontally at an altitude of 5 km and passes directly over a tracking beacon on the ground. When the angle between the beaconâ€™s line of sight to the plane and the ground is \(\pi/3\text{,}\) this angle is decreasing at a rate of \(\pi/6\) rad/min. How fast is the plane traveling at that time?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    First, we draw a picture of the scenario and label it with variable names and given information.
    ðŸ”—
    The problem asks us to find \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) when \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\) and \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\frac{\pi}{6}\text{,}\) given that the height of the triangle is 5. Again, we have no information about the hypotenuse so we would like to relate the two legs of the triangle directly. In order to see how to do this, notice that there is another triangle in the picture that also has height of 5 and that the planeâ€™s distance along the ground is the same as its distance in the air:
    Thus we see that
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta = \frac{5}{x} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Solving for \(x\text{,}\)

    and so taking the derivative to relate \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) to \(\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{,}\)

    A lighthouse is located 3 kilometers away from a long, straight beach wall. The beacon of light is rotating steadily at a rate of 1.5 revolutions per minute. Note that we do not know whether the light is rotating clockwise or counterclockwise, so we do not know whether the light is moving right or left along the wall. Our calculations below will thus be direction-less.

    If we picture the wall on the horizontal as below:

    Example 9.1.10. Riding a Ferris Wheel.

    A Ferris wheel with a radius of 10 m is rotating at a rate of one revolution every 2 minutes. How fast is a rider rising when their seat is 16 m above ground level?
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Hopefully this evokes memories of the beginnings of our exploration of trigonometric functions: the vertical movement of a point around the unit circle is described by the sine function. With this in mind, letâ€™s draw a circle of radius 10 representing our Ferris wheel and set our origin at the center of the circle. This means that "ground level" is at \(y=-10\text{:}\)
    We are told in the problem that \(\theta\) rotates once around every two minutes, so halfway around every minute, meaning \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi\) rad/min. 16 m above ground level is when \(y=6\text{,}\) hence we are asked to find \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) when \(y=6\text{,}\) given that \(\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    In this problem, we relate \(y\) and \(\theta\) using sine function and the constant hypotenuse:
    \begin{equation*} \sin\theta = \frac{y}{10} \end{equation*}
    and solving for \(y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} y = 10\sin\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Taking the derivative,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt} = 10\cos\theta\frac{d\theta}{dt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In order to find \(\cos\theta\) when \(y=6\text{,}\) we use the Pythagorean Theorem to find \(x\text{.}\) We have that \(x^2+6^2 = 10^2\) when \(x = \sqrt{100-36} = \sqrt{64} = 8\text{.}\) Hence \(\cos\theta = \frac{8}{10} = \frac{4}{5}\text{.}\) Now we can simplify
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt}\Big|_{\substack{y=6\\ \frac{d\theta}{dt} = \pi} } = 10\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)(\pi) = 8\pi\approx 25.13 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Assuming that the rider is on the way up, their seat is rising at a rate of about \(25.13\) meters per minute (equal to about 1.37 feet per second).

    Subsection 9.1.4 Summary

    Key ideas: The sides and angles of a right triangle are related via the trigonometric functions sine, cosine, and tangent. Use the trigonometric function that involves only quantities you wish to find and about which you are given information, then take the derivative to relate the derivatives of the quantities. Use the given information to determine the derivative you wish to find.
    ðŸ”—
    Other ideas reinforced: Use constant values for quantities that do not change, but do not use given values of variables until after taking the derivative. Use units as "key words" in deciphering whether values given in the problem are for a quantity or its derivative. Itâ€™s almost always necessary to draw a labeled picture (with variables and lengths) before realizing which equation to use.
