    Subsection 5.1.2 Curves Described by Equations

    In all of our studies with derivatives so far, we have worked with functions where the value of the dependent variable (often \(y\)) is given explicitly in terms of the value of the independent variable (often \(x\)). But there are many interesting curves that are defined by equations involving variables that are impossible to solve for one in terms of the other. In this section, you will see several examples of such curves and learn how to use calculus to study such curves in the absence of an explicit formula.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.1. A Lemniscate.

    The curve pictured below, called a lemniscate, is the collection of points that satisfy the equation \(x^3-y^3 = 6xy\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    What does it mean for a point to "satisfy the equation?" It means that when we plug in the values of the variables given by the point, we get a true statement from the equation.
    ðŸ”—
    For instance, if \(x=0\) and \(y=0\text{,}\) then the left side of the equation \(x^3-y^3 = 6xy\) is
    \begin{equation*} x^3-y^3 = 0^3-0^3 = 0 \end{equation*}
    and the right side of the equation is
    \begin{equation*} 6xy = 6(0)(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Since both sides of the equation evaluate to the same number, they are equal to each other for the given values of \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) making the equation "true." Hence the point \((x,y)=(0,0)\) "satisfies the equation" \(x^3-y^3 = 6xy\) and is included on the curve.
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, if \(x=-3\) and \(y=3\text{,}\) then the left side of the equation \(x^3-y^3 = 6xy\) is
    \begin{equation*} x^3-y^3 = (-3)^3-(3)^3 = -27-27=-54 \end{equation*}
    and the right side of the equation is
    \begin{equation*} 6xy = 6(-3)(3) = -54 \end{equation*}
    so the point \((-3,3)\) also satisfied the equation and is on the curve.
    ðŸ”—
    Take a moment to find the points \((0,0)\) and \((-3,3)\) on the curve in FigureÂ 5.1.2.
    ðŸ”—
    We can see that the lemniscate does not pass the vertical line test â€” for example, the line \(x=-2\) passes through the curve at three different \(y\)-values. Because the vertical line test fails, we know that the curve cannot be the graph of a function \(y=f(x)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    ðŸ”—
    But portions of the circle can be represented explicitly as a function of \(x\text{,}\) such as the highlighted arc that is magnified to the right of FigureÂ 5.1.4 below:

    Subsection 5.1.3 Finding Derivatives of Implicit Functions

    The following three examples remind us of some ways we can compute derivatives of functions in settings where the functionâ€™s formula is not known.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.7.

    Let \(f\) be a differentiable function of \(x\) (whose formula is not known) and recall that \(\frac{df}{dx}\) and \(f'(x)\) mean the same thing: the derivative of \(f\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2+f(x)] = 2x+\frac{df}{dx} \end{equation*}
    where we have used the sum rule to take the derivative of each term separately.
    ðŸ”—
    Likewise, if we are told that \(y\) is a function of \(x\) and wish to take the derivative of \(x^2+y\text{,}\) we can write:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2+y] = x^2+\frac{dy}{dx} \end{equation*}
    where \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) means "take the derivative of \(y\) using \(x\) as the variable."
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.8.

    If \(f\) is a differentiable function, find an expression in terms of \(x, f\text{,}\) and \(\frac{df}{dx}\) for
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2f(x)]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly, if \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{,}\) find the derivative of \(x^2y\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The derivative of \(x^2f(x)\) requires us to use the product rule, because we are multiplying \(x^2\) and \(f(x)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2f(x)] = (2x)\cdot f(x)+(x^2)\cdot \frac{df}{dx} = 2xf(x)+x^2\frac{df}{dx} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, if \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{,}\) then \(x^2y\) is also a product of \(x^2\) and \(y\text{,}\) so we use the product rule to take the derivative:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2y] = 2xy+x^2\frac{dy}{dx} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.9.

    If \(f(x)\) is a differentiable function with derivative \(\frac{df}{dx}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[(f(x))^2] = 2f(x)\, f'(x) \end{equation*}
    by the chain rule. Likewise,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[(f(x))^3] = 3(f(x))^2\, f'(x) \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    If we use the variable \(y\) in place of \(f(x)\text{,}\) and specify that \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{,}\) then we must still use the chain rule in order to compute the following derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[y^2] = 2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[y^3] = 3y^2\,\frac{dy}{dx} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The key idea being used is that we can treat the variable \(y\) as a function of \(x\) while taking the derivative. In other words, we treat \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\text{.}\) In so doing, we can "compute" the derivative of \(y\) as \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) without having a formula for \(y\text{,}\) and we can also "compute" the derivative of equations involving the variables \(x\) and \(y\) without needing to solve for \(y\) explicitly. This process is what is called implicit differentiation and is demonstrated fully in the next example.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.10. The Circle.

    Letâ€™s consider again the example of the circle given by \(x^2+y^2 = 16\text{.}\) How can we find a formula for the derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    By viewing \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\text{,}\) we think of \(y\) as some function whose formula \(f(x)\) is unknown, but which we can differentiate. Just as \(y\) represents an unknown formula, we can treat its derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as an unknown formula; we use it purely as a symbol.
    ðŸ”—
    Viewing \(y\) as an unknown differentiable function of \(x\text{,}\) we take the derivative of both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2+y^2] = \frac{d}{dx}[16] \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    On the right, the derivative of the constant \(16\) is \(0\text{,}\) and on the left we can apply the sum rule, so it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2]+\frac{d}{dx}[y^2] =0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Pay attention to the different roles being played by \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Because \(x\) is the independent variable, \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2]=2x\) as usual. But \(y\) is the dependent variable, meaning \(y\) is an implicit function of \(x\text{.}\) Recall ExampleÂ 5.1.9, where we computed \(\frac{d}{dx}[(f(x))^2] = 2f(x)\, f'(x)\text{.}\) Computing \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^2]\) is exactly the same, and requires the chain rule, by which we find that \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^2] = 2y\,\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The derivative of the equation \(x^2+y^2=16\) is then
    \begin{equation*} 2x+2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \end{equation*}
    once we put together the left and right sides computed above.
    ðŸ”—
    Now, we can solve this equation for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) by subtracting \(2x\) from both sides and dividing by \(2y\text{,}\) then canceling the 2. Some people refer to this as "isolating \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)"
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}2x+2y\,\frac{dy}{dx} \amp = 0\\ 2y\frac{dy}{dx} \amp = -2x\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \amp =\frac{-2x}{2y}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = -\frac{x}{y} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    And voila! We have managed to compute the derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) without ever knowing an explicit formula for \(y\) as a function of \(x\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 5.1.11.

    For the curve given implicitly by \(x^3+y^3-2xy = 2\text{,}\) find the derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) if we view \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We begin by differentiating the curveâ€™s equation implicitly, meaning by treating \(y\) as an implicit function of \(x\text{.}\) Taking the derivative of each side with respect to \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^3+y^2-2xy] = \frac{d}{dx}[2]\text{,} \end{equation*}
    by the sum rule and the fact that the derivative of a constant is zero, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^3]+\frac{d}{dx}[y^2]-\frac{d}{dx}[2xy] = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We have three derivatives to compute. \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^3]\) uses the power rule applied to the variable \(x\text{.}\) \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^2]\) requires the chain rule since \(y\) is an implicit function of \(x\text{.}\) And \(\frac{d}{dx}[2xy]\) requires the product rule since \(y\) is a function of \(x\) and we are multiplying \(2x\) by \(y\text{.}\) Applying these rules, we now find that
    \begin{equation*} 3x^2+2y\frac{dy}{dx}-\left(2x\frac{dy}{dx}+2y\right) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Now we want to solve this equation for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\) To do so, we first collect all of the terms involving \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) on one side of the equation, factor \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{,}\) then divide to isolate \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}3x^2+2y\frac{dy}{dx}-\left(2x\frac{dy}{dx}+2y\right) \amp = 0\\ 3x^2+2y\frac{dy}{dx}-2x\frac{dy}{dx}-2y \amp = 0\\ 2y\frac{dy}{dx}-2x\frac{dy}{dx} \amp = 2y-3x^2\\ \frac{dy}{dx}(2y-2x) \amp = 2y-3x^2\\ \frac{dy}{dx} \amp = \frac{2y-3x^2}{2y-2x}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \fbox{Summary: The process for computing \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) via implicit differentiation is as follows.
    ðŸ”—

      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Split the derivative up over each term and apply the appropriate derivative rules.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—

      ðŸ”—

    • Once you are finished taking the derivative of the equation, solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) by isolating it. Usually this consists of:
      ðŸ”—

        ðŸ”—

    Subsection 5.1.4 Tangent Lines to Implicitly Defined Curves

    Now that we have a formula for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{,}\) meaning the derivative of \(y\) with respect to the variable \(x\text{,}\) we can find equations for tangent lines. Remember that the value of the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line. We simply plug in the \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinate of the point to the expression for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{,}\) as demonstrated in the next example. We use the notation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{(x,y)=(a,b)} \end{equation*}
    to denote the evaluation of \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) at the point \((a,b)\). This is analogous to writing \(f'(a) = \frac{df}{dx}\Big|_{x=a}\) when \(f'(x)\) is the derivative of an explicit function.

    Subsection 5.1.5 Vertical and Horizontal Tangent Lines

    On an explicitly defined function, we say that the derivative is undefined at points where the tangent line is vertical, since we do not have an explicit value of the derivative at those points. However, on an implicitly defined curve, we wish to also consider vertical tangent lines since we are not restricting ourselves to explicit functions. While the slope of a horizontal tangent line must be zero, the slope of a vertical tangent line is infinite.
    ðŸ”—
    In many of the examples we have considered, the formula for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) is expressed as a fraction of expressions involving \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{p(x,y)}{q(x,y)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The tangent line is horizontal precisely when \(p(x,y)=0\) and \(q(x,y)\neq 0\text{,}\) making the slope of the tangent line zero. Similarly, the tangent line is vertical whenever \(q(x,y)=0\) and \(p(x,y)\neq 0\text{,}\) making the slope infinite.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 5.1.6 Summary

    The following terms were introduced in this section:
    ðŸ”—
    \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)\((a,b)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{(x,y)=(a,b)} \end{equation*}
