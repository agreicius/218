    Subsection 9.4.2 A Few New Indeterminate Forms

    Recall that if a limit is indeterminate, then we cannot initially say whether or not it existsâ€“or if it exists, what real number it is equal to. Many indeterminate limits can be resolved with algebra such as cancellation or factoring. Certain other indeterminate limits can be resolved by applying Lâ€™HÃ´pitalâ€™s rule, or some combination of algebra and Lâ€™HÃ´pitalâ€™s rule.
    ðŸ”—
    The following theorem identifies the seven indeterminate limit forms that are commonly encountered. We have already explored the first four forms (and will summarize methods for analyzing them in the last part of this section). The last three forms will be explored in depth in this section:
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 9.4.1.

    Each of the following is an indeterminate form, meaning that a limit in one of these forms may or may not exist, depending on the details of the situation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0}{0}\qquad \frac{\infty}{\infty}\qquad 0\cdot\infty\qquad \infty-\infty\qquad 0^0\qquad 1^\infty \qquad \infty^0 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Why are limits of the form \(0^0, 1^\infty\text{,}\) and \(\infty^0\) indeterminate? There is an inherent "conflict" in each of these forms that prevents us from knowing intuitively what the limit is equal to.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 9.4.2.

    If a limit has the form \(0^0\text{,}\) then the base is a number approaching 0 and the exponent is a number approaching 0; neither are exactly equal to 0. Letâ€™s explore the value of such a limit intuitively and see where the conflict arrises.
    ðŸ”—
    On the one hand, any number raised to the 0 power should be equal to 1. So you could argue that such a limit form should be equal to 1.
    ðŸ”—
    On the other hand, a base of 0 raised to any power should be equal to 0. So you could also argue that such a limit form should be equal to 0.
    ðŸ”—
    Further still, if we consider the exponent to be a slightly negative number and the base to be a very small positive number, we seem to get either a finite number or a number that may eventually go to infinity. For example, if the exponent is \(-0.1\) and the base starts at \(0.00001\) and gets closer to 0:
    ðŸ”—
    \(0.00001^{-0.1}\) \(0.000001^{-0.1}\) \(0.0000001^{-0.1}\) \(0.00000001^{-0.1}\)
    \(\approx3.162\) \(\approx3.981\) \(\approx5.012\) \(\approx6.310\)
    All this is to demonstrate that a limit of the form \(0^0\) could be equal to anything and that none of the intuitive arguments above are precise enough for us to know!
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 9.4.3.

    The limit form \(1^\infty\) is similarly indeterminate when we remember that the base could be a number slightly less than or slightly greater than 1, while the exponent is a number that gets very large.
    ðŸ”—
    For example, \(1.1^{1,000,000}\approx 4.84\times10^{41392}\) is VERY large, while \(0.9^{1,000,000}\approx 3.23\times10^{-45758}\) is VERY small.
    ðŸ”—
    Which of these is correct? Neither, really. We have no way of knowing the value of such a limit without more detailed analysis.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Checkpoint 9.4.4.

    Similar to the previous two examples, give intuitive explanations for the "conflict" in the indeterminate form \(\infty^0\text{.}\) In other words, explain how this limit form could potentially be equal to a finite number and how it could potentially be equal to an infinite number. Note that there is no "right" answer to what this limit form could be equal to besides the observation that no intuition will tell us definitively what such a form is equal to.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In contrast to the above examples, note that the following limit forms are NOT indeterminate:
    \begin{equation*} 0^\infty\qquad 0^1\qquad \infty^\infty\qquad \infty^1 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The first two (\(0^\infty\) and \(0^1\)) must both be equal to 0 (can you see why?) while the second two (\(\infty^\infty\) and \(\infty^1\)) must both be infinite (again, can you see why? Assume that by "\(\infty\)" we mean here a very large positive number).
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 9.4.3 Indeterminate Limits of the Form \(0^0, 1^\infty\text{,}\) and \(\infty^0\)

    Letâ€™s examine a few explicit limits to see if we can gain any further insight into the forms \(0^0, 1^\infty\text{,}\) and \(\infty^0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 9.4.5.

    Consider the limit
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to\infty}\left(\frac{x}{x-1}\right)^{x^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    First note that the base \(\frac{x}{x-1}\) approaches 1 as \(x\to\infty\text{,}\) while the exponent \(x^2\) approaches \(\infty\) as \(x\to\infty\text{.}\) Thus the limit has the form \(1^\infty\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Looking at a table of values:
    ðŸ”—
    \(x\) 5 10 100
    \(\left(\frac{x}{x-1}\right)^{x^2}\) \(264.70\) \(37648.6\) \(4.45\times10^{43}\)
    This limit seems to be approaching \(\infty\text{.}\) How can we confirm this?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 9.4.6.

    Consider the limit
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^+} x^{1/(x^2-1)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The base approaches \(1\) while the exponent \(\frac{1}{x^2-1}\) approaches \(\frac{1}{0^+} = \infty\text{,}\) so the limit has the form \(1^\infty\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Again looking at a table of values:
    ðŸ”—
    \(x\) 2 1.1 1.01 1.001 1.0001
    \(x^{1/(x^2-1)}\) \(1.25992\) \(1.57437\) \(1.64057\) \(1.64790\) \(1.64864\)
    It seems like this limit is approaching something around \(1.65\text{,}\) but what is the exact value, and how can we be sure?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In each of these limits there is a race between how fast the base approaches 1 and how fast the exponent approaches \(\infty\text{,}\) an in some sense the winner of that race determines the limit. But how can we determine who wins this race?
    ðŸ”—
    One difficulty with limits of the indeterminate forms \(0^0, 1^\infty\text{,}\) and \(\infty^0\) is that such limits involve a variable in both the base and the exponent. Fortunately, logarithms have the power to change exponentiation into multiplication, in the sense that \(\ln(a^b) = b\ln a\text{.}\) The key to using logarithms to calculate limits is the following theorem:
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 9.4.7. Relating the Limit of a Function to the Limit of its Logarithm.

    For a function \(f(x)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c}\ln(f(x)) = L\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c}f(x) = e^L\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In particular,
    • If \(L=\infty\text{,}\) then \(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x) = \ds\lim_{u\to\infty} e^u = \infty\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • If \(L=-\infty\text{,}\) then \(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x) = \ds\lim_{u\to-\infty} e^u = 0\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This theorem is true because of the composition rule for limits of continuous functions from TheoremÂ 3.2.33. To use this theorem to calculate a limit of the form
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c}u(x)^{v(x)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we consider instead the different limit
    \begin{equation*} \ln\left(\lim_{x\to c}u(x)^{v(x)}\right) = \lim_{x\to c}\ln(u(x)^{v(x)}) = \lim_{x\to c} v(x)\ln(u(x))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    TheoremÂ 3.2.33 is what allows us to reverse the order of the limit and the natural logarithm.
    ðŸ”—
    Notice that the logarithm allows us to consider the limit of a product rather than a limit involving an exponent; our previous methods will be much more effective with this new limit. Once we find the value \(L\) of this different limit, the theorem tells us that the answer to our original limit must be \(e^L\text{.}\) In other words, we take the natural logarithm to evaluate the limit, so we must undo this by raising \(e\) to the limit:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\ln(\text{ limit } ) \amp = L\\ \text{ limit } \amp = e^L \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Of course, if instead of a finite number for \(L\) we get \(L=\pm\infty\text{,}\) then our original limit must be equal to \(\lim_{u\to\pm\infty}e^{u}\text{,}\) which is either \(0\) or \(\infty\text{.}\)
