      🔗
      🔗

      🔗
      🔗

      🔗

    Subsection 3.1.2 The Idea of a Limit

    There are many instances when studying natural phenomena where we wish to know what happens near a point, but not specifically at a point. Alternately, we may wish to know about the long-term behavior of a quantity, but not specifically at some time in the future, no matter how distant. The mathematical idea of a "limit" is precisely designed to describe such things using the language of functions. Instead of looking at the output value of a function when plugging in a specific input, we look at the pattern of the output values over a sequence of input values.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.1. Using Limits to Describe Zeno’s Paradox.

    Zeno’s paradox
     1 
    See a delightful Numberphile video at https://youtu.be/u7Z9UnWOJNY for more on Zeno’s Paradox and infinite processes.
    is a wonderful example of a limit. Consider that in moving from one place to another, you must first travel half the distance, then half of the remaining distance, then half of that remaining distance, etc. Since there will never be zero distance left to travel, you will never reach your destination. We can express these distances as a sequence of fractions of the total distance:
    \begin{equation*} \left\{\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{8}, \frac{1}{16}, \ldots\right\} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that each term in the sequence has the form
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2^n} \end{equation*}
    for a positive integer \(n\text{.}\) Note as well that as \(n\) increases, the denominator \(2^n\) increases, with the result that the fractions \(\frac{1}{2^n}\) become smaller and smaller as \(n\) gets bigger and bigger. Eventually, the fractions \(\frac{1}{2^n}\) are so small as to be indistinguishable from zero; in a theoretical way we have not yet reached our destination, but in a practical sense, it makes no difference.
    🔗
    With the "limit" notation that you will be learning in this section, we would express this idea as
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{2^n} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    What it is saying is that we will eventually have 0 distance left to travel, though it will take us infinitely long to get there. So do we ever actually get where we are going?
    🔗
    🔗
    While interesting, thousands-of-years-old Greek riddles are not our main motivation for being interested in limits. Instead, they are motivated by the hundreds-of-years-old
     2 
    Calculus was invented in the 17th century by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibnitz (independently, and they disagreed with each other). See https://www.livescience.com/50777-calculus.html accessed September 27, 2022.
    question, "How much is a quantity changing?" We already studied one way to answer: average rates of change. The following example shows how this answer can be made more precise by studying smaller and smaller intervals of time, resulting in a limit.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.2. Instantaneous Velocity as a Limit of Average Velocity.

    Recall that, for an object moving in a straight line with position function \(s(t)\text{,}\) the average velocity of the object on the interval from \(t=a\) to \(t=b\text{,}\) denoted \(AV_{[a,b]}\text{,}\) is given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[a,b]} = \frac{s(b)-s(a)}{b-a} \end{equation*}
    where \(s(t)\) is the position of the object. Average velocity is the same as average rate of change of position.
    🔗
    Suppose that the height \(s\) of a ball at time \(t\) (in seconds) is given in feet by the formula \(s(t) = 64-16(t-1)^2\text{.}\) A graph of \(s(t)\) for \(0\leq t\leq 3\) is:
    🔗
    🔗
    We can see from the graph that the ball is 48 feet high when it is released, it is 64 feet high at its highest point, which occurs 1 second after it is released, and that the ball lands after 3 seconds.
    🔗
    Additionally, we can see that the ball is going up on the interval \(0\lt t\lt 1\) and going down on the interval \(1\lt t\lt 3\text{.}\) Think about what the ball is doing at the exact moment corresponding to \(t=1\text{.}\) How fast is it rising or falling?
    🔗
    To begin to answer this, we could look at the average velocity of the ball on the half-seconds before and after \(t=1\text{.}\) On the interval \([0.5,1]\) the average velocity is:
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[0.5,1]} = \frac{s(1)-s(0.5)}{1-0.5} = \frac{64-60}{0.5} = 8 \text{ ft/sec. } \end{equation*}
    🔗
    On the interval \([1,1.5]\text{,}\) the average velocity is
    \begin{equation*} AV_{[1,1.5]} = \frac{s(1.5)-s(1)}{1.5-1} = \frac{60-64}{0.5} = -8 \text{ ft/sec. } \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Neither of these seems particularly precise and we aren’t sure what we should conclude. (Maybe you are. What do you think we should do with these two numbers?) Half-second intervals seem fairly long, so let’s make the intervals shorter. Table 3.1.3 below gives the average velocity on intervals that are smaller and smaller on either side of \(t=1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    We start to notice a pattern: the average velocities get smaller and smaller as the interval gets narrower and narrower. Because the numbers are consistently on either side of 0, we could now guess that the ball’s speed at the precise moment \(t=1\) is 0 ft/sec. This momentary speed is called the instantaneous velocity at \(t=1\).
    🔗
    Our answer makes some intuitive sense: for the tiniest fraction of a millisecond, the ball is suspended in the air before it begins to fall. This is similar to the moment on a swing when you reach the peak of your swing and feel weightless, before going the other way.
    🔗
    As a limit, we express this idea as
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{b\to a} AV_{[a,b]} = \lim_{b\to a}\frac{s(b)-s(a)}{b-a} = 0 \text{ ft/sec }\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Notice, though, that we cannot simply have \(b=a\) in the average velocity formula, since in that case \(b-a = 0\) and we would be dividing by 0. So is the ball actually ever motionless in the air?

    Subsection 3.1.3 The Definition of a Limit

    Let’s make these ideas more precise and also explain what this "limit notation" actually means. The basic idea is that we ask what output values the function gets close to near an input value.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.5. Definition of a Limit.

    Given a function \(f\text{,}\) a fixed input \(x=c\text{,}\) and a real number \(L\text{,}\) we say that \(f\) has limit \(L\) as \(x\) approaches \(c\) and write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c}f(x) = L \end{equation*}
    provided that we can make \(f(x)\) as close to \(L\) as we like by taking \(x\) sufficiently close (but not equal) to \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If we cannot make \(f(x)\) as close to a single value as we would like as \(x\) approaches \(c\text{,}\) then we say that \(f\) does not have a limit as \(x\) approaches \(c\text{.}\)Another way to say this is that the limit “does not exist,” and we abbreviate with
    \begin{equation*} \ds \lim_{x\to c}f(x) \text{ DNE }\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    \fbox{ The symbols \(\ds\lim_{x\to c} f(x) = L\) are read, "the limit as \(x\) approaches \(c\) of \(f(x)\) is equal to \(L\text{.}\)" }
    🔗
    Here is an example of finding the limit of a function at a point that is not in the domain of a rational function:
    🔗

    Example 3.1.6.

    For example, the function \(g(x) = \dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}\) can be simplified by factoring the numerator as \(\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1} = \dfrac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \begin{cases}x+1, \amp \text{ for } x\neq 1\\ \text{ undefined } , \amp \text{ for } x=1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then for values of \(x\) near 1, the values of \(g(x)\) are still near \(x+1=(1)+1=2\text{,}\) even though \(g(1)\) is undefined, pictured in Figure 3.1.7 below:
    🔗
    Note the small hole in the graph of the line at the point \((1,2)\) because the graph does not actually contain this point. This is precisely the kind of situation that limits are designed for; we write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1} g(x) = 2 \end{equation*}
    because the limit does not care what happens at the point where \(x=1\text{,}\) only close to it.
    🔗
    In this example \(x=c=1\) is the value that the input approaches, and \(y=L=2\) is the value that the outputs approach.

    It’s not always the case that the output of a function will approach a finite number at a non-domain point.
    🔗

    Example 3.1.10.

    Consider the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{(x-1)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can see that the domain of \(f(x)\) is \(x\neq 1\) since the denominator is equal to zero when \(x-1=0\text{,}\) hence when \(x=1\text{.}\) Graphing \(f(x)\text{,}\) we see that the values of \(f(x)\) become infinitely large as \(x\) gets close to \(x=1\text{:}\)
    🔗
    Because the values of \(f(x)\) do not approach an actual number, technically the limit does not exist according to Definition Definition 3.1.5. However, we write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1}f(x) = \infty \end{equation*}
    to describe the fact that the values of the function become infinitely large as \(x\to 1\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.1.4 One-Sided Limits

    We’ve seen that limits can be quite useful for describing what happens when a function is undefined. They are also useful for describing what happens when two parts of a piecewise-defined function do not line up smoothly, as occurred at the point \(x=2\) in Example 3.1.8.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.14. Definition of One-Sided Limits.

    Given a function \(f\text{,}\) a fixed input \(x=c\) and real numbers \(L\) and \(R\text{,}\) we say that \(f\) has limit \(L\) as \(x\) approaches \(c\) from the left and write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) = L \end{equation*}
    provided that we can make \(f(x)\) as close to \(L\) as we like by taking \(x\) sufficiently close (but not equal) to \(c\text{,}\) while keeping \(x\) less than \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Similarly, we say that \(f\) has limit \(R\) as \(x\) approaches \(c\) from the right and write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to c^+}f(x) = R \end{equation*}
    provided that we can make \(f(x)\) as close to \(R\) as we like by taking \(x\) sufficiently close (but not equal) to \(c\text{,}\) while keeping \(x\) greater than \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.15.

    Recall the graph of the function \(g\) from Example Example 3.1.8:
    🔗
    🔗
    On the left of the jump at \(x=1\text{,}\) the \(y\)-values approached 3. Since \(x\lt 1\) on that side, we write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-} g(x) = 3 \end{equation*}
    and we say ``the limit from the left at 1 of \(g(x)\) is equal to 3". On the right of the jump, the \(y\)-values approached 2. Since \(x>1\) on that side, we write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^+} g(x) = 2 \end{equation*}
    and we say ``the limit from the right at 1 of \(g(x)\) is equal to 2."
    🔗
    🔗
    The terminology of “left” and “right” limit refers to the orientation around \(x=c\) on the \(x\)-axis and can be visualized:
    🔗
    \fbox{The regular limit \(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x)\) is sometimes called the “two-sided” limit of \(c\text{,}\) and requires that \(f(x)\) approaches one number for values of \(x\) on both sides of \(c\text{.}\) This means that in order for \(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x)\) to exist, the left and right limits at \(c\) must exist and be equal:
    🔗
    \(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=L\)\(\ds\lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) =L\)\(\ds\lim_{x\to c^+}f(x) =L\)

    Remark 3.1.17.

    Remember that the phrase "if and only if" is shorthand for saying that one thing can only be true if the other is also true. In particular, if either right or left limit does not exist, or the left and right limits are equal to different numbers, then the two-sided limit does not exist.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.18.

    For example, the function \(f\) graphed in Figure 3.1.19 has a different limit from the left than from the right as \(x\) approaches 1:
    🔗
    The purple sequence of values of \(x\) that approach \(x=1\) from the left determines a sequence of values of \(f(x)\) that approach \(y=2\text{,}\) while the red sequence determines values that approach \(y=3\text{.}\) The value of the function at \(x=1\) happens to be \(f(1)=3\text{,}\) but that is not relevant to either limit calculation. Since the limits from the left and right are not the same, there is no one real number that the function approaches as \(x\to c\) and we say the two-sided limit does not exist.
    🔗
    In summary, there are three limits associated to the point \(x=1\) in the above picture:
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-} f(x) = 2, \lim_{x\to 1^+} f(x) = 3, \lim_{x\to 1} f(x) \text{ DNE } \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.1.21.

    The function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x-1}\) is undefined at \(x=1\) because the denominator \(x-1\) is equal to zero. We can see in Figure 3.1.22 that as \(x\) approaches \(1\) from the right, the values of the function increase without bound, so that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^+} f(x) = \infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As \(x\) approaches \(1\) from the left, the values of the function decrease without bound, so that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1^-} f(x) = -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since these the left and right limits do not agree with each other, we cannot make any nice statement about the two-sided behavior of the function at the point, and we write
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 1} f(x) \text{ DNE } \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.5 Summary

    The following terms were introduced in this section:
    🔗
    \(f\)\(x\)\(c\)\(L\text{,}\)\(\ds\lim_{x\to c}f(x) = L\text{;}\)\(f\)\(x\)\(c\)\(L\text{,}\)\(\ds\lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) = L\text{;}\)\(f\)\(x\)\(c\)\(L\text{,}\)\(\ds\lim_{x\to c^+}f(x) = L\text{.}\)
    Key ideas: The limit of a function describes the behavior of the \(y\)-values of a function near a point, rather than at a point. A limit does not exist if the \(y\)-values of a function do not approach a single number. We can take limits separately from the left (less than the input value) or the right (greater than the input value).
    🔗
    Other ideas introduced: A limit only exists if both the right and left limits exist and are equal. Even though we can write that a limit is "equal to infinity," such a limit does not exist. Instantaneous velocity is the (one- or two-sided) limit of average velocity.
